Notes on [[The Top Study Habits to Improve Learning][Andrew Huberman - The Top Study Habits to Improve Learning]] youtube video.

* Notes: Structuring Days Like the Best Students (Huberman)

** Source study
- ~700 medical students, approx. equal male/female
- Self-report survey; correlation, not causation
- Identifies ~10 habits; top 5-6 account for most of the effect

** Top habits of high-performing students
- Scheduled, designated study time (treated as a calendar block)
- Studied alone, not in groups
- Phone away; signaled to friends/family as unreachable
- 3-4 hours/day, broken into 2-3 sessions (not one long block)
- Consistent ≥5 days/week
- Taught peers the material

** Teaching peers
- Counterintuitive: no observed penalty for "giving away" the material
- Lab heuristic: watch one, do one, teach one
- Teaching functions as a mastery test, not a concession
- Only applies where the act itself is safe

** Attention as a finite resource
- Adenosine accumulates during wake → sleepiness, degraded focus
- Sleep clears adenosine; focus budget resets
- Attention is limited but renewable
- Chronotype varies; late-night study viable for some

** Circadian entrainment of study time
- Brain/body entrains to regular schedules (like sleep/wake)
- ~3 days to establish a new rhythm
- Studying at fixed times makes focus semi-automatic at those times
- Recommendation: 1-2 fixed blocks/day, held through weekends where possible

** Focus itself is a trainable skill
- Not a trait; improves quickly with scheduling + repetition
- Distraction-elimination (phone, Wi-Fi if possible) is non-negotiable

** Motivation / identity
- Top students reported broad, abstract, long-horizon aspirations
  (family impact, life trajectory) — not granular goals
- Functions as pull-through during fatigue / motivation dips
- Less necessary when intrinsic interest in material is high

** Challenge is the mechanism
- Studying that feels hard is the most effective
- Effort is the cornerstone of learning — known for centuries
- No osmosis, no flow-state shortcut; desirable difficulty is the point

** Testing as a learning tool (not just evaluation)
- Among the most strongly supported findings in the peer-reviewed literature
- Retrieval practice builds and retains knowledge; offsets forgetting
- Self-quizzing during/after study is high-leverage, not optional

** Operational summary
- Fix 1-2 daily blocks, total ~3-4h, ≥5 days/week
- Solo, phone away, give it 3 days to entrain
- Close each block with retrieval, not rereading
- Teach the material to someone as a mastery check
- Attach an aspirational frame for friction days
