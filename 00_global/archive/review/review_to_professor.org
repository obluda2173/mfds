* Quick Summary of Project to Professor

** Philosophy

The traditional education approach heavily relies on paper notes, is static, and unserchable. To me, it also feels inherently ephemeral-existing only as neural patterns that cannot be externaly verified. Once exam is over, the textbooks are closed, the knowledge decays and is largely lost to time.

I have a strong desire and practical need to solidify my work, as otherwise it feels as through the knowledge simply slips through my fingers. Therefore, moving my education from the "Written Tradition" (passive accumulation) to the "Executable Tradition" (treating a degree as a continuous software project) is both an intuitive and necessary approach.

This philosophy is realized through the *Continuous Integration (CI) of the Mind*: applying DevOps principles to learning. By utilizung immediate LLM and programmatic feedback, logical errors are patched in real time in both the digital repository and my own brain.

** Tools Used:

  - Emacs/Org Mode :: The main workflow engine of the project. Plain text guarantees long-term searchability (=grep= my entire degree) and is the optimal format for future LLM ingestion.
  - LaTeX :: Heavily used for math notation throughout the repository.
  - CDLaTeX :: Speeding up LaTeX writing to match biological handwriting speed.
  - LLM Prompting :: Automation of monotonous work, solution validation, and exam preparation. *Crucially:* Used strictly for input validation and structural rigor, not for output generation.
  - Git/Github :: Provides version-controlled traceability. I can literally =diff= my comprehension of a topic over time.

** Results So Far

During the summer of 2025, I dedicated my time to learning the basics of Doom Emacs, LaTeX, and Org Mode. I utilized this approach from the very first day of my university studies.

Initially, my lack of speed and proficiency with CDLaTeX and the general workflow created a bottleneck, resulting in a grade of 4 on my STEOP exam. However, this struggle highlighted exactly where I needed to optimize my workflow. After refining my tool mastery and study methods, I recently achieved a grade of 2 on my Analysis exam.

This rapid improvement serves as a clear, objective demonstration of the method's efficacy, especially considering I am now preparing for my Linear Algebra exam with an even more streamlined process.

** Acknowledgement & Conclusion

I acknowledge that my current implementation only scratches the surface-perhaps reaching 30% of the project's ultimate potential. In its current state, the repository is primarily a "data ingestion" phase, focusing on raw data capture and high-level LLM interaction.

However, I believe this project represents a fundamental shift in educational principles. This initial data-capturing phase is not the finish line, but rather the fertile soil required for a compounding knowledge base to grow.

Regarding potential critiques of "overengineering": the high barrier to entry (learning Emacs, Git, LaTeX) acts as a forcing function for metacognition. I am forced to structure and process math before I can save it. These tools are a massive long-term investment. For me, this is not a fun weekend project; it is an absolute necessity-the foundational operating system for my academic and professional future.
