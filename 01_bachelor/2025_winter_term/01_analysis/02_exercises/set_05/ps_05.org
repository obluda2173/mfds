*** Solution

$\begin{aligned}
f(x) = e^{2x} + x \\
f'(x) = 2e^{2x} + 1 \\
\end{aligned}$

Since $\forall x \in \mathbb{R}, f'(x) > 0$, as $\forall x \in \mathbb{R}, e^{x} > 0$, $f(x)$ is monotonically increasing (injective).

The function $g : \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}, g(x) = e^{2x}$ is continuous.

The function $k : \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}, k(x) = x$ is also continuous.

Hence the function $f : \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}, f(x) = g(x) + k(x) = e^{2x} + x$ is continuous.

Since $\lim_{x \rightarrow -\infty} f(x) = -\infty$, $\lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} f(x) = \infty$ and the fact $f(x)$ is continious, by the intermediate value theorem $f(x)$ is surjective.

Given $f(x)$ is both injective and surjective, it is also bijective, which implies $f(x)$ is invertable.

$\begin{aligned}
(f^{-1})'(y) = \dfrac{1}{f'(x)} = \dfrac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(y))} \\
\end{aligned}$

$\begin{aligned}
1 = e^{2x} + x \\
x = 0 \\
(f^{-1})'(1) &= \dfrac{1}{2e^{2 \cdot 0} + 1}\\
             &= \dfrac{1}{2 \cdot 1 + 1}\\
             &= \dfrac{1}{3}\\
\end{aligned}$

#+begin_quote
*Mean Value Theorem.* If $f: \left[ a, b \right] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ is continuous and differentiable on $\left( a, b \right)$, then there is $\xi \in \left( a,b \right)$ such that

$$\frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b-a} = f'(\xi)$$
#+end_quote

1. Prove $f(x)$ is continuous on $\left[ 1,e \right]$.

   The natural logarithmic function $f(x) = log(x)$ is defined and continuous $\forall x > 0$. Since $\left[ 1,e \right] \subset \left( 0,\infty \right)$, $f(x)$ is continuous on $\left[ 1,e \right]$.

2. Prove $f(x)$ is differentiable on $\left( 1,e \right)$.

   $f'(x) = \dfrac{1}{x}$ is being defined $\forall x \neq 0$ and $0 \notin \left[ 1, e \right]$, implies $f(x)$ is differentiable on $\left( 1,e \right)$.

3. Apply /Mean Value Theorem/.

   $$\frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b-a} = f'(\xi)$$

   $\begin{aligned}
   \frac{1}{\xi} &= \dfrac{log(e) - \log(1)}{e - 1} & \text{(substituting values)} \\
   \frac{1}{\xi} &= \dfrac{1 - 0}{e - 1} & \text{(simplification)} \\
   \frac{1}{\xi} &= \dfrac{1}{e - 1} \\
   \end{aligned}$

4. Solve for $\xi$.

   $\begin{aligned}
   \frac{1}{\xi} &= \dfrac{1}{e - 1} \\
   \xi &= e - 1 \\
   \xi &\approx 1.7183... \\
   \end{aligned}$

5. Verify $\xi$ lies in the $\left( 1,e \right)$.

   $1 < 1.7183\dots < 2.718\dots$

   Hence, $\xi$ is valid.

*** Solution
Based on the visualization, the local extrema appear to be at $x=0$ (maximum) and $x=2$ (minimum).

1. *Find Critical Points ($f'(x) = 0$)*

   $\begin{aligned}
   f(x) &= x^{3} - 3x^{2} + 2 \\
   f'(x) &= 3x^{2} - 6x
   \end{aligned}$

   Set the derivative to zero:

   $\begin{aligned}
   0 &= 3x(x - 2) \\
   \implies x_{1} &= 0, \quad x_{2} = 2
   \end{aligned}$

2. *Classify Extrema (Second Derivative Test)*

   Calculate $f''(x)$:
   $$
   f''(x) = 6x - 6
   $$

   Evaluate at critical points:
   - *At $x_1 = 0$:*

     $f''(0) = 6(0) - 6 = -6 < 0$

     Since $f''(0)$ is negative, $f(x)$ has a *local maximum* at $x=0$.

   - *At $x_2 = 2$:*

     $f''(2) = 6(2) - 6 = 6 > 0$

     Since $f''(2)$ is positive, $f(x)$ has a *local minimum* at $x=2$.

   *Conclusion:* The guess was correct. There is a local maximum at $x=0$ and a local minimum at $x=2$.

*** Solution
To find the global extrema of a continuous function on a closed interval $[-2, 2]$, we must compare the function values at:
1.  *Critical points* inside the interval $(-2, 2)$.
2.  *Endpoints* of the interval.

*1. Find Critical Points*
Set $f'(x) = 0$:

$\begin{aligned}
f(x) &= x^{4} - 2x^{2} + 1 \\
f'(x) &= 4x^{3} - 4x \\
0 &= 4x(x^2 - 1) \\
0 &= 4x(x - 1)(x + 1)
\end{aligned}$

The critical points are $x \in \{0, 1, -1\}$. All lie within $[-2, 2]$.

*2. Evaluate Candidates*
We calculate $f(x)$ for all critical points and endpoints:
|-------------+-------+-------------------------------------+--------|
| Type        | $x$   | Calculation                         | $f(x)$ |
| :---        | :---: | :---                                | :---:  |
| Endpoint    | $-2$  | $(-2)^4 - 2(-2)^2 + 1 = 16 - 8 + 1$ | *9*    |
| Critical Pt | $-1$  | $(-1)^4 - 2(-1)^2 + 1 = 1 - 2 + 1$  | *0*    |
| Critical Pt | $0$   | $0^4 - 2(0)^2 + 1$                  | $1$    |
| Critical Pt | $1$   | $1^4 - 2(1)^2 + 1$                  | *0*    |
| Endpoint    | $2$   | $2^4 - 2(2)^2 + 1$                  | *9*    |
|-------------+-------+-------------------------------------+--------|
*3. Conclusion*
Comparing the values:
- The *Global Maximum* is *9*, occurring at $x = -2$ and $x = 2$.
- The *Global Minimum* is *0*, occurring at $x = -1$ and $x = 1$.

* Problem 5 :DONE:
** Task
Suppose a rectangle with side length $x$ and $y$ has the perimeter $20$.
(a) Express the area $A$ as a function of $x$.
(b) Determine the maximal possible area.

** Notes

** Solution
*** (a) Express Area $A(x)$
The perimeter is given by $2x + 2y = 20$. Dividing by 2, we get the constraint:
$$ x + y = 10 \implies y = 10 - x $$

The area $A$ is the product of the sides ($xy$). Substituting $y$:
$$ A(x) = x(10 - x) = 10x - x^2 $$

Since side lengths must be non-negative ($x \ge 0$ and $y = 10-x \ge 0$), the domain is $x \in [0, 10]$.

$$ A : [0, 10] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}, \quad A(x) = -x^2 + 10x $$

*** (b) Determine Maximal Area
To find the maximum, we look for critical points where the derivative is zero.

$\begin{aligned}
A(x) &= -x^2 + 10x \\
A'(x) &= -2x + 10 \\
0 &= -2x + 10 \\
2x &= 10 \\
x &= 5
\end{aligned}$

We verify this against the endpoints of the domain $[0, 10]$:
- $A(0) = 0$ (Minimum)
- $A(10) = 0$ (Minimum)
- $A(5) = 5(10 - 5) = 25$ (Maximum)

*Conclusion:* The maximal possible area is *25* (occurring when the rectangle is a square with $x=5$).
