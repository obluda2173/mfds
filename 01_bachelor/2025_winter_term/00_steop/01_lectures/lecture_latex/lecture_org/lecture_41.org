* Vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$
** Definition
A vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is an n-tuple $(x_1, \dots, x_n)$ of real numbers. Vectors correspond to points in space.
- $\mathbb{R}^1$: Real line.
- $\mathbb{R}^2$: Plane, $x = (x_1, x_2)$.
- $\mathbb{R}^3$: Space, $x = (x_1, x_2, x_3)$.
- $\mathbb{R}^n$: $x = (x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n)$.

** Notation
It is common in Linear Algebra to represent vectors as column vectors:
$$ x = \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n \end{pmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n \end{bmatrix} $$
Alternatively, using the transpose notation for space efficiency:
$$ x = (x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n)^T $$

** Operations
Given vectors $x, y \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and a scalar $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$:
1.  *Addition*:
    $$ x + y = (x_1 + y_1, x_2 + y_2, \dots, x_n + y_n) $$
2.  *Scalar Multiplication*:
    $$ \alpha x = (\alpha x_1, \alpha x_2, \dots, \alpha x_n) $$

** Properties of Operations
For all $x, y, z \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}$:
1.  $x + y = y + x$ (Commutativity)
2.  $x + (y + z) = (x + y) + z$ (Associativity)
3.  $(\alpha \beta)x = \alpha (\beta x)$
4.  $(\alpha + \beta)x = \alpha x + \beta x$
5.  $\alpha (x + y) = \alpha x + \alpha y$

* The Standard Basis and Inner Product
** The Standard Basis
We define the special basis vectors $e_i$ where the i-th component is 1 and all others are 0:
$$ e_1 = (1, 0, \dots, 0)^T $$
$$ e_2 = (0, 1, \dots, 0)^T $$
$$ e_n = (0, 0, \dots, 1)^T $$

Any vector $x$ can be written as a linear combination of basis vectors:
$$ x = x_1e_1 + x_2e_2 + \dots + x_ne_n = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i e_i $$

** Scalar / Dot / Inner Product
The scalar product (or dot/inner product) of two vectors $x, y \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the number:
$$ \langle x, y \rangle = x \cdot y = x^T y = x_1y_1 + x_2y_2 + \dots + x_ny_n = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i y_i $$

*** Properties
1.  $(x + y)^T z = x^T z + y^T z$
2.  $x^T y = y^T x$ (Symmetry)
3.  $(\alpha x)^T y = \alpha (x^T y)$
4.  Vectors $x$ and $y$ are *orthogonal* if and only if $x^T y = 0$.

*** Norm (Length)
The length of a vector $x$ is:
$$ |x| = \|x\| = \sqrt{x^T x} = \sqrt{x_1^2 + x_2^2 + \dots + x_n^2} $$

*** Examples of Inner Product Operations
1.  Selecting a component: $e_i^T x = x_i$
2.  Summing components: Let $\mathbf{1} = (1, 1, \dots, 1)^T$. Then $\mathbf{1}^T x = x_1 + \dots + x_n$.
3.  Averaging components: $\frac{\mathbf{1}^T}{n} x = \frac{x_1 + \dots + x_n}{n}$.
4.  Zero vector: $0 = (0, \dots, 0) \in \mathbb{R}^n$.

* Hyperplanes
** Definition
A *hyperplane* in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is the set:
$$ \{ x \in \mathbb{R}^n : a^T x = c \} $$
for any fixed vector $a \in \mathbb{R}^n$ ($a \neq 0$) and $c \in \mathbb{R}$.
The vector $a = (a_1, \dots, a_n)$ is the *normal vector* (orthogonal to the hyperplane).

Geometric interpretation:
- In $\mathbb{R}^2$: $a_1x_1 + a_2x_2 = c$ (A line).
- In $\mathbb{R}^3$: $a_1x_1 + a_2x_2 + a_3x_3 = c$ (A plane).
- In $\mathbb{R}^n$: A hyperplane.

** Example Problems
*** Problem 1: Parallel Hyperplane
Find a hyperplane parallel to $2x_1 - 3x_2 + x_3 - x_4 = 2$ that passes through the point $P(1, 1, 2, 2)$.

*Solution:*
A parallel hyperplane has the same normal vector $a = (2, -3, 1, -1)$. We need to find the new constant $c$ by substituting the point $P$:
$$ 2(1) - 3(1) + (2) - (2) = c $$
$$ 2 - 3 + 2 - 2 = -1 $$
$$ c = -1 $$
*Answer:* $2x_1 - 3x_2 + x_3 - x_4 = -1$.

*** Problem 2: Unit Vector
Find a unit vector in the direction of $v = (5, 2, -1, 0, 1)$.

*Solution:*
First, calculate the length of $v$:
$$ |v| = \sqrt{5^2 + 2^2 + (-1)^2 + 0^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{25 + 4 + 1 + 0 + 1} = \sqrt{31} $$
The unit vector is $\frac{v}{|v|}$:
$$ u = \frac{1}{\sqrt{31}}(5, 2, -1, 0, 1) $$

* General Vector Spaces
** Definition
A *vector space* $V$ is a collection of elements (called vectors) along with two operations: addition of vectors and multiplication by a scalar (number), such that the following axioms hold for all $a, b, c \in V$ and $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}$ (or $\mathbb{C}$):

1.  $a + b = b + a$ (Commutativity)
2.  $a + (b + c) = (a + b) + c$ (Associativity)
3.  There exists a *zero vector* $0 \in V$ such that $a + 0 = a$ for all $a \in V$.
4.  For every $a \in V$, there exists an *additive inverse* $b \in V$ such that $a + b = 0$. Notation: $b = -a$.
5.  $1 \cdot a = a$ (Identity)
6.  $(\alpha \beta) a = \alpha (\beta a)$
7.  $\alpha (a + b) = \alpha a + \alpha b$
8.  $(\alpha + \beta) a = \alpha a + \beta a$

** Examples
1.  $\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{R}^2, \dots, \mathbb{R}^n$.
2.  $\mathbb{C}, \mathbb{C}^n$.
3.  $P_n = \{ p(t) \in \mathbb{R}[t] : \text{deg}(p) \le n \}$ (Polynomials of degree at most $n$).
4.  $F = \{ f: [0, 1] \to \mathbb{R} \}$ (Functions mapping interval to reals).

** Non-Examples
1.  $P_n^= = \{ p(t) \in \mathbb{R}[t] : \text{deg}(p) = n \}$ (Polynomials of degree exactly $n$).
    - *Reason:* Not a vector space because it does not contain the zero polynomial (degree undefined or $-\infty$), and the sum of two degree $n$ polynomials might have degree $< n$.
2.  $V = \{ f: [0, 1] \to \mathbb{R}^+ \}$ (Non-negative functions).
    - *Reason:* Fails axiom 4. If $f(x) > 0$, then $-f(x)$ is not in the set.

** Theorems
1.  *Uniqueness of Zero*: A vector space has a unique zero vector.
    *Proof:* Suppose $0$ and $0'$ are zeros. Then $0' = 0' + 0 = 0 + 0' = 0$.
2.  *Properties*:
    - The additive inverse is unique.
    - $0 \cdot a = 0$ (Scalar zero times vector).
    - $\alpha \cdot 0 = 0$ (Scalar times vector zero).
    - $(-1) \cdot a = -a$.
