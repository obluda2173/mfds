* Binary Operations and Axioms
** Definitions
A binary operation (or function) over a set $S$ is a function $f: S \times S \to S$.
- Example: Addition $+: \mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ where $+(1, 2) = 3$.

Let $S$ be a set with two binary operations:
1.  $+$ (called addition).
2.  $\cdot$ (called multiplication).

** Axioms
We define the following axioms for elements $x, y, z \in S$:

*** Addition Axioms (A)
- **A1 (Closure):** If $x, y \in S$, then $x+y \in S$.
- **A2 (Commutativity):** $x + y = y + x$.
- **A3 (Associativity):** $x + (y + z) = (x + y) + z$.
- **A4 (Zero/Identity):** There exists an element $0$ such that $x + 0 = 0 + x = x$ for all $x \in S$.
- **A5 (Additive Inverse):** For every $x \in S$, there exists an element $(-x) \in S$ such that $x + (-x) = 0$. The equation $a + x = 0$ has a unique solution.

*** Multiplication Axioms (M)
- **M1 (Closure):** If $x, y \in S$, then $x \cdot y \in S$.
- **M2 (Commutativity):** $xy = yx$.
- **M3 (Associativity):** $x(yz) = (xy)z$.
- **M4 (Identity):** There exists an element $1 \in S$ such that $x \cdot 1 = 1 \cdot x = x$ for all $x \in S$.
- **M5 (Multiplicative Inverse):** For all $x \in S$ where $x \neq 0$, there exists an element $x^{-1} \in S$ such that $x \cdot x^{-1} = x^{-1} \cdot x = 1$. Every equation $ax = 1$ ($a \neq 0$) has a unique solution.

*** Distributive Axiom (D)
- **D:** $x(y+z) = xy + xz$ and $(x+y)z = xz + yz$.

* Algebraic Structures
We classify algebraic structures based on which axioms they satisfy.

** Definitions
1.  **Ring:** A set $(S, +, \cdot)$ that satisfies axioms **A1-A5**, **M1**, **M3**, and **D**.
2.  **Commutative Ring:** A ring that also satisfies **M2**.
3.  **Commutative Ring with Identity:** A ring that satisfies **A1-A5**, **M1-M4**, and **D**.
4.  **Integral Domain:** A commutative ring with identity that has no zero divisors.
    - Condition: If $xy = 0$, then $x = 0$ or $y = 0$.
5.  **Field:** A set $(S, +, \cdot)$ that satisfies all axioms (**A1-A5, M1-M5, D**).

** Hierarchy
Field $\subset$ Integral Domain $\subset$ Commutative Ring with Identity $\subset$ Commutative Ring $\subset$ Ring.

* Examples
1.  **Reals:** $(\mathbb{R}, +, \cdot)$ with usual operations is a field.
2.  **Integers:** $(\mathbb{Z}, +, \cdot)$ is an integral domain.
3.  **Rationals:** $(\mathbb{Q}, +, \cdot)$ is a field.
4.  **Modulo Arithmetic:** $\mathbb{Z}_m = \{0, 1, 2, \dots, m-1\}$ with modulo operations.
    - If $m=6$, $\mathbb{Z}_6$ is a commutative ring with identity but *not* an integral domain.
    - *Reason:* $\overline{2} \cdot \overline{3} = \overline{0}$, but $\overline{2} \neq \overline{0}$ and $\overline{3} \neq \overline{0}$.
5.  **Even Integers:** $2\mathbb{Z} = \{2n : n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ with usual operations is a commutative ring. It does not have an identity element.
6.  **Odd Integers:** $S = \{2n + 1 : n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ is *not* a ring because it is not closed under addition.
7.  **Functions:** $S = \{f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\}$ with pointwise addition $(f+g)(x) = f(x) + g(x)$ and multiplication $(fg)(x) = f(x)g(x)$.
    - Zero element: $h(x) = 0$ for all $x$.
    - Identity element: $g(x) = 1$ for all $x$.
    - This is a commutative ring with identity.
    - Is it an integral domain? No, if $f \cdot g = 0$, it does not imply $f=0$ or $g=0$ (functions can be zero on disjoint intervals).

* Properties and Theorems
** Lemma: Properties of Rings
Let $R$ be a ring. Then for all $a, b \in R$:
1.  $a \cdot 0 = 0 \cdot a = 0$.
2.  $a \cdot (-b) = (-a) \cdot b = -(ab)$.
3.  $(-a)(-b) = ab$.

*** Proof of (1)
$$ a \cdot 0 = a \cdot (0 + 0) = a \cdot 0 + a \cdot 0 $$
Adding the additive inverse $-(a \cdot 0)$ to both sides implies $0 = a \cdot 0$.

*** Proof of (2)
We show that $a(-b)$ is the additive inverse of $ab$, i.e., $ab + a(-b) = 0$.
$$ ab + a(-b) = a(b + (-b)) = a \cdot 0 = 0 $$
Thus, $a(-b) = -(ab)$. Similarly for $(-a)b$.

** Theorem: Fields and Integral Domains
Every field is an integral domain.

*** Proof
Let $F$ be a field. Let $x, y \in F$ such that $x \cdot y = 0$.
Suppose $x \neq 0$. By M5, $x^{-1}$ exists.
Multiply by $x^{-1}$:
$$ x^{-1} \cdot (x \cdot y) = x^{-1} \cdot 0 $$
$$ (x^{-1} \cdot x) \cdot y = 0 $$
$$ 1 \cdot y = 0 \implies y = 0 $$
Thus, no zero divisors exist.

** Theorem: Finite Integral Domains
A finite integral domain is a field.

*** Proof
Let $D = \{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\}$ be a finite integral domain.
Consider any $a \in D$ with $a \neq 0$.
Consider the set of products $\{ax_1, ax_2, \dots, ax_n\}$.
These elements are all distinct.
*Reason:* If $ax_i = ax_j$, then $a(x_i - x_j) = 0$. Since $D$ is an integral domain and $a \neq 0$, it implies $x_i - x_j = 0$, so $x_i = x_j$.
Since the set of products equals $D$ (surjection onto a finite set), one of the elements must be the identity $1$.
Therefore, there exists some $x_k$ such that $a \cdot x_k = 1$, which implies $x_k = a^{-1}$.
