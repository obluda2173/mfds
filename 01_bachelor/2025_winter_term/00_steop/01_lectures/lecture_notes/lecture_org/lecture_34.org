* Polynomial Rings
** Definition
Let $R$ be a commutative ring with identity. The sum
$$ a_n x^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \dots + a_1 x + a_0 $$
where $a_i \in R$, is a polynomial in the variable $x$ (symbol).
The set of all such polynomials is a ring, denoted $R[x]$.
Note: $R \subset R[x]$.

** Examples
- $\mathbb{R}[x], \mathbb{Z}[x], \mathbb{Q}[x], \mathbb{C}[x]$.

*** Example in $\mathbb{Z}_3[x]$
Let $p(x) = x^3 + 2x + 1$ and $g(x) = x^2 + x + 2$.
Calculating the sum (coefficients modulo 3):
$$ p(x) + g(x) = x^3 + x^2 + (2+1)x + (1+2) = x^3 + x^2 $$

** The Importance of the Ring
The properties of a polynomial depend on the ring over which it is defined.

*** Example: Is $x^2 + 1$ a perfect square?
1.  *In $\mathbb{Z}[x]$*: **No.**
    Suppose $x^2 + 1 = p(x)^2$.
    Degree analysis: $\deg(p^2) = 2 \implies \deg(p) = 1$. Let $p(x) = ax + b$.
    $$ p(x)^2 = a^2x^2 + 2abx + b^2 $$
    Comparing coefficients: $a^2 = 1, b^2 = 1$, and $2ab = 0$.
    Since $a, b \neq 0$, $2ab \neq 0$ in $\mathbb{Z}$. Thus, impossible.

2.  *In $\mathbb{Z}_2[x]$*: **Yes.**
    $$ (x+1)^2 = x^2 + 2x + 1 = x^2 + 1 $$
    (Since $2 \equiv 0$ in $\mathbb{Z}_2$).

* Roots and Factorization
** Fundamental Theorem of Algebra
Every non-constant polynomial in $\mathbb{C}[x]$ has a complex root.
Every polynomial in $\mathbb{C}[x]$ has a unique factorization:
$$ p(x) = a(x - x_1) \cdot \dots \cdot (x - x_n) $$
where $a \neq 0$ and $x_1, \dots, x_n \in \mathbb{C}$.

** Conjugate Roots Lemma
If a polynomial $p(x) \in \mathbb{R}[x]$ has a root $z = a + bi$, then it has a root $\bar{z} = a - bi$.
$$ p(a+bi) = 0 \iff p(a-bi) = 0 $$

*** Proof
Let $p(x) = a_n x^n + \dots + a_1 x + a_0$ with $a_i \in \mathbb{R}$.
Suppose $p(z) = 0$.
$$ a_n z^n + \dots + a_1 z + a_0 = 0 $$
Take the conjugate of the entire equation:
$$ \overline{a_n z^n + \dots + a_1 z + a_0} = \overline{0} $$
Using properties of conjugation ($\overline{z_1 + z_2} = \overline{z_1} + \overline{z_2}$ and $\overline{z_1 z_2} = \overline{z_1} \cdot \overline{z_2}$):
$$ \overline{a_n} \overline{z}^n + \dots + \overline{a_1} \overline{z} + \overline{a_0} = 0 $$
Since the coefficients are real ($a_i \in \mathbb{R}$), $\overline{a_i} = a_i$.
$$ a_n \overline{z}^n + \dots + a_1 \overline{z} + a_0 = 0 $$
Thus, $p(\bar{z}) = 0$.

*** Note on the Ring
The ring matters! Consider $p(x) = x^2 + ix$ in $\mathbb{C}[x]$. This logic does not hold because coefficients are not all real.

** Equality of Polynomials
Question: When is $p = g$?
1.  *Algebraic perspective:* $p = g$ if and only if $\deg(p) = \deg(g)$ and corresponding coefficients are equal.
2.  *Functional perspective:* Let $p: D \to R$ and $g: D \to R$. $p = g$ if and only if $D = D'$ and $p(x) = g(x)$ for all $x \in D$.

* Polar Coordinates
** Definitions
- *Cartesian Coordinates:* $P(x, y)$
- *Polar Coordinates:* $P(r, \theta)$
  - $r = |OP|$ (distance from origin)
  - $\theta$ is the angle between $OP$ and the x-axis.
  - Domains: $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$ or $\theta \in (-\pi, \pi]$.

** Graphing in Polar Coordinates
Let $f$ be a function. The graph of $f$ in polar coordinates is the collection of all points $(r, \theta)$ such that $r = f(\theta)$.

** Conversion Formulas
From Polar to Cartesian:
$$ x = r \cos \theta $$
$$ y = r \sin \theta $$

From Cartesian to Polar:
$$ r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} $$
$$ \theta = \arctan\left(\frac{y}{x}\right) $$

** Examples
*** 1. Constant Radius
Equation: $r = 2$
Shape: Circle centered at origin with radius 2.
In Cartesian: $x^2 + y^2 = 2^2$.

*** 2. Archimedean Spiral
Equation: $r = \theta$
Shape: A spiral starting at the origin and expanding as $\theta$ increases.

*** 3. Cardioid
Equation: $r = 1 - \sin \theta$
Shape: A heart shape.
- $\theta = 0 \implies r = 1$
- $\theta = \frac{\pi}{2} \implies r = 0$ (Cusp at origin)
- $\theta = \pi \implies r = 1$
- $\theta = \frac{3\pi}{2} \implies r = 2$
