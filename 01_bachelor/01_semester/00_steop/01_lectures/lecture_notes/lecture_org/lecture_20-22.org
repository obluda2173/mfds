* Functions
** Definition
Let $X$ and $Y$ be two sets. What is a function from $X$ to $Y$?
A *function* $f$ from $X$ to $Y$ is a relation $f \subseteq X \times Y$ such that for every $x \in X$, there exists exactly one $y \in Y$ such that $(x, y) \in f$.

** Notation
$$ f: X \to Y $$
$$ x \mapsto y \quad \text{or} \quad y = f(x) \iff (x, y) \in f $$
- $x \in X$ is an *argument*.
- $y = f(x)$ is a *value*.
- The function maps $X$ *into* $Y$.
- Synonyms: map, mapping, correspondence, transformation.

** Graph of a Function
The set $\{ (x, f(x)) : x \in X \}$ is called the *graph* of $f$.

** Domain and Range
For $f: X \to Y$:
- The set $X$ is called the *domain* of $f$.
- The set $Y$ is called the *codomain* (or target set) of $f$.
- The set of actual values $\{ f(x) : x \in X \}$ is called the *range* or *image* of $f$.

** Examples
1.  Function from $\mathbb{Z}$ to $\mathbb{Z}$:
    - $f = \{ (x, y) \in \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z} : 3x + 2y = 0 \}$
      If $x=1$, then $3(1) + 2y = 0 \implies 2y = -3$, which has no integer solution. Thus, this relation is *not* a function on $\mathbb{Z}$.
    - $g = \{ (x, y) \in \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z} : y = 0 \}$. This is a function $g(x) = 0$.

2.  Defining Functions:
    - $f_1: \mathbb{R} \setminus \{2\} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $f_1(x) = \frac{x^2 - 4}{x-2}$.
    - $f_2: \mathbb{R} \setminus \{2\} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $f_2(x) = x+2$.
    - Are $f_1$ and $f_2$ equal? *YES!* Because their domains are the same and for all $x$ in the domain, $\frac{x^2-4}{x-2} = x+2$.
    - Note: $g: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $g(x) = x+2$ is *not* equal to $f_1$ because the domains differ ($f_1$ is not defined at $x=2$).

3.  Function on pairs:
    - $f: \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}$ defined by $f(m, n) = m - n$.

* Properties of Functions
Let $f: X \to Y$.

** Equality of Functions
Two functions $f: X_1 \to Y_1$ and $g: X_2 \to Y_2$ are *equal* if:
1.  $X_1 = X_2$ (Same Domain).
2.  $Y_1 = Y_2$ (Same Codomain - sometimes omitted depending on context).
3.  $f(x) = g(x)$ for all $x \in X_1$.

** Injectivity (One-to-One)
A function $f$ is *injective* (or one-to-one) if:
$$ \forall x_1, x_2 \in X, f(x_1) = f(x_2) \implies x_1 = x_2 $$
Equivalently:
$$ x_1 \ne x_2 \implies f(x_1) \ne f(x_2) $$

*Examples:*
- $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}, f(x) = x^2$ is *not* injective ($f(1) = f(-1)$).
- $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}, f(x) = x^3$ is injective.
- $f: \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\} \to \mathbb{R}, f(x) = \frac{1}{x} + 1$ is injective.

** Surjectivity (Onto)
A function $f$ is *surjective* (or onto) if:
$$ \forall y \in Y, \exists x \in X \text{ such that } f(x) = y $$
This means the range of $f$ is equal to the codomain $Y$ ($f(X) = Y$).

*Examples:*
- $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}, f(x) = x^2$ is *not* surjective (negative numbers are not in the image).
- $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}, f(x) = x^2$ *is* surjective.
- $f: \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\} \to \mathbb{R}, f(x) = \frac{1}{x} + 1$ is *not* surjective (cannot take value 1).

** Bijectivity
A function $f$ is *bijective* if it is both injective and surjective.

*Example:*
- $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}, f(x) = x+2$ is bijective.

** Complex Example
Let $f: \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}$ defined by $f(m, n) = (m+n, m+2n)$.

*Injectivity Check:*
Suppose $f(m, n) = f(a, b)$.
$$ (m+n, m+2n) = (a+b, a+2b) $$
$$ \implies \begin{cases} m+n = a+b \\ m+2n = a+2b \end{cases} $$
Subtracting the first eq from the second: $n = b$.
Substituting back: $m+b = a+b \implies m = a$.
Thus $(m, n) = (a, b)$. $f$ is injective.

*Surjectivity Check:*
Let $(a, b) \in \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}$. Can we find $(m, n)$ such that $f(m, n) = (a, b)$?
$$ \begin{cases} m+n = a \\ m+2n = b \end{cases} $$
Subtracting: $n = b - a$.
Then $m = a - n = a - (b - a) = 2a - b$.
Since $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$, $m$ and $n$ are integers.
Thus $f$ is surjective.
*Conclusion:* $f$ is bijective.

* Composition of Functions
** Definition
Let $f: X \to Y$ and $g: Y \to Z$.
The *composition* of $g$ and $f$ is the function $g \circ f: X \to Z$ defined by:
$$ (g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x)) $$

*Note:* $g \circ f$ makes sense when codomain of $f$ matches domain of $g$. $f \circ g$ usually makes no sense unless $X=Z$. Even if defined, $f \circ g \ne g \circ f$ generally.

** Associativity
Composition is associative.
Let $f: A \to B, g: B \to C, h: C \to D$.
Then $(h \circ g) \circ f = h \circ (g \circ f)$.
*Proof:*
For any $x \in A$:
$$ ((h \circ g) \circ f)(x) = (h \circ g)(f(x)) = h(g(f(x))) $$
$$ (h \circ (g \circ f))(x) = h((g \circ f)(x)) = h(g(f(x))) $$
The domains and codomains match.

** Properties under Composition
Let $f: X \to Y$ and $g: Y \to Z$.
1.  If $f$ and $g$ are injective $\implies g \circ f$ is injective.
    *Proof:* $(g \circ f)(x_1) = (g \circ f)(x_2) \implies g(f(x_1)) = g(f(x_2))$. Since $g$ is injective, $f(x_1) = f(x_2)$. Since $f$ is injective, $x_1 = x_2$.
2.  If $f$ and $g$ are surjective $\implies g \circ f$ is surjective.
    *Proof:* For any $z \in Z$, since $g$ is surjective, $\exists y \in Y$ such that $g(y) = z$. Since $f$ is surjective, $\exists x \in X$ such that $f(x) = y$. Then $(g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x)) = g(y) = z$.

* Inverse Functions
** Identity Function
The *identity function* on $X$ is $id_X: X \to X$ defined by $id_X(x) = x$.

** Inverse Relation
Let $R \subseteq X \times Y$ be a relation. The *inverse relation* $R^{-1} \subseteq Y \times X$ is:
$$ R^{-1} = \{ (y, x) \in Y \times X : (x, y) \in R \} $$

** Inverse Function Definition
Let $f: X \to Y$ be a *bijection*.
The *inverse function* $f^{-1}: Y \to X$ is the inverse relation of $f$.
$$ f^{-1}(y) = x \iff f(x) = y $$

*Properties:*
- $f^{-1}$ is a function only if $f$ is bijective.
- $f^{-1} \circ f = id_X$
- $f \circ f^{-1} = id_Y$
- Note: $f^{-1} \ne \frac{1}{f}$.

** Calculation of Inverses
1.  $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}, f(x) = x^3 - 1$.
    Let $y = x^3 - 1 \iff x^3 = y+1 \iff x = \sqrt[3]{y+1}$.
    Thus $f^{-1}(y) = \sqrt[3]{y+1}$.

2.  $f: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2, f(x, y) = ((x^2+1)y, x^3)$.
    Let $(a, b) = ((x^2+1)y, x^3)$.
    From 2nd component: $x^3 = b \implies x = b^{1/3}$.
    From 1st component: $(x^2+1)y = a \implies (b^{2/3}+1)y = a \implies y = \frac{a}{b^{2/3}+1}$.
    Thus $f^{-1}(a, b) = (b^{1/3}, \frac{a}{b^{2/3}+1})$.

3.  $f: \mathbb{Z}^2 \to \mathbb{Z}^2, f(m, n) = (5m+4n, 4m+3n)$.
    Solving the system for $m, n$ yields:
    $f^{-1}(a, b) = (4b - 3a, 4a - 5b)$. (Checking determinant $5(3) - 4(4) = -1$, so inverse exists in integers).

* Image and Preimage
Let $f: X \to Y$, $A \subseteq X$, $B \subseteq Y$.

** Definitions
- *Image of A:* $f(A) = \{ y \in Y : \exists a \in A, f(a) = y \} = \{ f(a) : a \in A \}$.
- *Preimage of B:* $f^{-1}(B) = \{ x \in X : f(x) \in B \}$.
  (Note: $f^{-1}(B)$ makes sense even if $f$ has no inverse function).

** Example
$f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}, f(x) = x^2$.
- $f(\{1, 2\}) = \{1, 4\}$.
- $f^{-1}(\{1, 4\}) = \{-1, 1, -2, 2\}$.
- $f^{-1}(f(A)) \ne A$ generally (e.g., $f^{-1}(f(\{1\})) = \{-1, 1\} \ne \{1\}$).
- $f^{-1}((1, 9)) = (-3, -1) \cup (1, 3)$.
- $f((-\infty, 1)) = [0, \infty)$ (assuming typo in notes, usually range is non-negative).

** Set Theoretic Properties
Let $A, B \subseteq X$ and $C, D \subseteq Y$.
1.  $f(A \cap B) \subseteq f(A) \cap f(B)$ (Equality does not hold generally).
2.  $f(A \cup B) = f(A) \cup f(B)$.
3.  $A \subseteq f^{-1}(f(A))$.
4.  $f^{-1}(C \cap D) = f^{-1}(C) \cap f^{-1}(D)$.
5.  $f^{-1}(C \cup D) = f^{-1}(C) \cup f^{-1}(D)$.
6.  $f(f^{-1}(C)) \subseteq C$.

*Proof of (5):*
$x \in f^{-1}(C \cup D) \iff f(x) \in C \cup D \iff f(x) \in C \lor f(x) \in D \iff x \in f^{-1}(C) \lor x \in f^{-1}(D) \iff x \in f^{-1}(C) \cup f^{-1}(D)$.

* Cardinality and Functions on Finite Sets
Let $X, Y$ be finite sets.
1.  If $f: X \to Y$ is *injective*, then $|X| \le |Y|$.
    *Proof:* $f(X) \subseteq Y$ has $|X|$ distinct elements. Thus $|X| = |f(X)| \le |Y|$.
2.  If $f: X \to Y$ is *surjective*, then $|X| \ge |Y|$.
    *Proof:* $|f(X)| = |Y|$. Since the image can't have more elements than the domain, $|Y| \le |X|$.
3.  If $f: X \to Y$ is *bijective*, then $|X| = |Y|$.
