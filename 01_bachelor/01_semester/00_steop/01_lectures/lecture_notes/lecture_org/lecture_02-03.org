* Propositional Logic
** Definitions and Basics
- *Definition*: A **proposition** is a statement that has a definite truth value (True or False, but not both).
- *Notation*: We use $P, Q, R, \dots$ to denote propositions.
  - $T = \text{True} = 1$
  - $F = \text{False} = 0$
- *Example*: $P(x)$: "A number $x$ is a prime number". (This becomes a proposition when $x$ is assigned a value).

** Connectives
*** Conjunction (AND)
- *Definition*: $P \wedge Q$ is true if **both** $P$ and $Q$ are true, and false otherwise.

| P | Q | $P \wedge Q$ |
|---+---+--------------|
| T | T | T            |
| T | F | F            |
| F | T | F            |
| F | F | F            |

*** Disjunction (OR)
- *Definition*: $P \vee Q$ is true if $P$ is true, **or** $Q$ is true, **or both**. It is false otherwise.
- *Note*: In mathematics, "or" is **inclusive**. In English, it is often exclusive (e.g., "soup or salad").

| P | Q | $P \vee Q$ |
|---+---+------------|
| T | T | T          |
| T | F | T          |
| F | T | T          |
| F | F | F          |

*** Negation (NOT)
- *Definition*: $\neg P$ (or $\sim P$, $\bar{P}$) means "it is not true that P".

| P | $\neg P$ |
|---+----------|
| T | F        |
| F | T        |

** Truth Tables and Examples

*Note*: The table demonstrates that logical expressions can be evaluated algorithmically for every combination of inputs.

** Logical Laws
*** 1. De Morgan's Laws
- $\neg(P \wedge Q) = \neg P \vee \neg Q$
- $\neg(P \vee Q) = \neg P \wedge \neg Q$

*Explanation*: The negation of "A and B" is "Not A or Not B". If it is not true that I ate both soup and salad, then I must have skipped at least one (Not soup or Not salad).

*** 2. Associative Laws
- $(P \wedge Q) \wedge R = P \wedge (Q \wedge R)$
- $(P \vee Q) \vee R = P \vee (Q \vee R)$

*** 3. Commutative Laws
- $P \wedge Q = Q \wedge P$
- $P \vee Q = Q \vee P$

*** 4. Distributive Laws
- $P \wedge (Q \vee R) = (P \wedge Q) \vee (P \wedge R)$
- $P \vee (Q \wedge R) = (P \vee Q) \wedge (P \vee R)$

*** 5. Double Negation Law
- $\neg(\neg P) = P$

** Conditional Propositions (Implication)
- *Definition*: $P \Rightarrow Q$ (or $P \rightarrow Q$) is read "If P, then Q".
- $P$ is the **hypothesis** (or antecedent).
- $Q$ is the **conclusion** (or consequent).

*** Truth Table for Implication

| P | Q | $P \Rightarrow Q$ |
|---+---+-------------------|
| T | T | T                 |
| T | F | F                 |
| F | T | T                 |
| F | F | T                 |

- *Crucial Note*: The implication is only false when $P$ is True and $Q$ is False.
- *Vacuous Truth*: If $P$ is False, the statement $P \Rightarrow Q$ is automatically True.
  - *Example*: "If I am elected, I will lower taxes." If I am not elected, the statement is not falsified regardless of whether taxes are lowered.

*** Equivalent Form
$$ P \Rightarrow Q \equiv \neg P \vee Q $$

*** Terminology for $P \Rightarrow Q$
- If $P$, then $Q$.
- $Q$ if $P$.
- $P$ is sufficient for $Q$.
- $Q$ when $P$.
- A necessary condition for $P$ is $Q$.
- $Q$ unless $\neg P$.
- $P$ implies $Q$.
- $P$ only if $Q$.
- $Q$ follows from $P$.

*** Variations of Conditional Statements
Given $P \Rightarrow Q$:
1.  **Converse**: $Q \Rightarrow P$
2.  **Contrapositive**: $\neg Q \Rightarrow \neg P$
    - *Note*: The contrapositive is logically equivalent to the original statement ($P \Rightarrow Q \equiv \neg Q \Rightarrow \neg P$).
3.  **Inverse**: $\neg P \Rightarrow \neg Q$

** Biconditional Propositions
- *Definition*: $P \Leftrightarrow Q$ (or $P \leftrightarrow Q$) means "$P$ is equivalent to $Q$".
- It represents $(P \Rightarrow Q) \wedge (Q \Rightarrow P)$.
- *Phrasing*: "$P$ if and only if $Q$" (iff).
- *Truth Table*: True only when $P$ and $Q$ have the same truth value.

| P | Q | $P \Leftrightarrow Q$ |
|---+---+-----------------------|
| T | T | T                     |
| T | F | F                     |
| F | T | F                     |
| F | F | T                     |

** Boolean Variables
- **Bit**: A binary digit that takes two values: 0 or 1.
- **Boolean Variable**: A variable that takes two values: True or False.

* Quantifiers
Let $X = \{x_1, x_2, \dots\}$.

** Universal Quantifier ($\forall$)
- *Definition*: "For all" or "For every".
- Notation: $\forall x \in X, P(x)$.
- Meaning: $P(x_1) \wedge P(x_2) \wedge \dots$ is true. All elements in $X$ have property $A$.

** Existential Quantifier ($\exists$)
- *Definition*: "There exists" or "There is".
- Notation: $\exists x \in X, P(x)$.
- Meaning: $P(x_1) \vee P(x_2) \vee \dots$ is true. There is at least one element in $X$ that has property $A$.

** Negation of Quantifiers (Generalized De Morgan's Laws)
- $\neg (\forall x \in X, P(x)) \equiv \exists x \in X, \neg P(x)$
- $\neg (\exists x \in X, P(x)) \equiv \forall x \in X, \neg P(x)$

*** Examples
1.  *Statement*: "For all real $x$, there is a real $y$ such that $x = y^3$."
    - Notation: $\forall x \in \mathbb{R}, \exists y \in \mathbb{R} : x = y^3$.
    - *Negation*: $\exists x \in \mathbb{R}, \forall y \in \mathbb{R} : x \neq y^3$.
2.  *Truth Value Check*:
    - $\forall x \in \mathbb{R} : x^2 > 0$ $\rightarrow$ **False** (Counterexample: $x=0$).
    - $\forall x \in \mathbb{R}, \exists n \in \mathbb{N} : x^n \ge 0$ $\rightarrow$ **True** (For any $x$, if $n$ is even, $x^n \ge 0$).
    - $\exists a \in \mathbb{R}, \forall x \in \mathbb{R} : a \cdot x = x$ $\rightarrow$ **True** (Let $a=1$).

* Set Theory and Logic Connection
There is a direct correspondence between Logical Operators and Set Operators. Let $P$ correspond to $x \in A$ and $Q$ correspond to $x \in B$.

| Logic                 | Set Theory           | Meaning                                 |
|-----------------------+----------------------+-----------------------------------------|
| $P \wedge Q$          | $A \cap B$           | Intersection ($x$ in $A$ and $B$)       |
| $P \vee Q$            | $A \cup B$           | Union ($x$ in $A$ or $B$)               |
| $\neg P$              | $A^c$ (or $\bar{A}$) | Complement ($x$ not in $A$)             |
| $P \Rightarrow Q$     | $A \subseteq B$      | Subset (If $x$ in $A$, then $x$ in $B$) |
| $P \Leftrightarrow Q$ | $A = B$              | Equality                                |

** Set Laws
The logical laws translate directly to sets:
- **De Morgan's Laws**:
  - $(A \cup B)^c = A^c \cap B^c$
  - $(A \cap B)^c = A^c \cup B^c$
- **Distributive Laws**:
  - $A \cap (B \cup C) = (A \cap B) \cup (A \cap C)$
  - $A \cup (B \cap C) = (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)$

** Proofs with Sets
*** Task 1: Subset Definition
$A \subseteq B \iff (\forall x)(x \in A \rightarrow x \in B)$.

*** Task 2: Set Equality
$A = B \iff A \subseteq B \wedge B \subseteq A$.
To prove equality, we usually prove two inclusions:
1.  $x \in A \Rightarrow x \in B$
2.  $x \in B \Rightarrow x \in A$

*** Problem: Prove $A \cup B = A \cup (B \setminus A)$
*Proof*:
1.  ($\subseteq$) Let $x \in A \cup B$.
    - If $x \in A$, then $x \in A \cup (B \setminus A)$.
    - If $x \notin A$, then since $x \in A \cup B$, $x$ must be in $B$. Thus $x \in B$ and $x \notin A$, which means $x \in B \setminus A$. Therefore $x \in A \cup (B \setminus A)$.
2.  ($\supseteq$) Let $x \in A \cup (B \setminus A)$.
    - This means $x \in A$ or $x \in (B \setminus A)$.
    - If $x \in A$, then $x \in A \cup B$.
    - If $x \in B \setminus A$, then $x \in B$. Thus $x \in A \cup B$.
