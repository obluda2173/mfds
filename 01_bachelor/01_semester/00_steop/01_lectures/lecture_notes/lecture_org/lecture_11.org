* Rationality of Square Roots (Continued Proof)
** Theorem: Rational Square Roots of Integers
Let $n \in \mathbb{N}$. Then $\sqrt{n}$ is rational if and only if $n$ is a perfect square.

*** Proof
We use the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic (Unique Prime Factorization).

Let the prime factorization of $n$ be:
$$ n = p_1^{\alpha_1} p_2^{\alpha_2} \dots p_k^{\alpha_k} $$

*Forward Direction ($\Leftarrow$)*:
If $n$ is a perfect square, then $n = a^2$ for some $a \in \mathbb{Z}$. Thus $\sqrt{n} = a \in \mathbb{Q}$.

*Reverse Direction ($\Rightarrow$)*:
Suppose $\sqrt{n} \in \mathbb{Q}$. Then we can write $\sqrt{n} = \frac{a}{b}$ where $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}, b \neq 0$, and $\gcd(a, b) = 1$ (fraction is in simplest form).

Squaring both sides:
$$ n = \frac{a^2}{b^2} \implies n \cdot b^2 = a^2 $$

Let the prime factorizations be:
$$ n = \prod p_i^{\alpha_i}, \quad b = \prod q_j^{\beta_j}, \quad a = \prod r_l^{\gamma_l} $$

Substituting these into the equation $n \cdot b^2 = a^2$:
$$ \left( \prod p_i^{\alpha_i} \right) \cdot \left( \prod q_j^{\beta_j} \right)^2 = \left( \prod r_l^{\gamma_l} \right)^2 $$
$$ \prod p_i^{\alpha_i} \cdot \prod q_j^{2\beta_j} = \prod r_l^{2\gamma_l} $$

By the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, the exponent of any prime factor on the Left Hand Side (LHS) must equal the exponent of that prime on the Right Hand Side (RHS).
- The exponents on the RHS ($2\gamma_l$) are all *even*.
- The exponents coming from $b^2$ on the LHS ($2\beta_j$) are all *even*.
- Therefore, the exponents from $n$ ($\alpha_i$) must also be *even* to maintain equality.

Since all $\alpha_i$ are even, let $\alpha_i = 2k_i$. Then:
$$ n = p_1^{2k_1} \dots p_k^{2k_k} = (p_1^{k_1} \dots p_k^{k_k})^2 $$
Thus, $n$ is a perfect square.

*** Example
Let $n = 3^2 \cdot 5^7$. Is $\sqrt{n}$ rational?
- The exponent of 5 is 7 (odd).
- Therefore, $n$ is not a perfect square, and $\sqrt{n}$ is irrational.

* Modular Arithmetic
** Introduction: Parity Arithmetic
Before defining general modular arithmetic, consider the arithmetic of Even ($e$) and Odd ($o$) numbers.

| $+$ | $e$ | $o$ |
|-----+-----+-----|
| $e$ | $e$ | $o$ |
| $o$ | $o$ | $e$ |

| $\times$ | $e$ | $o$ |
|----------+-----+-----|
| $e$      | $e$ | $e$ |
| $o$      | $e$ | $o$ |

This arithmetic corresponds to calculations modulo 2.

** Definition: Congruence Modulo $m$
Let $a, b, m \in \mathbb{Z}$ with $m > 1$.
We say $a$ is congruent to $b$ modulo $m$ if $m$ divides the difference $a - b$.

$$ a \equiv b \pmod{m} \iff m \mid (a - b) $$

*** Properties
Congruence is an equivalence relation. For $m > 1$:
1.  *Reflexive*: $a \equiv a \pmod{m}$ (since $m \mid 0$).
2.  *Symmetric*: $a \equiv b \pmod{m} \iff b \equiv a \pmod{m}$.
3.  *Transitive*: If $a \equiv b \pmod{m}$ and $b \equiv c \pmod{m}$, then $a \equiv c \pmod{m}$.

** Arithmetic Operations
If $a \equiv c \pmod{m}$ and $b \equiv d \pmod{m}$, then:
1.  $a + b \equiv c + d \pmod{m}$
2.  $a \cdot b \equiv c \cdot d \pmod{m}$

*** Example: Clock Arithmetic
Consider time modulo 12 ($m=12$).
- $9:00 + 5:00 = 14:00$.
- $14 \equiv 2 \pmod{12}$.
- So, $9 + 5 \equiv 2 \pmod{12}$.

** Proofs of Arithmetic Properties
*** Addition Property
*Claim*: $a \equiv c \pmod{m}$ and $b \equiv d \pmod{m} \implies a + b \equiv c + d \pmod{m}$.

*Proof*:
By definition:
$$ a - c = k_1 m $$
$$ b - d = k_2 m $$
Adding the equations:
$$ (a - c) + (b - d) = k_1 m + k_2 m $$
$$ (a + b) - (c + d) = m(k_1 + k_2) $$
Since $k_1 + k_2$ is an integer, $m \mid ((a+b) - (c+d))$.
Therefore, $a + b \equiv c + d \pmod{m}$.

*** Multiplication Property
*Claim*: $a \equiv c \pmod{m}$ and $b \equiv d \pmod{m} \implies ab \equiv cd \pmod{m}$.

*Proof*:
We write $a = c + mk_1$ and $b = d + mk_2$.
$$ ab = (c + mk_1)(d + mk_2) $$
$$ ab = cd + c(mk_2) + d(mk_1) + m^2 k_1 k_2 $$
$$ ab - cd = m(ck_2 + dk_1 + m k_1 k_2) $$
Since the RHS is a multiple of $m$, $m \mid (ab - cd)$.
Therefore, $ab \equiv cd \pmod{m}$.

*** Power Property
By induction on the multiplication property:
If $a \equiv b \pmod{m}$, then $a^n \equiv b^n \pmod{m}$ for $n \in \mathbb{N}$.

* Applications and Problems
** Problem 1: Calculating Large Powers
Calculate the remainder of $6^{30}$ when divided by 5, 7, and 13.

*** Case 1: Modulo 5
$$ 6 \equiv 1 \pmod{5} $$
$$ 6^{30} \equiv 1^{30} \equiv 1 \pmod{5} $$

*** Case 2: Modulo 7
$$ 6 \equiv -1 \pmod{7} $$
$$ 6^{30} \equiv (-1)^{30} \equiv 1 \pmod{7} $$

*** Case 3: Modulo 13
We compute powers of 6 modulo 13 until we find a pattern (usually 1 or -1):
- $6^1 \equiv 6$
- $6^2 = 36 = 2 \cdot 13 + 10 \equiv 10 \equiv -3 \pmod{13}$
- $6^3 \equiv 6 \cdot (-3) = -18 \equiv -5 \pmod{13}$ (Note: $-18 = -2 \cdot 13 + 8$, so also 8)
- $6^4 \equiv 6 \cdot (-5) = -30 \equiv -4 \equiv 9 \pmod{13}$
- $6^5 \equiv 6 \cdot (-4) = -24 \equiv 2 \pmod{13}$
- $6^6 \equiv 6 \cdot 2 = 12 \equiv -1 \pmod{13}$

Using $6^6 \equiv -1 \pmod{13}$:
$$ 6^{30} = (6^6)^5 \equiv (-1)^5 \equiv -1 \equiv 12 \pmod{13} $$

** Problem 2: Diophantine Equation
Solve $x^2 - 3y = 2$ in integers.

*** Solution
We analyze the equation modulo 3.
$$ x^2 - 3y = 2 \implies x^2 \equiv 2 \pmod{3} $$

We check all possible values of $x \pmod{3}$ to see if $x^2 \equiv 2$ is possible.
| $x \pmod{3}$ | $x^2 \pmod{3}$ |
|--------------+----------------|
| $0$          | $0$            |
| $1$          | $1$            |
| $2$          | $4 \equiv 1$   |
|              |                |

*Observation*: The square of an integer modulo 3 is always 0 or 1. It is never 2.
*Conclusion*: There are no integer solutions for $x^2 - 3y = 2$.
