* Limits and Sequences
** Examples of Limits
1.  *Squeeze Theorem Application*:
    $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{\sin n}{n} = 0 $$
    *Proof:* Since $-1 \le \sin n \le 1$, we have:
    $$ -\frac{1}{n} \le \frac{\sin n}{n} \le \frac{1}{n} $$
    As $n \to \infty$, both bounds go to 0, so the limit is 0.

2.  *Trigonometric Sequence*:
    $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} n \cdot \pi \cdot \sin(n\pi) = 0 $$
    *Reasoning:* Since $n$ is an integer, $\sin(n\pi) = 0$ for all $n$. The sequence is constantly 0.

** Quiz / Problems
1.  Calculate the limit:
    $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{2n + n^2}{2025! + n} = +\infty $$
    *Reasoning:* The numerator grows as $n^2$. The denominator is linear in $n$ ($2025!$ is a large constant). The term $n^2$ dominates.

2.  Standard Factorial Limit:
    $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n!}{n^n} = 0 $$
    *Proof sketch:*
    $$ 0 < \frac{1 \cdot 2 \cdot \dots \cdot n}{n \cdot n \cdot \dots \cdot n} = \frac{1}{n} \cdot \frac{2}{n} \cdot \dots \cdot \frac{n}{n} \le \frac{1}{n} $$
    By the Squeeze Theorem, the limit is 0.

* Construction of Real Numbers and Exponentials
** Real Numbers as Limits
Real numbers can be defined as limits of sequences of rational numbers.
- *Example:* $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.414235\dots$
  Consider the sequence $a_n \in \mathbb{Q}$:
  $$ 1, 1.4, 1.41, 1.414, 1.4142, \dots $$
  This sequence is increasing and bounded above. Therefore, it must converge.
  $$ \sqrt{2} = \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n $$

** Exponential Functions
We want to define $a^x$ for $a > 1$ and $x \in \mathbb{R}$.
We already know $a^x$ for $x \in \mathbb{Q}$ (rational exponents).

*** Proposition: Monotonicity on $\mathbb{Q}$
The function $f: \mathbb{Q} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $f(q) = a^q$ is increasing.
*Proof Sketch:*
Let $\frac{m_1}{n_1} < \frac{m_2}{n_2}$. This implies $m_1 n_2 < m_2 n_1$.
Since $a > 1$, raising to positive integers preserves order.
Eventually, this leads to showing $a^{q_1} < a^{q_2}$.

*** Definition for Real Exponents
To define $a^x$ for $x \in \mathbb{R}$, we approximate $x$ with a rational sequence $(q_n)$ such that $\lim q_n = x$.
- *Example:* $a^{\sqrt{2}}$.
  Using the sequence approximating $\sqrt{2}$:
  $$ a^{\sqrt{2}} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \{ a^1, a^{1.4}, a^{1.41}, a^{1.414}, \dots \} $$

Alternatively, using the supremum definition:
$$ a^x = \sup \{ a^q : q \le x \land q \in \mathbb{Q} \} $$

** Properties of the Exponential Function
Let $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ where $f(x) = a^x$.
1.  *Algebraic Properties:*
    $$ a^{x+y} = a^x \cdot a^y $$
    $$ (a^x)^y = a^{xy} $$
2.  *Range:*
    $$ f(\mathbb{R}) = (0, +\infty) $$
    *Proof:* For any $y \in (0, +\infty)$, there exists $x \in \mathbb{R}$ such that $a^x = y$ (existence of logarithms).
3.  *Case $0 < a < 1$:*
    Defined via reciprocal:
    $$ a^x = \frac{1}{(1/a)^x} $$
    Since $1/a > 1$, we use the previous definition.

* Recursive Sequences and Convergence
** Problem 1: Nested Radical
Consider the sequence defined by:
$$ x_1 = \sqrt{2}, \quad x_{n+1} = \sqrt{2 + x_n} $$
Find its limit.
$$ \sqrt{2 + \sqrt{2 + \sqrt{2 + \dots}}} $$

*Solution:*
1.  *Boundedness:* We show $x_n \le 2$ by induction.
    - Base case: $\sqrt{2} < 2$.
    - Step: If $x_n \le 2$, then $x_{n+1} = \sqrt{2 + x_n} \le \sqrt{2+2} = 2$.
2.  *Monotonicity:* $(x_n)$ is increasing.
    $$ x_{n+1} \ge x_n \iff \sqrt{2+x_n} \ge x_n \iff 2+x_n \ge x_n^2 \iff x_n^2 - x_n - 2 \le 0 $$
    The roots of $x^2 - x - 2$ are $2$ and $-1$. Since $0 < x_n < 2$, the inequality holds.
3.  *Limit Calculation:*
    Since the sequence is bounded and increasing, it converges to some limit $L$.
    $$ L = \lim_{n \to \infty} x_{n+1} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt{2 + x_n} = \sqrt{2+L} $$
    $$ L^2 = 2 + L \implies L^2 - L - 2 = 0 $$
    Solutions: $L_1 = 2$, $L_2 = -1$.
    Since limits of positive sequences must be non-negative, $L = 2$.

** Inequalities in Limits
*Question:* If $a_n > b_n$ for all $n$, and the sequences converge to $A$ and $B$, is it true that $A > B$?

*Answer:* **No.** The strict inequality is not necessarily preserved in the limit.
- *Counter-example:*
  Let $a_n = \frac{1}{n}$ and $b_n = 0$.
  Clearly $a_n > b_n$ for all $n$.
  However, $\lim a_n = 0$ and $\lim b_n = 0$, so $A = B$.

*Theorem:* If $a_n \ge b_n$ (or $a_n > b_n$) and limits exist, then $\lim a_n \ge \lim b_n$.

** Heron's Method (Newton's Method for Square Roots)
To approximate $\sqrt{2}$:
$$ x_1 = 2, \quad x_{n+1} = \frac{1}{2}\left( x_n + \frac{2}{x_n} \right) $$
This sequence converges to $\sqrt{2}$ very rapidly.
