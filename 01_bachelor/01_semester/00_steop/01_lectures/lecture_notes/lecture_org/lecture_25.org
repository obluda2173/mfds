* Cardinality of Sets
** Finite Sets
Let $A$ and $B$ be finite sets.
We denote $|A|$ as the number of elements in $A$.
Two sets have the same size if they have the same number of elements:
$$ |A| = |B| \iff \text{number of elements in } A = \text{number of elements in } B $$

** Infinite Sets and Equivalence
For infinite sets, we cannot simply count elements.
*** Definition
Two sets $A$ and $B$ are said to have equal size (or *cardinality*), or are *equivalent*, if there exists a bijection from $A$ to $B$.
- *Notation:* $A \sim B$ or $|A| = |B|$.

*** Theorem: Equivalence Relation
The relation $\sim$ is an equivalence relation.
*Proof:*
1.  *Reflexive:* $A \sim A$.
    The identity map $id_A: A \to A$ is a bijection.
2.  *Symmetric:* If $A \sim B$, then $B \sim A$.
    If $f: A \to B$ is a bijection, then the inverse function $f^{-1}: B \to A$ exists and is also a bijection.
3.  *Transitive:* If $A \sim B$ and $B \sim C$, then $A \sim C$.
    Let $f: A \to B$ and $g: B \to C$ be bijections. The composition $g \circ f: A \to C$ is a bijection.

* Countable Sets
** Definition
A set $A$ is called *countable* (or denumerable) if $A \sim \mathbb{N}$ (the set of natural numbers).
- *Notation:* The cardinality of $\mathbb{N}$ is denoted by $\aleph_0$ (aleph-null).

*** Terminology Note
There is sometimes ambiguity in terminology:
1.  *Denumerable / Infinitely Countable:* Specifically $A \sim \mathbb{N}$.
2.  *Countable:* Either finite or denumerable.
In this context, we often focus on infinite countable sets.

** Examples
1.  *Hilbert's Hotel:* An infinite hotel with rooms $1, 2, 3, \dots$ is full. If a new guest arrives, move guest in room $n$ to $n+1$. Room 1 becomes free. The set $\mathbb{N} \cup \{x\} \sim \mathbb{N}$.
2.  *Even Natural Numbers:* Let $E = \{2n : n \in \mathbb{N}\} = \{2, 4, 6, \dots\}$.
    Define $f: \mathbb{N} \to E$ by $f(n) = 2n$. This is a bijection, so $E \sim \mathbb{N}$.
3.  *Integers ($\mathbb{Z}$):* $\mathbb{Z} = \{0, 1, -1, 2, -2, \dots\}$.
    We can list them: $0 \to 1, 1 \to 2, -1 \to 3, 2 \to 4, -2 \to 5, \dots$
    Thus $\mathbb{Z} \sim \mathbb{N}$.

** Theorems on Countability
*** Theorem: Infinite Subsets
Every infinite subset of $\mathbb{N}$ is countable.
*Proof Sketch:*
Let $A \subset \mathbb{N}$ be infinite.
We can list elements of $A$ in increasing order:
- $a_1 = \min A$
- $a_2 = \min (A \setminus \{a_1\})$
- $a_3 = \min (A \setminus \{a_1, a_2\})$
- ...
This defines a bijection $f: \mathbb{N} \to A$ where $f(i) = a_i$.

*** Theorem: Rationals ($\mathbb{Q}$)
The set of rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$ is countable ($|\mathbb{Q}| = \aleph_0$).
*Proof Strategy:*
1.  Show positive rationals $\mathbb{Q}_{>0}$ are countable.
    Arrange fractions $p/q$ in a 2D grid:
    $$
    \begin{matrix}
    1/1 & 1/2 & 1/3 & 1/4 & \dots \\
    2/1 & 2/2 & 2/3 & 2/4 & \dots \\
    3/1 & 3/2 & 3/3 & 3/4 & \dots \\
    4/1 & 4/2 & 4/3 & 4/4 & \dots \\
    \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots
    \end{matrix}
    $$
    Traverse diagonals (snake-like path): $\frac{1}{1}, \frac{1}{2}, \frac{2}{1}, \frac{3}{1}, \frac{2}{2}, \frac{1}{3}, \dots$.
    Skip fractions that are not in simplest form (repetitions like $2/2$).
    This creates a listing $q_1, q_2, q_3, \dots$.
2.  Extend to all rationals $\mathbb{Q}$ by interleaving:
    $\mathbb{Q} = \{0, q_1, -q_1, q_2, -q_2, \dots\}$.

*** Theorem: Union of Countable Sets
The union of countably many countable sets is countable.
*Proof Sketch:*
Let $A_1, A_2, A_3, \dots$ be countable sets.
Let $A_n = \{a_{n1}, a_{n2}, a_{n3}, \dots\}$.
We can arrange all elements $a_{nm}$ in a grid and traverse diagonals (similar to the proof for $\mathbb{Q}$).
$$ \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n = \{a_{11}, a_{21}, a_{12}, a_{13}, a_{22}, a_{31}, \dots\} $$

* Uncountable Sets
** Comparing Cardinalities
- We say $|A| \le |B|$ if there exists an *injection* from $A$ to $B$.
- We say $|A| < |B|$ if $|A| \le |B|$ and $|A| \ne |B|$ (i.e., no bijection exists).

** Cantor's Theorem: Uncountability of (0,1)
The open interval $(0,1)$ is uncountable.
*Proof (Cantor's Diagonal Argument):*
Suppose for contradiction that $A = (0,1)$ is countable.
Then we can list all real numbers in $(0,1)$ as a sequence $r_1, r_2, r_3, \dots$.
We use the unique decimal representation (avoiding infinite strings of 9s).

$$
\begin{aligned}
r_1 &= 0.a_{11} a_{12} a_{13} \dots \\
r_2 &= 0.a_{21} a_{22} a_{23} \dots \\
r_3 &= 0.a_{31} a_{32} a_{33} \dots \\
&\vdots
\end{aligned}
$$

We construct a new number $b = 0.b_1 b_2 b_3 \dots \in (0,1)$ that is not in the list.
Define the digit $b_n$ as:
$$
b_n = \begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } a_{nn} \ne 1 \\
2 & \text{if } a_{nn} = 1
\end{cases}
$$
(Note: Using 1 and 2 ensures the decimal expansion does not end in infinite 9s, avoiding representation ambiguity).

For every $n$, the number $b$ differs from $r_n$ in the $n$-th decimal place ($b_n \ne a_{nn}$).
Therefore, $b \ne r_n$ for any $n \in \mathbb{N}$.
This contradicts the assumption that the list contains all numbers in $(0,1)$.
Thus, $(0,1)$ is uncountable.

** Theorem: Supersets of Uncountable Sets
Let $A \subset B$. If $A$ is uncountable, then $B$ is uncountable.
*Proof:*
Assume $B$ is countable.
Since any subset of a countable set is countable, $A$ must be countable.
This contradicts the hypothesis that $A$ is uncountable.
Therefore, $B$ must be uncountable.
