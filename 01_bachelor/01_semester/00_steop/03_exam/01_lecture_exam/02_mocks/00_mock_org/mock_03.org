*** C1: Factorial Inequality Induction
*Goal:* Show $\sum_{i=1}^n i(i!) = (n+1)! - 1$.
1.  **Base Case ($n=1$):** $1(1!) = 1$. RHS: $2! - 1 = 1$. Valid.
2.  **Step:** Assume $\sum_{i=1}^k i(i!) = (k+1)! - 1$.
    Add $(k+1)(k+1)!$ to both sides.
    RHS becomes: $((k+1)! - 1) + (k+1)(k+1)!$
    Factor out $(k+1)!$: $(k+1)![1 + (k+1)] - 1$
    $= (k+1)!(k+2) - 1 = (k+2)! - 1$.
3.  **Conclusion:** True for all $n$.

*** C2: Boolean Rings
Given ring where $a^2 = a$ for all $a$ (Boolean Ring).
1.  Consider $(a+b)^2 = a+b$.
    $a^2 + ab + ba + b^2 = a + b$.
    $a + ab + ba + b = a + b \implies ab + ba = 0 \implies ab = -ba$.
2.  Consider $2a$. Since $(2a)^2 = 2a$:
    $4a^2 = 2a \implies 4a = 2a \implies 2a = 0$.
    Thus $a = -a$.
3.  Substitute into (1): $ab = -ba = ba$. The ring is commutative.

*** C3: Countability of Circles
A circle is determined by center $(x,y)$ and radius $r$. The set of parameters is $(x, y, r) \in \mathbb{Q}^3$.
Since $\mathbb{Q}$ is countable, the Cartesian product $\mathbb{Q} \times \mathbb{Q} \times \mathbb{Q}$ is countable.
Therefore, the set of circles with rational centers and radii is countable.

*** C4: Epsilon-Delta Limit Proof
Limit to prove: $\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{3n^2+1}{2n^2-n} = \frac{3}{2}$.
\[ \left| \frac{3n^2+1}{2n^2-n} - \frac{3}{2} \right| = \left| \frac{2(3n^2+1) - 3(2n^2-n)}{2(2n^2-n)} \right| \]
\[ = \left| \frac{6n^2+2 - 6n^2+3n}{4n^2-2n} \right| = \left| \frac{3n+2}{4n^2-2n} \right| \]
*Bounding:* For large $n$, $3n+2 < 4n$ and $4n^2-2n > 2n^2$ (implies $2n^2 > 2n \to n>1$).
\[ \text{Expression} < \frac{4n}{2n^2} = \frac{2}{n} \]
To ensure $\frac{2}{n} < \epsilon$, choose $N > \frac{2}{\epsilon}$.

*** C7: Injectivity of Sets Function
Claim: $f$ is injective iff $f(A \cap B) = f(A) \cap f(B)$.
*Proof ($\Leftarrow$):*
Assume the intersection property holds. Let $A=\{x\}$ and $B=\{y\}$.
$f(\{x\} \cap \{y\}) = f(\emptyset) = \emptyset$.
By hypothesis: $f(\{x\}) \cap f(\{y\}) = \{f(x)\} \cap \{f(y)\}$.
If $f(x) = f(y)$, then $\{f(x)\} \cap \{f(y)\} = \{f(x)\} \neq \emptyset$.
This contradicts the LHS being $\emptyset$ (unless $x=y$).
Thus $x \neq y \implies f(x) \neq f(y)$.
