* Exam Syllabus and Strategy Coverage
The course covers a vast breadth of discrete and continuous mathematics. Based on the lecture depth and problem set coverage, the exam will be weighted as follows:

** Topic Weight Breakdown
*** High Weight (Core Skills)
- *Proof Techniques:* Mathematical Induction (Weak/Strong) and Proof by Contradiction appear repeatedly.
- *Functions & Relations:* Injectivity/Surjectivity/Bijectivity and Equivalence Relations/Partial Orders are fundamental.
- *Number Theory:* Modular arithmetic, GCD/Euclidean algorithm, and Diophantine equations.
- *Sequences & Limits:* $\epsilon-N$ definitions and convergence tests are heavily emphasized in the later lectures.

*** Medium Weight
- *Cardinality:* Countable vs. Uncountable sets (Cantor's diagonalization).
- *Combinatorics:* Permutations, combinations, and the Inclusion-Exclusion Principle.
- *Polynomials & Complex Numbers:* Roots, factorization, and polar forms.

*** Lower Weight (but present)
- *Vectors & Geometry:* Dot products, lines, and planes.
- *Algebraic Structures:* Rings and Fields definitions.

** Time Calibration (120 Minutes)
*** Section A (20 MCQs)
- *Target:* 30 minutes (1.5 mins/question).
- *Focus:* Definitions, simple counter-examples, and immediate calculations.

*** Section B (10 Open Problems)
- *Target:* 90 minutes (9 mins/question).
- *Constraint:* Standard textbook proofs often take 15+ minutes.
- *Strategy:* The Open Problems will be specific and computational where possible (e.g., "Find the inverse" or "Calculate the limit") or standard, concise proofs (e.g., "Prove by induction that..."). Open-ended "Essay" style proofs for harder topics like construction of real numbers will be avoided to ensure the exam is finishable.

** Difficulty Distribution Strategy
- *Easy (25%):* Basic set operations, computing limits of rational functions, modular arithmetic calculations.
- *Medium (50%):* Standard induction proofs, finding inverse functions, solving combinatorial problems, complex number powers (De Moivre).
- *Hard (25%):* $\epsilon-N$ limit proofs, proofs involving cardinality of specific sets, and rigorous proofs regarding equivalence relations or polynomial properties.
