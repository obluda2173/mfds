function forward_substitution(L, b)
    n = size(L, 1)
    y = zeros(Float64, n)

    for i in 1:n
        sum = b[i]

        for j in 1:(i-1)
            sum -= L[i, j] * y[j]
        end

        y[i] = sum / L[i, i]
    end
    return y
end

function backward_substitution(U, y)
    n = size(U, 1)
    x = zeros(Float64, n)

    for i in n:-1:1
        sum = y[i]

        for j in (i+1):n
            sum -= U[i, j] * x[j]
        end
        x[i] = sum / U[i, i]
    end
    return x
end

      3]

Let $V$ be a vector space over a field $\mathbb{F}$. Let $L = \left\{ v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_m \right\}$ be linearly independent and $S = \left\{ w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_{n} \right\}$ be a spanning set over $V$. Then $m \le n$, and there exists $S' \subseteq S$ with $|S'| = n - m$ such that $S' \cup L$ spans $V$.

- *Custom Exam Style Proof.*

  - *Theorem.*

    Let $L = \left\{ v_1, \ldots, v_m \right\}$ be linearly independent and $S = \left\{ w_1, \ldots, w_n \right\}$ span $V$. Then $m \leq n$, and $\exists S' \subseteq S$ with $|S'| = n - m$ such that $L \cup S'$ spans $V$.

  - *Proof.*

    $m = 0$: Trivial, $L \cup S = S$ spans $V$ and $0 \leq n$.

    Assume case holds for $m-1$. Applied to $\left\{ v_1, \ldots, v_{m-1} \right\}$ and $S$, we have obtain $S_{m-1} \subseteq S$ with $|S_{m-1}| = n - (m - 1)$ such that $\left\{ v_1, \ldots, v_{m-1} \right\} \cup S_{m-1}$ spans $V$.

*Formal Proof.*

- *Lemma (Exchange Lemma).*

  Let $V$ be a $\mathbb{F}$ - vector space, $A \subseteq V$ a subset, and $v \in \langle A \rangle$ an arbitrary element.

  ~here we just defined that $V$ is our vector space, $A$ is a subset of $V$ (because of notation $\langle A \rangle$), and let $v$ be some vector that lies in the span of $A$~

  Let $a_0 \in A$ be an element such that there is a representation of $v$ as a linear combination of elements of $A$ in which $a_0$ has a coefficient $\lambda_0 \neq 0$.

  ~here we are basically linking $a_0$ to arbitrary element $v$, by saying that $v$ can be represented as a linear combination, where $a_0$ takes place (as $\lambda_0 \neq 0$)~

  Then the set $A' := (A \setminus \left\{ a_0 \right\}) \cup \left\{ v \right\}$, which is obtained by replacing the vector $a_0$ in $A$ by $v$, satisfies $\langle A' \rangle = \langle A \rangle$.

  ~here we are constructing a new set $A'$ which is a set $A$ with replaced $v$ over $a_0$, then saying that $\langle A' \rangle = \langle A \rangle$ (meaning they have a same span, which implies $A'$ being a spanning set over $V$)~

- *Lemma Proof (Exchange Lemma).*

  By assumption we have $v \in \langle A \rangle$, hence $A' \subseteq \langle A \rangle$, hence $\langle A' \rangle \subseteq \langle A \rangle$.

  ~here as I can see we are starting to prove from both sides (e.g. $\langle A' \rangle \subseteq \langle A \rangle$ and $\langle A \rangle \subseteq \langle A' \rangle$ implies $\langle A \rangle = \langle A' \rangle$). since $v$ was assumed to be a vector that lies in the span of $A$, $A'$ must be a subset of span $A$~

  Conversely, by assumption there exists elements $a_1, \ldots, a_n \in A \setminus \left\{ a_0 \right\}$ and scalars $\lambda_0, \ldots, \lambda_n$ with $\lambda_0 \neq 0$ such that $v = \lambda_0 a_0 + \sum_{j=1}^{n} \lambda_j a_j$.

  ~here we are just saying that since by assumption $v$ is a linear combination of span $A$, we can write $v$ in the form of $v = \lambda_0 a_0 + \sum_{j=1}^{n} \lambda_j a_j$~

  Hence, $a_0 = \lambda_0^{-1} \left( v - \sum_{j=1}^n \lambda_j a_j \right) \in \langle A' \rangle$.

  ~here we have just rewrote the previous equiation for $a_0$, since the RHS is fully constructed of $v$ and $a_j$, remembering that $A' := (A \setminus \left\{ a_0 \right\}) \cup \left\{ v \right\}$ we conclude that $a_0 \in \langle A' \rangle$~

  Since obviously $A \setminus \left\{ a_0 \right\} \subseteq \langle A' \rangle$ holds, we obtain $A \subseteq \langle A' \rangle$ and therefore $\langle A \rangle \subseteq \langle A' \rangle$.

  ~here we stated obvious statement, and by the work done before (that $a_0 \in \langle A' \rangle$) stated $\langle A \rangle \subseteq \langle A' \rangle$, hence the two sided proof finishes~

- *Theorem (Steinitz Exchange Theorem).*

  Let $V$ be a finitely generated $\mathbb{F}$ vector space, and let $\left\{ v_1, \ldots, v_n \right\}$ be a spanning set of $V$. Then:

  a. If $A \subseteq V$ is a linearly independent subset, then $A$ is finite and has at most $n$ elements. If $A = \left\{ a_1, \ldots, a_k \right\}$ with $k < n$, then one can renumber the elements $v_i$, in such a way $\left\{ a_1, \ldots, a_k, v_{k+1}, \ldots v_n \right\}$ is a spanning set of $V$.

  b. If $A \subseteq V$ is an arbitrary spanning set, then there exists a finite subset $A' \subseteq A$ with at most $n$ elements that is also a spanning set of $V$.

Let $T : V \rightarrow W$ be a linear map between vector spaces $V$ and $W$ over a field $\mathbb{F}$.

- *Kernel of $T$:*

  $$ker(T) = \left\{ v \in V : T(v) = 0_{W} \right\}$$

  A kernel is the preimage of a zero vector in $W$. It is a subspace of $V$.

- *Image of $T$:*

  $im(T) = \left\{ T(v) : v \in V \right\} = T(V)$

  The image is the set of all outputs of $T$. It is a subspace of $W$.

- *Rank of $T$:*

  $$rank(T) = dim(im(T))$$

  Rank is the dimension of the image space.

*Theorem (Dimension Theorem for Linear Maps).*

$$dim(V) = dim(im(f)) + dim(ker(f))$$

/define basis of a kernel/

Since the vectors $b_i$ are linearly independent, /Corollary III.1.48(ii)/ shows they can be extended to a basis $\left\{ b_1, \ldots, b_n \right\}$ of the vector space $V$.

/using Corollary to state the basis set can be extended (with linear independent vectors) to span the whole vector space $V$/

We claim that the vectors $f(b_{k+1}), \ldots, f(b_{n}) \in W$ form a basis of the subspace $im(f) \in W$, which of course proves the theorem.

/outlining the whole point of the proof, to demonstrate $\left\{ f(b_{k+1}), \ldots, f(b_n) \right\}$ (extended part of initial kernel basis) is basis of image under $f$/

/using proposition to state $\left\{ f(b_1), \ldots, f(b_n) \right\}$ spans the $im(f)$, foundation/

Assume $\exists \, \lambda_{k+1}, \ldots, \lambda_n \in \mathbb{F}$ (scalars) such that $\lambda_{k+1}f(b_{k+1}) + \ldots + \lambda_nf(b_n) = 0$.

Hence, (rewriting) $f(\lambda_{k+1}b_{k+1} + \ldots + \lambda_nb_n) = 0$.

Thus, $\lambda_{k+1}b_{k+1} + \ldots + \lambda_nb_n \in ker(f)$ (as their sum is zero).

Since $\left\{ b_1, \ldots, b_k \right\}$ are basis, there must exist scalars $\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_k \in \mathbb{F}$ such that $\lambda_{k+1}b_{k+1} + \ldots + \lambda_nb_n = \mu_1b_1 + \ldots + \mu_kb_k$

**** Exam Style

- *Theorem:*

  For a finite dimensional vector space $V$ and a linear map $f : V \rightarrow W$, $dim(V) = dim(ker(f)) + dim(im(f))$.

- *Proof:*

  Let $dim(V) = n$ and $dim(ker(f)) = k$.

  1. Basis Extension

     Choose a basis $\left\{ b_1, \ldots, b_k \right\}$ for $ker(f)$. Extend it to basis $\left\{ b_1, \ldots, b_n \right\}$ for $V$.

  2. Claim

     The set $S = \left\{ f(b_{k+1}), \ldots, f(b_n) \right\}$ is basis for $im(f)$.

  3. Proof of Span

     Let $y \in im(f)$, then $\exists x \in V : y = f(x)$. Since $\left\{ b_1, \dots, b_n \right\}$ is a basis for $V$, we can write $x = \sum_{i = 1}^n c_i b_i$. By linearity, and because $b_1, \ldots, b_k \in ker(f)$:

     $$y = f\left( \sum_{i=1}^{n}c_i b_i \right) = \sum_{i=1}^{k}c_i f(b_i) + \sum_{i=k+1}^n c_i f(b_i) = \sum_{i=k+1}^n c_i f(b_i)$$

     Thus, the set $S$ spans $im(f)$.

  4. Proof of Linear Independence

     Assume $\sum_{i=k+1}^n c_i f(b_i) = 0$. By linearity, $f(\sum_{i=k+1}^n c_i b_i) = 0$, meaning the vector $v = \sum_{i=k+1}^n c_i b_i$ is in $ker(f)$. Hence,

     Therefore, $dim(V) = dim(ker(f)) + dim(im(f))$.

*** Solution

**** Annotated Rewriting

*Proposition (Sum of subspaces)*

Let $W$ and $\hat{W}$ be subspaces of a $\mathbb{F}$ vector space $V$. Then:

1. An element $v \in V$ lies in $W + \hat{W}$ iff there exists elements $w \in W$ and $\hat{w} \in \hat{W} : v = w + \hat{w}$.

2. If $W$ and $\hat{W}$ are finite dimensional, then $W + \hat{W}$ is also finite dimensional and

   $$dim(W + \hat{W}) = dim(W) + dim(\hat{W}) - dim(W \cap \hat{W})$$

*Proof.*

- Setup

  Let $U, W \in V$ be finite dimensional subspaces.

  Let $I := U \cap W$.

  Choose $B_I$ be basis of $I$.

- Extend Basis

  Extend $B_I$ to be basis of $U$:

  $$B_U = B_I \cup B_1$$

  Extend $B_I$ to be basis of $W$:

  $$B_W = B_I \cup B_2$$

  Observe:

  $$dim(U) = |B_U| = |B_I| + |B_1|$$

  $$dim(W) = |B_W| = |B_I| + |B_2|$$

- Claim 1: Span

  Claim, $B_I \cup B_1 \cup B_2$ spans $U + W$.

  Let $v \in U + W$, hence, $v = u + w : u \in U, w \in W$. $u$ can be written as a linear combination of basis $B_U = B_I + B_1$, and $w$ as a linear combination of $B_W = B_I + B_2$, hence, $u + w$ can be represented as a linear combination of $B_I \cup B_1 \cup B_2$. Hence, $B_I \cup B_1 \cup B_2$ spans $U + W$.

- Claim 2: Linear Independence

  Claim, $B_I \cup B_1 \cup B_2$ is linearly independent.

  Assume

  $$\sum_{b \in B_I} \alpha_b b + \sum_{b \in B_1} \beta_b b + \sum_{b \in B_2} \gamma_b b = 0$$

  Observe that

  $$\left( \sum_{b \in B_I} \alpha_b b + \sum_{b \in B_1} \beta_b b \right) + \left( \sum_{b \in b_2} \gamma_b b \right) = 0$$

  where first parenthesis is a vector in $U$ (as constructed with $B_I$ and $B_1$), while second paranthesis is a vector in $W$ (as constructed with $B_2$).

  $$\left( \sum_{b \in B_I} \alpha_b b + \sum_{b \in B_1} \beta_b b \right) = - \left( \sum_{b \in b_2} \gamma_b b \right)$$

  Hence, all scalars $\beta$ must be equal to zero.

  As $\beta$'s equal to zero, the original equality simplifies to:

  $$\sum_{b \in B_I} \alpha_b b + \sum_{b \in B_2} \gamma_b b = 0$$

  Where the left side is a linear combination of $W$ basis. This forces all $\gamma$ scalars to be equal to zero, due to linear independence of the basis.

  Hence, all coefficients are zero. Thus, the original vector combination of basis $B_I, B_1, B_2$ is linear independent.

- Conclusion

  As $B_I \cup B_1 \cup B_2$ spans $U + W$, and is linearly independent, it is a basis of $U + W$.

  Therefore,

  $$dim(U + W) = dim(U) + dim(W) - dim(U \cap W)$$

**** (1)

*Definition (Field).* A field $\mathbb{F}$ is a set equipped with additive and multiplicative operations ($+, \times$, respectively), such that it fullfils the following axioms:

Let $a, b, c \in \mathbb{F}$.

1. Additive associativity

   $(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)$

2. Additive commutativity

   $a + b = b + a$

3. Additive identity

   $\exists 0 \in \mathbb{F} : \forall a \in \mathbb{F}, a + 0 = a$

4. Additive inverse

   $\forall a \in \mathbb{F}, \exists (-a) : a + (-a) = 0$

5. Multiplicative associativity

   $(a \times b) \times c = a \times (b \times c)$

6. Multiplicative commutativity

   $a \times b = b \times a$

7. Multiplicative identity

   $\exists 1 \in \mathbb{F} : 1 \neq 0, \forall a \in \mathbb{F}, a \times 1 = a$

8. Multiplicative inverse

   $\forall a \in \mathbb{F} : a \neq 0, \exists a^{-1} : a \times a^{-1} = 1$

9. Distributivity

   $a \times (b + c) = (a \times b) + (a \times c)$
