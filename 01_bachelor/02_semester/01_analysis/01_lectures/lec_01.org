* Definition Compactness

A metric space $X$ is called compact if every sequence $(x_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}} \in X$ has a convergence subsequence.

* Lemma: Every compact metric space is complete

Let \((X,d)\) be compact and let \((x_n)\) be a Cauchy sequence in \(X\). Compactness implies sequential compactness, so \((x_n)\) has a convergent subsequence \((x_{n_k})\) with \(x_{n_k}\to x\in X\).

Fix \(\varepsilon>0\). Since \((x_n)\) is Cauchy, there exists \(N\in\mathbb{N}\) such that
\[
d(x_n,x_m)<\frac{\varepsilon}{2}\quad\text{for all }n,m\ge N.
\]
Choose \(k\) with \(n_k\ge N\) and \(d(x_{n_k},x)<\varepsilon/2\) (possible because \(x_{n_k}\to x\)). Then for every \(n\ge N\),
\[
d(x_n,x)\le d(x_n,x_{n_k})+d(x_{n_k},x)<\frac{\varepsilon}{2}+\frac{\varepsilon}{2}=\varepsilon.
\]
Thus \(x_n\to x\), so every Cauchy sequence in \(X\) converges. Hence \(X\) is complete.
