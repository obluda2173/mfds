* Definition 10.1.1:  Metric space

Given a set $X \neq \emptyset$, we say that a mapping $d_X : X \times X \rightarrow \left[ 0, \infty \right)$ is a metric if the following three properties are satisfied:

1. symmetry: for all $x, y \in X$,

   $$d_{X}(x, y) = d_X(y, x)$$

2. triangle inequality: for all $x, y, z \in X$,

   $d_X(x,z) \le d_X(x,y) + d_X(y,z)$

3. definiteness: for all $x, y \in X$.

   $$d_X(x, y) = 0 \iff x = y$$

If $d_X$ is a metric on $X$, then $(X, d_{X})$ is called a metric space.

* Definition 10.2.1:  Cauchy sequence

Let $(X, d_X)$ be a metric space. A sequence $(a_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}} \subseteq X$ is called Cauchy sequence if

$$\forall \epsilon>0, \exists N \in \mathbb{N} : \forall n, m \ge N, d_X(a_n, a_m) \le \epsilon$$

* Definition 10.2.5:  Completeness

A metric space $X$ is called complete if every Cauchy sequence in $X$ is convergent in $X$.

* Definition 10.3.1:  Compactness

A metric space $X$ is called compact if every sequence $(x_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}} \in X$ has a convergent subsequence $(x_{n_j})_{j \in \mathbb{N}}$ (with limit in $X$).

/Metric space $X$ is compact iff no matter how you pick an infinite sequence of points in $X$, you can always extract subsequence that converges to some point still inside $X$./

** Q&A

- Q_01

  About Sequence and Subsequence. Take for example the interval $(0,1)$, do we consider it as a sequence and then argue that its subsequence $\frac{1}{n}$ approaches $0$, which is outside the interval, making it not compact? Or it is just an interval and a subsequence that I gave, is actually a sequence that still proves its non compactness? Or maybe there is a third option of a sequence being its own subsequence?

- A_01

  The interval $(0, 1)$ is a set (the space), not a sequence. The sequence $\frac{1}{n}$ is a sequence in that set, and to disprove compactness you show that this sequence has no subsequence converging to a limit inside $(0, 1)$ — which is true because the whole sequence converges to $0$, forcing every subsequence to converge to $0$ as well, and $0 \notin (0, 1)$. Your second interpretation was essentially right: you exhibit a sequence in the space and show it defeats the compactness condition.

- Q_02

  Can a sequence in a compact space "escape" to a limit outside the space, while compactness only guarantees some subsequence stays inside?

- A_02

  No. Compactness actually prevents this entirely, for two reasons working together.

  First, a compact metric space is always closed, meaning if a sequence of points in $X$ converges at all, the limi tmust already belong to $X$ - there is nowhere to escape to.

  Second, if the full sequence converges to some limit $L$, then every subsequence converges to that same $L$, so there is no way for the sequence and a subsequence to "disagree" on where they end up.

  The only interesting case compactness handles is when the full sequence does not converge at all. Imagine a sequence that oscilates from $0$ to $1$, its subsequence, consisting only of $1$'s is convergent.

* Lemma      10.3.2:  Compactness implies completeness

Every compact metric space is complete.

*Proof.*

Let $(x_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}} \in X$ be a Cauchy sequence. Since $X$ is compact, it has a convergent subsequence $x_j \rightarrow x \in X$ for $j \rightarrow \infty$. Given $\epsilon > 0$, there are $N_1, N_2 \in \mathbb{N}$ such that

\begin{align*}
d_X(x_n, x_m) &\le \frac{\epsilon}{2}, & \forall n, m &\ge N_1 \\
d_X(x_{n_j}, x) &\le \frac{\epsilon}{2}, & \forall j &\ge N_2 \\
\end{align*}

Let $N := max(N_1, N_2)$. Then for all $n \ge N$ we have $n_N \ge N_1$ and

$$d_X(x_n,x) \le d_X(x_n, x_{n_N}) + d_X(x_{n_N}, x) \le \frac{\epsilon}{2} + \frac{\epsilon}{2} = \epsilon$$

Thus, $x_n \rightarrow x$.

* Definition 10.3.3:  Open and closed subsets

Let $X$ be a metric space and $x \in X$.

1. We define a ball $B_{\epsilon}(x)$ of radius $\epsilon \ge 0$ and its interior $\mathring{B}(x)$ by

   \begin{align*}
   B_{\epsilon}(x) := \left\{ y \in X : d_X(x, y) \le \epsilon \right\} \\
   \mathring{B}_{\epsilon}(x) := \left\{ y \in X : d_X(x, y) < \epsilon \right\} \\
   \end{align*}

2. We say a set $S \subseteq X$ is open if

   $$\forall x \in S \quad \exists \epsilon > 0 : B_{\epsilon}(x) \subseteq S$$

3. We say that $S \subseteq X$ is closed if its complement $X \setminus S$ is open

4. We call $S \subseteq X$ a neighborhood of $x$ if there is $\epsilon > 0$ such that $B_{\epsilon}(x) \subseteq S$

** Q&A

- Q_01

  But why does the open set definition uses closed ball and not an open ball?

- A_01

  The two formulations define exactly the same concept of openness because fitting a closed ball of radius $\epsilon$ inside a set implies you can fit an open ball of the same radius (it's smaller), and fitting an open ball of radius $\epsilon$ implies you can fit a closed ball of radius $\frac{\epsilon}{2}$ inside it. Your lecturer chose the closed ball version, but it's purely a matter of convention — the resulting notion of "open set" is identical either way.

* Lemma      10.3.6:  Sequential closure

Let $X$ be a metric space and $S \subseteq X$ a nonempty subset. Then $S$ is closed iff all sequences $(x_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}} \subseteq S$ with $x_n \rightarrow x \in X$ satisfy $x \in S$.

/In other words, $S$ is closed iff it contains all limits of its converging sequences./

*Proof.*

- ($\Rightarrow$) Let $S$ be closed. Proof by contradiction, so assume that $(x_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}} \subseteq S$ and $x_n \rightarrow x \in X \setminus S$. Since $X \setminus S$ is open, there is $\epsilon > 0$ such that $B_{\epsilon}(x) \subseteq X \setminus S$. By convergence, there is $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that

  $$d_{X}(x_n, x) < \epsilon \quad \forall n \ge N$$

  In particular, $x_N \in B_{\epsilon}(x) \subseteq X \setminus S$, which contradicts $(x_n)_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \subseteq S$.

  /so basically, we are using convergence to find a $x_N \in B_{\epsilon}(x) \subseteq X \setminus S$, which will contradict the assumption that $(x_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}} \subseteq S$/

- ($\Leftarrow$) We prove the contrapositive. Suppose $S$ is not closed. Then $X \setminus S$ is not open, so there exists $x \in X \setminus S$ such that for all $\epsilon > 0$, the ball $B_{\epsilon}(x)$ is not contained in $X \setminus S$. That is, for every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists a point in $B_{\epsilon}(x) \cap S$. Choosing $\epsilon = \frac{1}{n}$ for each $n \ge 1$ yields a point $y_n \in B_{1/n}(x) \cap S$. By construction, $d_{X}(y_n, x) < \frac{1}{n}$ for all $n \ge 1$, so $y_n \rightarrow x$. Since each $y_n \in S$ but $x \in X \setminus S$, we have found a sequence in $S$ converging to a limit outside $S$.

  /here we are proving by contrapositive, choosing some point $x \in X \setminus S : \forall \epsilon > 0, B_{\epsilon}(x) \not\subseteq X \setminus S$ (equivalently, $B_{\epsilon}(x) \cap S \neq \emptyset$), then taking balls of radius $1/n$ gives a sequence that is inside of $S$ that converges to $x \in X \setminus S$, hence it is not sequentially closed./


** Q&A

- Q_01

  But isn't it the same as compactness?

- A_01

  Closedness says "limits that exist stay inside," while compactness says "convergent subsequences are guaranteed to exist and their limits stay inside." Closedness is the weaker condition — it doesn't prevent sequences from diverging to infinity, it only controls where limits land when convergence does occur. Compactness implies closedness, but not the other way around; you additionally need boundedness (in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$) to upgrade closedness to compactness.

* Lemma      10.3.7:  Compactness implies boundedness and closure

Let $X$ be a metric space. If $S \subseteq X$ is compact, then $S$ is bounded and closed.

*Proof.*

Assume that $S$ is unbounded. Fix $x_0 \in X$. Then for every $n \in \mathbb{N}$ there exists $x_n \in S$ such that

$$d(x_n, x_0) \ge n.$$

Thus we obtain a sequence $(x_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}} \subseteq S$.

Due to compactness, there exists a subsequence $(x_{n_k})_{k\in\mathbb{N}}$ that converges to some $x \in S$.

Every convergent sequence in a metric space is bounded by /Lemma 10.1.11/, hence there exists $M > 0$ such that

$$d(x_{n_k}, x_0) \le M \quad \forall k \in \mathbb{N}.$$

On the other hand, by construction,

$$d(x_{n_k}, x_0) \ge n_k \ge k,$$

which is unbounded as $k \rightarrow \infty$. This is a contradiction. Therefore, $S$ must be bounded.

To verify that $S$ is closed, let $(x_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}} \subseteq S$ satisfy $x_n \rightarrow x \in X$. We aim to show that $x$ is contained in $S$, so that /Lemma 10.3.6/ then yields that $S$ is closed. Since $S$ is compact, there is a subsequence $(x_{n_j})_{j\in\mathbb{N}}$ that converges to some $y \in S$. Since $(x_{n_j})_{j\in\mathbb{N}}$ is a subsequence of a sequence converging to $x$, it must also converge to $x$. By uniqueness of limits, $x = y \in S$.

* Theorem    10.3.11: Heine-Borel theorem in $\mathbb{R}$

Suppose that $\mathbb{R}$ is equipped with the standard metric. A set $K \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is compact if and only if it is closed and bounded.

*Proof.*

- ($\Rightarrow$) Suppose that $K \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is compact but unbounded. Thus, there is a sequence $(x_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}} \subseteq K$ such that $|x_n| > n$, for $n \in \mathbb{N}$. This sequence must have a monotone subsequence in $K$ that is also unbounded. However, every subsequence of this subsequence must also be unbounded, hence, cannot converge. This violates compactness since every sequence must have a convergent subsequence. Hence, $K$ must be bounded.

  Now suppose that $K \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is compact but not closed. Thus, the complement is not open and there must be some $x \in \mathbb{R} \setminus K$ such that $B_\epsilon(x) \not\subseteq \mathbb{R} \setminus K$ for all $\epsilon > 0$. In particular, for all $\epsilon = \frac{1}{n+1}$ for $n \in \mathbb{N}$. Therefore, there is $x_n \in B_{\frac{1}{n+1}}(x) \cap K$. This sequence $(x_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}} \subseteq K$ converges towards $x \notin K$. Hence, none of its subsequences can converge in $K$. Hence, $K$ must be closed.

- ($\Leftarrow$) Conversely, if $K$ is closed and bounded, then every sequence in $K$ is bounded and hence admits a convergent subsequence by Bolzano-Weierstraß. Closedness ensures that the limit lies in $K$, so $K$ is compact.
