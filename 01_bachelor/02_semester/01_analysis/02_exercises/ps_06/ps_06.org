* Problem 1 :taylor_formula:multi_index:chain_rule:
** Task
#+begin_quote
( ... used in the proof of the Taylor formula)

(a) Suppose that $\alpha \in \mathbb{N}^n$ and $h \in \mathbb{R}^n$ such that $\|h\|_\infty \leq 1$. Prove that
\[ |h^\alpha| \leq \|h\|_{\infty}^{|\alpha|} \]

(b) Let $k \in \mathbb{N}$ and suppose that $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is $k$ times continuously differentiable. For $x, x_0 \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and $h = x - x_0$, define

\[ g : [-1, 1] \to \mathbb{R}, \quad g(t) := f(x_0 + th) \]

For $j \in \{0, \ldots, k\}$ prove that $g$ is $k$ times continuously differentiable and

\[ g^{(j)}(t) = \sum_{\substack{\alpha \in \mathbb{N}^n \\ |\alpha| = j}} \frac{j!}{\alpha!} (\partial^\alpha f)(x_0 + th)\, h^\alpha, \quad t \in [-1, 1] \]

(hint: induction and chain rule)
#+end_quote

** Solution

*** (a)

Define

$$|h^{\alpha}| = \prod_{i=1}^n |h_i|^{\alpha_i}, \quad \|h\|_\infty^{|\alpha|} = \prod_{i=1}^n \|h\|_{\infty}^{\alpha_i}$$

As, $|h_i| \le \|h\|_{\infty}$,

$$|h^{\alpha}| = \prod_{i=1}^n |h_i|^{\alpha_i} \le \prod_{i=1}^n \|h\|_{\infty}^{\alpha_i} = \|h\|_\infty^{|\alpha|}$$

*** (b)

*Setup.* For $k \in \mathbb{N}$, $f : \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}, \, f \in C^k$. Let $x, x_0 \in \mathbb{R}^n, \, h = x - x_0$. Define $g : \left[ -1, 1 \right] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}, \, g(t) := f(x_0 + th)$.

*Proposition.* for $j \in \{ 0, \ldots , k \}$:

1. $g \in C^k$
2. For such $j$, $g^{(j)}(t) = \sum_{|\alpha| = j} \frac{j!}{\alpha!} (\partial^\alpha f)(x_0 + th)\, h^\alpha$

*Base case.* $j = 0$, only $\alpha = 0 \in \mathbb{N}^n$ contributes.

\begin{align*}
g^{(0)}(t) &= \sum_{|\alpha| = 0} \frac{0!}{0! \ldots 0!} (\partial_1^0 \ldots \partial_n^0 f)(x_0 + th) h_1^0 \ldots h_n^0 \\
&= \frac{1}{1} f(x_0 + th) \cdot 1 \\
&= f(x_0 + th) \\
\end{align*}

Holds.

*Inductive hypothesis.*

* Problem 2 :taylor_formula:hessian:gradient:
** Task
#+begin_quote
Suppose $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is twice continuously differentiable.

(a) Prove that the Taylor formula centered at $x_0 \in \mathbb{R}^n$ of order 2 is
\[ f(x) = f(x_0) + (\nabla f(x_0))^\top (x - x_0) + \frac{1}{2}(x - x_0)^\top H_f(x_0)(x - x_0) + o(\|x - x_0\|^2) \]

(b) Implement and plot the Taylor approximation of $f$ of order 2 by a heatmap for the function
\[ f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}, \quad f(x, y) = x^4 + y^4 + x^2 + y^2 \]
#+end_quote

** Solution

*** (a)

$$f(x) = \sum_{|\alpha| \le 2} \frac{\partial^\alpha f(x_0)}{\alpha!} (x - x_0)^\alpha + o(\|x - x_0\|^2) \quad (x \rightarrow x_0)$$

* Problem 3 :local_extrema:gradient:
** Task
#+begin_quote
Consider the function $f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$,
\[ f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2 + 2x + 4y \]

(a) Determine all local extrema of $f$.

(b) Plot $f$ in Julia by a heatmap to check your calculations in Part (a).
#+end_quote

** Solution

*** (a)

The function $f$ is twice continuously differentiable on $\mathbb{R}^2$. Compute the partial derivatives:

$$\partial_1 f(x, y) = 2x + 2, \quad \partial_2 f(x, y) = 2y + 4.$$

A critical point satisfies $\operatorname{grad} f(x, y) = 0$, i.e.

\begin{align*}
2x + 2 &= 0 \implies x = -1, \\
2y + 4 &= 0 \implies y = -2.
\end{align*}

Hence $f$ has the unique critical point $x_0 = (-1, -2)$.

The second-order partial derivatives are

$$\partial_1 \partial_1 f = 2, \quad \partial_2 \partial_2 f = 2, \quad \partial_1 \partial_2 f = \partial_2 \partial_1 f = 0,$$

so the Hessian matrix at $x_0$ is

$$H_f(x_0) = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}.$$

For any $h = (h_1, h_2) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{0\}$,

$$h^\top H_f(x_0) h = 2 h_1^2 + 2 h_2^2 > 0,$$

so $H_f(x_0)$ is positive definite. By Theorem 7.2.5(1), $x_0 = (-1, -2)$ is a strict local minimum, with

$$f(-1, -2) = (-1)^2 + (-2)^2 + 2(-1) + 4(-2) = -5.$$

Since $x_0$ is the only critical point and $H_f$ is positive definite on all of $\mathbb{R}^2$, $f$ has no other local extrema.

* Problem 4 :local_extrema:quadratic_form:hessian:
** Task
#+begin_quote
Consider the function $f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$,

\[ f(x, y) = x^2 + 3y^2 + 2xy \]

(a) Determine all local extrema of $f$.

(b) Plot $f$ in Julia by a heatmap to check your calculations in Part (a).
#+end_quote

** Solution

*** (a)

The function $f$ is twice continuously differentiable on $\mathbb{R}^2$. Compute the partial derivatives:

$$\partial_1 f(x, y) = 2x + 2y, \quad \partial_2 f(x, y) = 6y + 2x.$$

A critical point satisfies $\operatorname{grad} f(x, y) = 0$, i.e.

From the system:

\begin{align*}
2x + 2y &= 0 \implies x = -y, \\
6y + 2x &= 0 \implies x = -3y.
\end{align*}

Combining gives $-y = -3y$, hence $y = 0$ and $x = 0$. Thus $f$ has the unique critical point $x_0 = (0, 0)$.

The second-order partial derivatives are

$$\partial_1 \partial_1 f = 2, \quad \partial_2 \partial_2 f = 6, \quad \partial_1 \partial_2 f = \partial_2 \partial_1 f = 2,$$

so the Hessian matrix at $x_0$ is

$$H_f(x_0) = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 2 \\ 2 & 6 \end{pmatrix}.$$

For any $h = (h_1, h_2) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{0\}$,

\begin{align*}
h^\top H_f(x_0) h &= 2 h_1^2 + 4 h_1 h_2 + 6 h_2^2 \\
&= 2(h_1^2 + 2 h_1 h_2 + h_2^2) + 4 h_2^2 \\
&= 2(h_1 + h_2)^2 + 4 h_2^2 > 0,
\end{align*}

since the sum vanishes only if $h_2 = 0$ and $h_1 + h_2 = 0$, i.e. $h = 0$. Hence $H_f(x_0)$ is positive definite. By Theorem 7.2.5(1), $x_0 = (0, 0)$ is a strict local minimum, with

$$f(0, 0) = 0.$$

Since $x_0$ is the only critical point and $H_f$ is constant and positive definite on all of $\mathbb{R}^2$, $f$ has no other local extrema.
