* 4.1 Multiplying square matrices

** Setup

- $A = (a_{ik})$, $B = (b_{kj})$ square $n \times n$, dense.
- Product $C = A \cdot B$: each entry $c_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{ik} \cdot b_{kj}$
- Computing $C$ means computing $n^2$ entries, each a sum of $n$ products.

** Naive: =MATRIX-MULTIPLY=

- Three nested loops (row $i$, column $j$, summation $k$), each $1$ to $n$.
- Computes $C = C + A \cdot B$ (accumulates; init $C = 0$ if you want just $A \cdot B$).
- Initialising $C$: $\Theta(n^2)$, absorbed into the $\Theta(n^3)$ total.

** Divide-and-Conquer: =MATRIX-MULTIPLY-RECURSIVE=

*** Block decomposition

Partition $A, B, C$ into four $n/2 \times n/2$ submatrices each. The block product yields four equations (4.5–4.8), e.g.:

$C_{11} = A_{11} B_{11} + A_{12} B_{21}$

This requires *8 multiplications* and *4 additions* of $n/2 \times n/2$ matrices.

*** Algorithm design

- Base case $n = 1$: scalar multiply-add, $\Theta(1)$.
- Partition via index arithmetic (not copying) → $\Theta(1)$ divide cost.
- 8 recursive calls, each on $n/2 \times n/2$ subproblems.
- No separate combine step: each call accumulates into the right $C_{ij}$ block.

*** Recurrence

\[
T(n) = 8\,T(n/2) + \Theta(1) \implies T(n) = \Theta(n^3)
\]

No improvement over the naive version.

** Why No Speedup? (Recursion Tree Intuition)

- Branching factor 8 with $\Theta(1)$ work per node → leaf-dominated.
- Number of leaves: $8^{\lg n} = n^{\lg 8} = n^3$.
- Compare merge sort: branching 2, work $\Theta(n)$ per level → balanced across all $\lg n$ levels → $\Theta(n \lg n)$.
- The "bushier" tree (8 children vs 2) overwhelms the smaller per-node cost.

** Takeaway: Path to Strassen

The bottleneck is the 8 recursive multiplications. Strassen reduces this to 7 (at the cost of more $\Theta(n^2)$ additions), giving:

\[
T(n) = 7\,T(n/2) + \Theta(n^2) \implies T(n) = \Theta(n^{\lg 7}) \approx \Theta(n^{2.81})
\]

Core principle: *trading one multiplication for several additions is worth it* because multiplications control the branching factor and thus the asymptotic growth.

* 4.2 Strassen's algorithm for matrix multiplication

** Core Idea

Strassen observed that the block matrix product $C = A \cdot B$ can be computed
with *7 multiplications* instead of 8, at the cost of more additions and
subtractions ($\Theta(n^2)$ work). Since additions are cheap relative to the
recursive branching that multiplications cause, this is a net win.

** The Four Steps

1. *Divide*: partition $A, B, C$ into $n/2 \times n/2$ submatrices (same as 4.1).
2. *Create* 10 auxiliary matrices $S_1, \dots, S_{10}$, each an addition or
   subtraction of two submatrices → $\Theta(n^2)$.
3. *Recursively compute* 7 products $P_1, \dots, P_7$ using the $S_i$ and
   original submatrices.
4. *Combine*: express $C_{11}, C_{12}, C_{21}, C_{22}$ as sums/differences
   of the $P_i$ → $\Theta(n^2)$.

** The 10 Auxiliary Matrices

\begin{align*}
S_1 &= B_{12} - B_{22}  &  S_6  &= B_{11} + B_{22} \\
S_2 &= A_{11} + A_{12}  &  S_7  &= A_{12} - A_{22} \\
S_3 &= A_{21} + A_{22}  &  S_8  &= B_{21} + B_{22} \\
S_4 &= B_{21} - B_{11}  &  S_9  &= A_{11} - A_{21} \\
S_5 &= A_{11} + A_{22}  &  S_{10} &= B_{11} + B_{12}
\end{align*}

Each is one $n/2 \times n/2$ addition/subtraction → 10 total, all $\Theta(n^2)$.

** The 7 Products

\begin{align*}
P_1 &= A_{11} \cdot S_1       &  P_5 &= S_5 \cdot S_6 \\
P_2 &= S_2 \cdot B_{22}       &  P_6 &= S_7 \cdot S_8 \\
P_3 &= S_3 \cdot B_{11}       &  P_7 &= S_9 \cdot S_{10} \\
P_4 &= A_{22} \cdot S_4
\end{align*}

Each is a *recursive* $n/2 \times n/2$ multiplication → 7 recursive calls.

** Combining the Results

\begin{align*}
C_{11} &= P_5 + P_4 - P_2 + P_6 \\
C_{12} &= P_1 + P_2 \\
C_{21} &= P_3 + P_4 \\
C_{22} &= P_5 + P_1 - P_3 - P_7
\end{align*}

Each is $\Theta(n^2)$ (additions of $n/2 \times n/2$ matrices).

** Recurrence and Analysis

- 7 recursive calls on $n/2 \times n/2$ subproblems.
- $\Theta(n^2)$ work outside the recursion (creating $S_i$, combining $P_i$).

\[
T(n) = 7\,T(n/2) + \Theta(n^2) \implies T(n) = \Theta(n^{\lg 7}) \approx \Theta(n^{2.807})
\]

By the master method: $a = 7$, $b = 2$, $f(n) = \Theta(n^2)$,
$n^{\log_b a} = n^{\lg 7} \approx n^{2.807} > n^2$, so case 1 applies.

** Why It Works (Intuition)

- The 7 products are carefully chosen so that when combined they reproduce
  exactly the same 8 terms from equations (4.5)–(4.8).
- Each $P_i$ "encodes" contributions to multiple $C_{ij}$ blocks simultaneously.
- We pay more additions ($\Theta(n^2)$), but save one multiplication — reducing
  the branching factor from 8 to 7 across all recursion levels.

** Practical Notes

- Strassen's has larger constant factors → only faster for large $n$.
- Common to switch to naive multiplication below a crossover $n_0$.
- Numerical stability is slightly worse due to more additions/subtractions.
- Not the asymptotic optimum: current best is $\approx O(n^{2.371})$ (not practical).

* 4.3 The substitution method for solving recurrences

** The Method

Two steps:
1. *Guess* the form of the solution with symbolic constants (e.g. $T(n) \leq cn \lg n$).
2. *Prove* by strong induction that the guess holds — substitute the hypothesis into the recurrence and verify the algebra closes.

Called "substitution" because you literally substitute your guessed bound for $T$ on smaller inputs into the recurrence.

Best to prove $O$ and $\Omega$ separately, then combine for $\Theta$.

** Worked Example: $T(n) = 2T(\lfloor n/2 \rfloor) + \Theta(n)$

- Guess $T(n) = O(n \lg n)$, i.e. $T(n) \leq cn \lg n$ for some $c > 0$, $n \geq n_0$.
- Inductive hypothesis: assume $T(\lfloor n/2 \rfloor) \leq c \lfloor n/2 \rfloor \lg \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$.
- Substitute:

\begin{align*}
T(n) &\leq 2(c(n/2)\lg(n/2)) + \Theta(n) \\
     &= cn\lg n - cn\lg 2 + \Theta(n) \\
     &= cn\lg n - cn + \Theta(n) \\
     &\leq cn\lg n
\end{align*}

  Last step holds when $c$ is large enough that $cn$ dominates the $\Theta(n)$ term.

- Base case: pick $n_0 = 2$, choose $c \geq \max\{T(2), T(3)\}/(2\lg 2)$.

** Making a Good Guess

- *Pattern matching*: if it looks like a known recurrence, guess a similar solution. E.g. $T(n) = 2T(n/2 + 17) + \Theta(n)$ looks like merge sort — the $+17$ is negligible for large $n$, so guess $O(n \lg n)$.
- *Bracket and tighten*: start with a loose lower bound ($\Omega(n)$) and a loose upper bound ($O(n^2)$), then try to narrow both toward the tight answer.
- *Recursion trees* (Section 4.4) are the main tool for generating guesses.

** Trick: Subtracting a Lower-Order Term

Sometimes the right guess fails in the algebra by a small leftover. Fix: *strengthen the hypothesis* by subtracting a lower-order term.

Example: $T(n) = 2T(n/2) + \Theta(1)$, guess $T(n) = O(n)$.

- Trying $T(n) \leq cn$ gives $T(n) \leq cn + \Theta(1)$ — doesn't close.
- Strengthen to $T(n) \leq cn - d$:

\begin{align*}
T(n) &\leq 2(c(n/2) - d) + \Theta(1) \\
     &= cn - 2d + \Theta(1) \\
     &\leq cn - d
\end{align*}

  as long as $d \geq \Theta(1)$. Now it closes.

*Why subtract, not add?* With $a$ recursive calls, a subtracted term $-d$ gets
subtracted $a$ times, creating slack. An added term $+d$ gets added $a$ times,
making things worse.

** Pitfall: Don't Use Asymptotic Notation in the Hypothesis

Using $T(n) = O(n)$ as the inductive hypothesis lets the hidden constant
change at each level — the "proof" becomes circular.

Wrong:
\begin{align*}
T(n) &\leq 2 \cdot O(n/2) + \Theta(n) = O(n) \quad \Leftarrow \textit{fallacy!}
\end{align*}

Right: always write explicit constants ($T(n) \leq cn$) and prove the
*exact* inequality $T(n) \leq cn$, not just "$T(n)$ is $O(n)$."

* 4.5 The Master method of solving recurrences

*Theorem 4.1 (Master Theorem)*

Let $a > 0$ and $b > 1$ be constants, and let $f(n)$ be a driving function that is defined and nonnegative on all sufficiently large reals. Define the recurrence $T(n)$ on $n \in \mathbb{N}$ by

$$T(n) = aT(n/b) + f(n)$$

where $aT(n/b)$ actually means $a'T(\lfloor n/b \rfloor) + a''T(\lceil n/b \rceil)$ for some constants $a' \ge 0$ and $a'' \ge 0$ satisfying $a = a' + a''$. Then the asymptotic behavior of $T(n)$ can be characterized as follows:

1. If there exists a constant $\epsilon > 0$ such that $f(n) = O(n^{\log_b a - \epsilon})$, then $T(n) = \Theta(n^{\log_b a})$.

2. If there exists a constant $k \ge 0$ such that $f(n) = \Theta(n^{\log_b a} \lg^k n)$, then $T(n) = \Theta(n^{\log_b a} \lg^{k+1} n)$.

3. If there exists a constant $\epsilon > 0$ such that $f(n) = \Omega(n^{\log_b a + \epsilon})$, and if $f(n)$ additionally satisfies the regularity condition $af(n/b) \le cf(n)$ for some constant $c < 1$ and all sufficiently large $n$, then $T(n) = \Theta(f(n))$.
