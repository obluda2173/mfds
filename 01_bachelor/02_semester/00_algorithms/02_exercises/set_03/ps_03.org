* Problem 30 :binomial_coefficient:stirling_approximation:asymptotics:
** Task
#+begin_quote
Prove that

(a) $\binom{2n}{n} = \frac{2^{2n}}{\sqrt{\pi n}} \left(1 + O\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right)$,

(b) $\binom{n}{k} \geq \frac{n^k}{4 k!}$ if $k \leq \sqrt{n}$.
#+end_quote

** Solution

*** (a)

Stirling approximation:

$$n! = \sqrt{2 \pi n} \left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n \left(1 + O\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right)$$
\begin{align*}
\binom{2n}{n} &= \frac{(2n)!}{(n!)^2} \\
&= \frac{\sqrt{4\pi n} \left(\frac{2n}{e}\right)^{2n} \left(1 + O\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right)}{\left(\sqrt{2 \pi n} \left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n \left(1 + O\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right)\right)^2} \\
&= \frac{2\sqrt{\pi n} \cdot 2^{2n} \left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^{2n} \left(1 + O\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right)}{2 \pi n \left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^{2n} \left(1 + O\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right)^2} \\
&= \frac{2^{2n}}{\sqrt{\pi n}} \cdot \frac{1 + O\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)}{\left(1 + O\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right)^2} \\
&= \frac{2^{2n}}{\sqrt{\pi n}} \left(1 + O\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right)
\end{align*}

*** (b)

\begin{align*}
\binom{n}{k} &= \frac{n (n-1) \cdots (n-k+1)}{k!} \\
&= \frac{n^k}{k!} \cdot \prod_{i=0}^{k-1} \left(1 - \frac{i}{n}\right)
\end{align*}

Using $\prod (1 - a_i) \geq 1 - \sum a_i$ for $a_i \in [0,1]$:

\begin{align*}
\prod_{i=0}^{k-1} \left(1 - \frac{i}{n}\right) &\geq 1 - \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} \frac{i}{n} = 1 - \frac{k(k-1)}{2n} > 1 - \frac{k^2}{2n} \geq 1 - \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{2} \geq \frac{1}{4}
\end{align*}

Hence $\binom{n}{k} \geq \frac{n^k}{k!} \cdot \frac{1}{4} = \frac{n^k}{4\,k!}$. $\qed$

* Problem 32 :conditional_independence:conditional_probability:
** Task
#+begin_quote
Let $A$, $B$, $C$ be events, i.e., subsets of $\Omega$. We call $A$ and $B$ conditionally independent given $C$, if

$\Pr(A \cap B \mid C) = \Pr(A \mid C) \Pr(B \mid C)$.

Give an example of three events $A$, $B$, and $C$ so that $A$ and $B$ are not independent but are conditionally independent given $C$.
#+end_quote

** Solution

Let $\Omega = \left\{ (F, HH), (F, HT), (F, TH), (F, TT), (B, HH) \right\}$, with $\Pr(F, \cdot) = \frac{1}{8}$ and $\Pr(B, HH) = \frac{1}{2}$.

Define $A = \text{first flips heads}, B = \text{second flips heads}, C = \text{fair coin chosen}$.

\begin{align*}
A &= \left\{ (F, HH), (F, HT), (B, HH) \right\} \\
B &= \left\{ (F, HH), (F, TH), (B, HH) \right\} \\
C &= \left\{ (F, HH), (F, HT), (F, TH), (F, TT) \right\} \\
\end{align*}

- Non independence of $A$, $B$:

  \begin{align*}
  A \cap B &= \left\{ (F, HH), (B, HH) \right\}, \qquad \Pr(A \cap B) = \frac{1}{8} + \frac{1}{2} = \frac{5}{8} \\
  \Pr(A) &= \underbrace{\frac{1}{8}}_{(F, HH)} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{8}}_{(F, HT)} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}}_{(B, HH)} = \frac{3}{4} \\
  \Pr(B) &= \underbrace{\frac{1}{8}}_{(F, HH)} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{8}}_{(F, TH)} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}}_{(B, HH)} = \frac{3}{4} \\
  \Pr(A)\Pr(B) &= \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{3}{4} = \frac{9}{16} \neq \frac{5}{8} = \Pr(A \cap B) \\
  \end{align*}

- Conditional independence given $C$:

  \begin{align*}
  \Pr(A \mid C) &= \frac{\Pr(A \cap C)}{\Pr(C)} = \frac{1/4}{1/2} = \frac{1}{2} \\
  \Pr(B \mid C) &= \frac{\Pr(B \cap C)}{\Pr(C)} = \frac{1}{2} \\
  \Pr(A \cap B \mid C) &= \frac{\left\{ (F, HH) \right\}}{\Pr(C)} = \frac{1/8}{1/2} = 1/4 = \Pr(A \mid C) \Pr(B \mid C) \\
  \end{align*}

* Problem 33 :expected_value:central_limit_theorem:
** Task
#+begin_quote
You are a fantasy role playing fan, and you know that the damage you cause with your fireball attack is $40d6$, which means that you have to roll 40 ordinary 6 sided dice and add up all the numbers (of course you could also roll the same 6 sided dice 40 times). Since this is rather tedious, you say that you would like to replace the rolling of the dice by the expected value. What is the expected value, and what is the probability that you are off by more than 1 when you actually do roll the dice?
#+end_quote

** Solution

Let $S = \sum_{i=1}^{40} X_i$ where $X_i$ are i.i.d (independent and identical distributed) rolls of a fair die.

\begin{align*}
E[X_i] &= \sum_{x=1}^6 x \cdot \frac{1}{6} = \frac{7}{2} \\
E[S] &= 40 \cdot \frac{7}{2} = 140
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}
E[X_i^2] &= \sum_{x=1}^6 x^2 \cdot \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1 + 4 + 9 + 16 + 25 + 36}{6} = \frac{91}{6} \\
\mathrm{Var}(X_i) &= E[X_i^2] - (E[X_i])^2 = \frac{91}{6} - \frac{49}{4} = \frac{35}{12}
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}
\mathrm{Var}(S) &= 40 \cdot \frac{35}{12} = \frac{350}{3}, \qquad
\sigma_S = \sqrt{\frac{350}{3}} \approx 10.80
\end{align*}

By CLT, $S \dot\sim N(140, \frac{350}{3})$. Standardizing:

\begin{align*}
P(|S - 140| > 1) &= 1 - P\!\left(|Z| \leq \frac{1}{\sigma_S}\right) \\
&= 1 - P(|Z| \leq 0.0926) \\
&= 1 - (2\Phi(0.0926) - 1) \\
&= 1 - (2 \cdot 0.5369 - 1) \\
&= 1 - 0.0738 \\
&\approx 0.926
\end{align*}

The expected value is $140$. The probability of being off by more than $1$ is $\approx 92.6\%$.
