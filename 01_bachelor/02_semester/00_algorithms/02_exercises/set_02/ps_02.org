* Problem 11 :DONE:
** Task
#+begin_quote
For sequences $f(n)$, $g(n)$, $h(n)$, and $k(n)$ prove that $f(n) = \Omega(h(n))$ and $g(n) = \omega(k(n))$ implies $f(n) + g(n) = \Omega(h(n) + k(n))$.
#+end_quote

** Solution

Assume $f(n) = \Omega(h(n)), \, g(n) = \omega(k(n))$. Show $f(n) + g(n) = \Omega(h(n) + k(n))$.

$$\Omega(h(n)) = \left\{ f(n) : \exists c_1, n_1 > 0 : 0 \le c_1 \cdot h(n) \le f(n), \forall n \ge n_1 \right\}$$

$$\omega(k(n)) = \left\{ g(n) : \forall c_2 > 0, \exists n_2 > 0 : 0 \le c_2 \cdot k(n) < g(n), \forall n \ge n_2 \right\}$$

Let $n_0 = \max(n_1, n_2)$ and $c_2 = c_1$. Then $\forall n \ge n_0$:

\begin{align*}
0 \le c_1 \cdot h(n) &\le f(n) \\
0 \le c_1 \cdot k(n) &< g(n) \\
0 \le c_1 \cdot \left( h(n) + k(n) \right) &\le f(n) + g(n)
\end{align*}

Hence $f(n) + g(n) = \Omega(h(n) + k(n))$. $\blacksquare$

* Problem 12 :DONE:
** Task
#+begin_quote
Let $f(n) = O(g(n))$ and $g(n) = o(h(n))$. Prove that $f(n) = o(h(n))$.
#+end_quote

** Solution

Assume $f(n) = O(g(n))$ and $g(n) = o(h(n))$. Show $f(n) = o(h(n))$.

$$O(g(n)) = \left\{ f(n) : \exists c_1, n_1 > 0 : 0 \le f(n) \le c_1 \cdot g(n), \, \forall n \ge n_1 \right\}$$

$$o(h(n)) = \left\{ g(n) : \forall c_2 > 0, \exists n_2 > 0 : 0 \le g(n) < c_2 \cdot h(n), \, \forall n \ge n_2 \right\}$$

Let $c > 0$ be arbitrary. Choose $c_2 = \frac{c}{c_1}$ and $n_0 = \max(n_1, n_2)$. Then $\forall n \ge n_0$:

\begin{align*}
0 \le f(n) &\le c_1 \cdot g(n) \\
  &< c_1 \cdot c_2 \cdot h(n) \\
  &< c_1 \cdot \frac{c}{c_1} \cdot h(n) \\
  &< c \cdot h(n)
\end{align*}

Hence $f(n) = o(h(n))$. $\blacksquare$

* Problem 13 :DONE:
** Task
#+begin_quote
Use Stirling's formula

\[
n! = \sqrt{2\pi n} \left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n \left(1 + \Theta\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right).
\]

to show that

(a) $n! = o(n^n)$,

(b) $n! = \omega(2^n)$,

(c) $\lg(n!) = \Theta(n \lg n)$.
#+end_quote

** Solution

*** (a)

\begin{align*}
\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \dfrac{\sqrt{2\pi n} \left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n \left(1 + \Theta\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right)}{n^n}
  &= \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \sqrt{2\pi n} \cdot \frac{1}{e^n} \cdot \left(1 + \Theta\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right) \\
  &= \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \sqrt{2\pi n} \cdot \frac{1}{e^n} \cdot 1 \\
  &= 0
\end{align*}

since $\sqrt{2\pi n}$ grows polynomially while $e^n$ grows exponentially.

Hence $n! = o(n^n)$. $\blacksquare$

*** (b)

\begin{align*}
\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \dfrac{\sqrt{2\pi n} \left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n \left(1 + \Theta\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right)}{2^n}
  &= \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \sqrt{2\pi n} \cdot \left(\frac{n}{2e}\right)^n \cdot \left(1 + \Theta\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right) \\
  &= \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \sqrt{2\pi n} \cdot \left(\frac{n}{2e}\right)^n \cdot 1 \\
  &= \infty \\
\end{align*}

Hence, $n! = \omega(2^n)$. $\blacksquare$

*** (c)

\begin{align*}
\lg(n!) &= \lg\left(\sqrt{2\pi n}\right) + n \lg n - n \lg e + \lg\left(1 + \Theta\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right) \\
\frac{\lg(n!)}{n \lg n} &= \frac{\frac{1}{2}\lg(2\pi n)}{n \lg n} + 1 - \frac{\lg e}{\lg n} + \frac{\lg\left(1 + \Theta\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right)}{n \lg n} \\
\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \frac{\lg(n!)}{n \lg n} &= 0 + 1 - 0 + 0 = 1
\end{align*}

Since the limit is a positive constant, $\lg(n!) = \Theta(n \lg n)$. $\blacksquare$

* Problem 15 :DONE:
** Task
#+begin_quote
Use the substitution method to prove that the recursion
\[
T(n) = 4T\left(\frac{n}{2}\right) + n
\]
implies $T(n) = \Theta(n^2)$.

Hint: Subtract a lower order term.
#+end_quote

** Solution

- Upper bound: $T(n) = O(n^2)$

  Trying $T(n) \leq cn^2$ directly gives $cn^2 + n$, which doesn't close
  because the $+n$ term cannot be absorbed. To fix this, subtract a lower-order
  term from the hypothesis.

  - Inductive hypothesis: assume $T(k) \leq ck^2 - dk$ for all $k < n$.
  - Substitute:

    \begin{align*}
    T(n) &\leq 4\left(c \cdot \frac{n^2}{4} - d \cdot \frac{n}{2}\right) + n \\
         &= cn^2 - 2dn + n \\
         &= cn^2 - dn - (dn - n) \\
         &\leq cn^2 - dn
    \end{align*}

    Last step holds when $dn - n \geq 0$, i.e. $d \geq 1$. Pick $d = 1$.

  Since $cn^2 - dn \leq cn^2$, this establishes $T(n) = O(n^2)$.

- Lower bound: $T(n) = \Omega(n^2)$

  - Inductive hypothesis: assume $T(k) \geq ck^2$ for all $k < n$.

  - Substitute:

    \begin{align*}
    T(n) &\geq 4\left(c \cdot \frac{n^2}{4}\right) + n \\
         &= cn^2 + n \\
         &\geq cn^2
    \end{align*}

    Holds trivially since $n > 0$.

- Conclusion

  Since $T(n) = O(n^2)$ and $T(n) = \Omega(n^2)$, we have $T(n) = \Theta(n^2)$. $\blacksquare$

* Problem 16 :DONE:
** Task
#+begin_quote
For the recurrence

$$T(n) = 4T\left(\frac{n}{3}\right) + n$$

sketch the recursion tree and derive a guess for an asymptotic upper bound of its solution. Then use induction to prove your guess.
#+end_quote

** Solution

- Deriving the guess via recursion tree

  The recurrence $T(n) = 4T(n/3) + n$ spawns 4 subproblems of size $n/3$ at each level. At level $k$ there are $4^k$ nodes, each contributing $n/3^k$ work, so the total work at level $k$ is $(4/3)^k \cdot n$. The tree has depth $\log_3 n$. Since the ratio $4/3 > 1$, the geometric series is dominated by its last term:

  $$4^{\log_3 n} = n^{\log_3 4}$$

  This suggests $T(n) = O(n^{\log_3 4})$.

- Proof by substitution

  - Inductive hypothesis: assume $T(k) \leq ck^{\log_3 4} - dk$ for all $k < n$.

  - Substitute:

    \begin{align*}
    T(n) &\leq 4\left(\frac{n^{\log_3 4}}{4} - d \cdot \frac{n}{3}\right) + n \\
         &= cn^{\log_3 4} - \frac{4dn}{3} + n \\
         &= cn^{\log_3 4} - dn - \left(\frac{dn}{3} - n\right) \\
         &\leq cn^{\log_3 4} - dn
    \end{align*}

    Last step holds when $\frac{dn}{3} - n \geq 0$, i.e. $d \geq 3$. Pick $d = 3$.

  Since $cn^{\log_3 4} - dn \leq cn^{\log_3 4}$, this establishes $T(n) = O(n^{\log_3 4})$. $\blacksquare$
