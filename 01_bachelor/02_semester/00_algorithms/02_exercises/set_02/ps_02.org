* Problem 18 :DONE:
** Task
#+begin_quote
Use the Master Theorem to provide $\Theta$ bounds for the following recursions (assume $n_0 = 1$ for all of them).

(a) $T(n) = 2T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{4}\right) + \Theta(1)$,

(b) $T(n) = 2T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{4}\right) + \Theta(\sqrt{n})$,

(c) $T(n) = 2T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{4}\right) + \Theta(\sqrt{n} \lg^2 n)$,

(d) $T(n) = 2T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{4}\right) + \Theta(n)$,

(e) $T(n) = 2T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{4}\right) + \Theta(n^2)$.
#+end_quote

** Solution

*** (a)

\begin{align*}
a &= 2 \quad (\text{number of subproblems}) \\
b &= 4 \quad (\text{factor by which input shrinks}) \\
d &= 0 \quad (\text{since the non recursive block is } \Theta(n^0) = \Theta(1)) \\
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}
\text{since } d &< \log_ba \\
              0 &< \log_42 \\
              0 &< \frac{1}{2} \\
\end{align*}

It is a Master Theorem Case 1, such that

$$T(n) = \Theta(n^{\log_ba}) = \Theta(n^{\log_42}) = \Theta(\sqrt{n})$$

*** (b)

\begin{align*}
a &= 2 \quad (\text{number of subproblems}) \\
b &= 4 \quad (\text{factor by which input shrinks}) \\
d &= \frac{1}{2} \quad (\text{since the non recursive block is } \Theta(\sqrt{n}) = \Theta(n^{1/2})) \\
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}
\text{since } d &= \log_ba \\
              \frac{1}{2} &= \log_42 \\
              \frac{1}{2} &= \frac{1}{2} \\
\end{align*}

It is a Master Theorem Case 2, such that

$$T(n) = \Theta(n^{\log_ba} \lg^{k+1} n) = \Theta(n^{\log_42} \lg n) = \Theta(\sqrt{n} \lg n)$$

*** (c)

\begin{align*}
a &= 2 \quad (\text{number of subproblems}) \\
b &= 4 \quad (\text{factor by which input shrinks}) \\
\end{align*}

Notice that $f(n) = \Theta(\sqrt{n}\lg^2 n) =  \Theta(n^{1/2} \lg^2 n)$. The polynomial part equals $n^{\log_b a}$. The extra $\lg^2 n$ factor matches Case 2 pattern.

\begin{align*}
f(n) &= \Theta(n^{\log_b a} \lg^k n) \quad \text{ with } k=2 \\
\text{Hence, } T(n) &= \Theta(n^{\log_b a} \lg^{k+1} n) \\
                    &= \Theta(n^{1/2} \lg^{2+1} n) \\
                    &= \Theta(\sqrt{n} \lg^3 n) \\
\end{align*}

*** (d)

\begin{align*}
a &= 2 \quad (\text{number of subproblems}) \\
b &= 4 \quad (\text{factor by which input shrinks}) \\
d &= 1 \quad (\text{since the non recursive block is } \Theta(n) = \Theta(n^1)) \\
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}
\text{since } d &> \log_ba \\
              1 &> \log_42 \\
              1 &> \frac{1}{2} \\
\end{align*}

It is a Master Theorem Case 3, such that

$$T(n) = \Theta(f(n)) = \Theta(n)$$

*** (e)

\begin{align*}
a &= 2 \quad (\text{number of subproblems}) \\
b &= 4 \quad (\text{factor by which input shrinks}) \\
d &= 2 \quad (\text{since the non recursive block is } \Theta(n^2)) \\
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}
\text{since } d &> \log_ba \\
              2 &> \log_42 \\
              2 &> \frac{1}{2} \\
\end{align*}

It is a Master Theorem Case 3, such that

$$T(n) = \Theta(f(n)) = \Theta(n^2)$$

* Problem 19 :DONE:
** Task
#+begin_quote
Professor Caesar wants to develop a matrix-multiplication algorithm that is asymptotically faster than Strassen's algorithm. His algorithm will use the divide-and-conquer method, dividing each matrix into submatrices of size $\dfrac{n}{4} \times \dfrac{n}{4}$, and the divide and combine steps together will take $\Theta(n^2)$ time. Suppose that the professor's algorithm creates $a$ recursive subproblems of that size. What is the largest integer value of $a$ for which his algorithm could possibly run asymptotically faster than Strassen's? How does $a$ change if the subproblems have size $\dfrac{n}{3} \times \dfrac{n}{3}$ or $\dfrac{n}{5} \times \dfrac{n}{5}$?
#+end_quote

** Solution

Strassen algorithm is:
$$T(n) = 7\,T(n/2) + \Theta(n^2) \implies T(n) = \Theta(n^{\lg 7}) \approx \Theta(n^{2.807})$$

- Subproblem size $n/4 \times n/4$

  Professor Caesar's algorithm is:

  $$T(n) = aT(n/4) + \Theta(n^2) \implies T(n) = \Theta(n^{\log_4 a})$$

  Assume Caesar's algorithm outperforms Strassen's. Hence,

  \begin{align*}
  n^{\log_4 a} &< n^{\lg 7} \\
  \implies \log_4 a &< \log_2 7 \\
  \implies a &< 4^{\log_2 7} \\
              &< (2^2)^{\log_2 7} \\
              &< 2^{2\log_2 7} \\
              &< 7^2 = 49 \\
  \end{align*}

  Hence, the largest integer value of $a$ is $48$.

- Subproblem size $n/3 \times n/3$

  $$T(n) = aT(n/3) + \Theta(n^2) \implies T(n) = \Theta(n^{\log_3 a})$$

  \begin{align*}
  \log_3 a &< \log_2 7 \\
  \implies a &< 3^{\log_2 7} \\
              &< 3^{2.807\ldots} \\
              &< 21.85\ldots \\
  \end{align*}

  Hence, the largest integer value of $a$ is $21$.

- Subproblem size $n/5 \times n/5$

  $$T(n) = aT(n/5) + \Theta(n^2) \implies T(n) = \Theta(n^{\log_5 a})$$

  \begin{align*}
  \log_5 a &< \log_2 7 \\
  \implies a &< 5^{\log_2 7} \\
              &< 5^{2.807\ldots} \\
              &< 70.18\ldots \\
  \end{align*}

  Hence, the largest integer value of $a$ is $70$.

* Problem 20 :DONE:
** Task
#+begin_quote
Consider the function $f(n) = \lg n$. Argue that although $f\!\left(\dfrac{n}{2}\right) < f(n)$, the regularity condition $af\!\left(\dfrac{n}{b}\right) \leq cf(n)$ with $a = 1$ and $b = 2$ does not hold for any constant $c < 1$. Argue further that for any $\varepsilon > 0$, the condition in Case 3 of the Master theorem that $f(n) = \Omega(n^{\log_b a + \varepsilon})$ does not hold.
#+end_quote

** Solution

- Part 1

  We need to check whether $af(\frac{n}{b}) \le cf(n)$ holds for $a = 1, b = 2$ and $f(n) = \lg n$ for any constant $c < 1$.

  \begin{align*}
  af(\frac{n}{b}) &\le cf(n) \\
  1 \cdot f(\frac{n}{2}) &\le cf(n) \\
  \lg \frac{n}{2} &\le c\lg n \\
  \lg n - \lg 2 &\le c\lg n \\
  \lg n - 1 &\le c\lg n \\
  (1 - c) \lg n &\le 1 \\
   \lg n &\le \frac{1}{1 - c} \\
  \end{align*}

  But $\lg n$ grows without bound as $n \rightarrow \infty$. For a fixed $c < 1$, this could not hold for all large $n$.

  Hence, the regularity condition fails $\forall c < 1$.

- Part 2

  Case 3 of Master Theorem requires $\exists \epsilon > 0 : f(n) = \Omega(n^{\log_b a + \epsilon})$.

  With $a = 1, b = 2$, $\log_b a = \log_2 1 = 0$.

  So the Case 3 would require:

  $$f(n) = \Omega(n^{\epsilon})$$

  for some $\epsilon > 0$.

  However, logarithmic function could not grow polynomially, no matter hom small $\epsilon > 0$ is.

  $$\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \frac{\lg n}{n^\epsilon} = 0$$

  Thus,

  $$f(n) \neq \Omega(n^{\epsilon})$$

  for any $\epsilon > 0$.

* Problem 21 :DONE:
** Task
#+begin_quote
Show that for suitable constants $a > 0$, $b > 1$, $\varepsilon > 0$ the function $f(n) := 2^{\lceil \lg n \rceil}$ satisfies all conditions of Case 3 of the Master theorem except for the regularity condition.
#+end_quote

** Solution

Master Theorem Case 3 requires two conditions to hold for $f(n)$:
- Dominance condition: $f(n) = \Omega(n^{\log_b a + \varepsilon})$ for some $\varepsilon > 0$.
- Regularity condition: $a f(n/b) \le c f(n)$ for some constant $c < 1$ and all sufficiently large $n$.

Choose the constants $a = 1$, $b = 1.5$, and $\varepsilon = 0.5$. These satisfy the problem's requirements since $a > 0$, $b > 1$, and $\varepsilon > 0$.

- Dominance Condition

  Show: $f(n) = \Omega(n^{\log_b a + \varepsilon})$.

  First, note that $\log_{1.5} 1 = 0$. Thus, substituting our constants, we need to show $f(n) = \Omega(n^{0.5})$.

  We bound $f(n) = 2^{\lceil \lg n \rceil}$ to find its asymptotic growth:

  \begin{align*}
  \lg n \le &\lceil \lg n \rceil \le \lg n + 1 \\
  2^{\lg n} \le &2^{\lceil \lg n \rceil} \le 2^{\lg n + 1} \\
  n \le &2^{\lceil \lg n \rceil} \le 2n
  \end{align*}

  Hence, $f(n) = \Theta(n)$.

  Since $f(n) = \Theta(n)$, it grows strictly faster than $n^{0.5}$, meaning:

  $$f(n) = \Omega(n^{0.5})$$

  The dominance condition holds.

- Regularity Condition

  Show: $a f(n/b) \le c f(n)$ fails for any $c < 1$.

  Substituting our chosen constants and $f(n)$, we check if the following inequality can hold for $c < 1$:

  $$1 \cdot 2^{\lceil \lg(n/1.5) \rceil} \le c \cdot 2^{\lceil \lg n \rceil}$$

  To demonstrate that this fails, we only need to find a subsequence of $n$ where the inequality breaks down. Consider the sequence where $n$ is an exact power of 2, i.e., $n = 2^k$ for integers $k \ge 1$.

  For these values, $\lceil \lg n \rceil = \lceil \lg(2^k) \rceil = k$, so:

  $$f(n) = 2^k$$

  Now evaluate $f(n/1.5)$ for this same subsequence:

  $$f(n/1.5) = 2^{\lceil \lg(2^k / 1.5) \rceil} = 2^{\lceil k - \lg 1.5 \rceil}$$

  Since $\lg 1.5 \approx 0.58$, the fractional part subtracted from $k$ is small enough that the ceiling function rounds it right back up to $k$. Specifically, $\lceil k - 0.58 \rceil = k$. Therefore:

  $$f(n/1.5) = 2^k = f(n)$$

  Substituting this back into the regularity condition yields:

  $$f(n) \le c f(n) \implies 1 \le c$$

  This directly contradicts the requirement that $c < 1$. Therefore, the regularity condition fails for all $c < 1$.

* Problem 22 :SKIP:
** Task
#+begin_quote
Show that $f(n) = n^2$ satisfies the polynomial growth condition but $g(n) = 2^n$ does not.
#+end_quote

** Solution

*Definition:* A function $h(n)$ has polynomial growth if $\exists k > 0$ such that $h(n) = O(n^k)$.

- $f(n) = n^2$:

  Let $k = 2$. By definition of Big-O, we need $c > 0$ and $n_0 > 0$ such that $n^2 \le c \cdot n^k$ for all $n \ge n_0$.

  $$n^2 \le 1 \cdot n^2 \quad \forall n \ge 1$$

  Setting $c = 1$ and $n_0 = 1$ satisfies the condition.
  $$\implies f(n) = O(n^2)$$

  Therefore, $f(n)$ satisfies the polynomial growth condition.

- $g(n) = 2^n$:

  Assume for contradiction that $\exists k > 0$ such that $2^n = O(n^k)$.
  This implies that $\limsup_{n \to \infty} \frac{2^n}{n^k} < \infty$.

  Evaluate the limit using L'Hôpital's rule. Let $m = \lceil k \rceil$:

  $$\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{2^n}{n^k} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{\frac{d^m}{dn^m}(2^n)}{\frac{d^m}{dn^m}(n^k)}$$

  The $m$ th derivative of the numerator is $2^n(\ln 2)^m$. Since $m \ge k$, the $m$ th derivative of the denominator $n^k$ is a constant $C$ (where $C = k!$ if $k \in \mathbb{N}$, or $0$ otherwise).

  $$\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{2^n(\ln 2)^m}{C} = \infty$$

  $$\implies 2^n = \omega(n^k)$$

  This contradicts $2^n = O(n^k)$. Thus, $2^n \neq O(n^k)$ for any $k > 0$, and $g(n)$ does not satisfy the polynomial growth condition.

* Problem 24 :DONE:
** Task
#+begin_quote
Prove that $f(n) = n \lg n$ and $f(n) = n \lg \lg n$ satisfy the polynomial growth condition.
#+end_quote

** Solution

*Definition:* A function $f(n)$ has polynomial growth if $\exists k > 0$ such that $f(n) = O(n^k)$.

- $f(n) = n \lg n$:

  Choose $k = 2$. We know that $\lg n < n$ for all $n \ge 1$.

  $$n \lg n \le n \cdot n = n^2 \quad \forall n \ge 1$$

  By the definition of Big-O, setting $c = 1$ and $n_0 = 1$:
  $$n \lg n \le 1 \cdot n^2 \implies n \lg n = O(n^2)$$

  Therefore, $n \lg n$ is polynomially bounded and satisfies the condition.

- $f(n) = n \lg \lg n$:

  Choose $k = 2$. For $n \ge 4$, the iterated logarithm is well-defined and strictly bounded by the logarithm, so $\lg \lg n < \lg n < n$.

  $$n \lg \lg n < n \cdot n = n^2 \quad \forall n \ge 4$$

  By the definition of Big-O, setting $c = 1$ and $n_0 = 4$:
  $$n \lg \lg n \le 1 \cdot n^2 \implies n \lg \lg n = O(n^2)$$

  Therefore, $n \lg \lg n$ is polynomially bounded and satisfies the condition.

* Problem 26 :DONE:
** Task
#+begin_quote
Use the Akra-Bazzi-Theorem to compute $\Theta$ expressions for the following recurrences:

(a) $T(n) = T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{2}\right) + T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{3}\right) + T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{6}\right) + \Theta(n \lg n)$,

(b) $T(n) = 3T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{3}\right) + 8T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{4}\right) + \Theta\!\left(\dfrac{n^2}{\lg n}\right)$,

(c) $T(n) = \dfrac{2}{3}\, T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{3}\right) + \dfrac{1}{3}\, T\!\left(\dfrac{2n}{3}\right) + \Theta(\lg n)$,

(d) $T(n) = \dfrac{1}{3}\, T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{3}\right) + \dfrac{1}{n}$,

(e) $T(n) = 3T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{3}\right) + 3T\!\left(\dfrac{2n}{3}\right) + n^2$.
#+end_quote

** Solution

*Akra-Bazzi Theorem:*
For $T(n) = \sum_{i=1}^k a_i T(b_i n) + g(n)$, find $p$ such that $\sum_{i=1}^k a_i b_i^p = 1$.
Then, $T(n) = \Theta\left(n^p \left(1 + \int_1^n \frac{g(u)}{u^{p+1}} du\right)\right)$.

- *(a)* $T(n) = T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{2}\right) + T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{3}\right) + T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{6}\right) + \Theta(n \lg n)$

  $\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^p + \left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^p + \left(\frac{1}{6}\right)^p = 1 \implies p = 1$

  $g(n) = n \lg n$

  $\int_1^n \frac{u \lg u}{u^2} du = \int_1^n \frac{\lg u}{u} du = \Theta(\lg^2 n)$

  $T(n) = \Theta\left(n^1 (1 + \lg^2 n)\right) = \Theta(n \lg^2 n)$

- *(b)* $T(n) = 3T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{3}\right) + 8T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{4}\right) + \Theta\!\left(\dfrac{n^2}{\lg n}\right)$

  $3\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^p + 8\left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^p = 1$.
  Observe that for $p=2$, $3(1/9) + 8(1/16) = 5/6 < 1$. Thus, $p < 2$.

  $g(n) = \frac{n^2}{\lg n}$

  $\int_1^n \frac{u^2 / \lg u}{u^{p+1}} du = \int_1^n \frac{u^{1-p}}{\lg u} du = \Theta\left(\frac{n^{2-p}}{\lg n}\right)$ (since $1-p > -1$)

  $T(n) = \Theta\left(n^p \left(1 + \frac{n^{2-p}}{\lg n}\right)\right) = \Theta\left(n^p + \frac{n^2}{\lg n}\right) = \Theta\left(\frac{n^2}{\lg n}\right)$

- *(c)* $T(n) = \dfrac{2}{3}\, T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{3}\right) + \dfrac{1}{3}\, T\!\left(\dfrac{2n}{3}\right) + \Theta(\lg n)$

  $\frac{2}{3}\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^p + \frac{1}{3}\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^p = 1 \implies p = 0$

  $g(n) = \lg n$

  $\int_1^n \frac{\lg u}{u^1} du = \Theta(\lg^2 n)$

  $T(n) = \Theta\left(n^0 (1 + \lg^2 n)\right) = \Theta(\lg^2 n)$

- *(d)* $T(n) = \dfrac{1}{3}\, T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{3}\right) + \dfrac{1}{n}$

  $\frac{1}{3}\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^p = 1 \implies \left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^{p+1} = 1 \implies p = -1$

  $g(n) = \frac{1}{n}$

  $\int_1^n \frac{1/u}{u^0} du = \int_1^n \frac{1}{u} du = \Theta(\lg n)$

  $T(n) = \Theta\left(n^{-1} (1 + \lg n)\right) = \Theta\left(\frac{\lg n}{n}\right)$

- *(e)* $T(n) = 3T\!\left(\dfrac{n}{3}\right) + 3T\!\left(\dfrac{2n}{3}\right) + n^2$

  $3\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^p + 3\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^p = 1$.
  Observe that for $p=2$, $3(1/9) + 3(4/9) = 15/9 > 1$. Thus, $p > 2$.

  $g(n) = n^2$

  $\int_1^n \frac{u^2}{u^{p+1}} du = \int_1^n u^{1-p} du$.
  Since $p > 2 \implies 1-p < -1$, the integral converges to a constant $\Theta(1)$ as $n \to \infty$.

  $T(n) = \Theta\left(n^p (1 + \Theta(1))\right) = \Theta(n^p)$ (where $p$ is the solution to $3^{1-p} + 3(2/3)^p = 1$)
