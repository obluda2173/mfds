* Problem 2.17 :rotation:unitary_operator:matrix:
** Task
#+begin_quote
(Rotations). The goal of this exercise is to show that rotations in 2d and 3d are unitary operators.

1. Let $E = (\mathbb{R}^2, \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_2)$ and let $\mathcal{E}$ be the canonical basis. Given $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$, we consider the mapping $R_\theta : E \to E$ whose matrix representation in $\mathcal{E}$ is

\[
[R_\theta]_{\mathcal{E}}^{\mathcal{E}} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos(\theta) & -\sin(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta) & \cos(\theta) \end{pmatrix}
\]

This operation is the rotation of angle $\theta$ in the 2d plane. Show that $R_\theta$ is a unitary operator.

2. Let $E = (\mathbb{R}^3, \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_2)$.

   (a) Interpret the action of the mappings given by the following matrices
   \[
   [R_{e_1, \alpha}]_{\mathcal{E}}^{\mathcal{E}} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \cos(\alpha) & -\sin(\alpha) \\ 0 & \sin(\alpha) & \cos(\alpha) \end{pmatrix}, \quad [R_{e_2, \beta}]_{\mathcal{E}}^{\mathcal{E}} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos(\beta) & 0 & \sin(\beta) \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -\sin(\beta) & 0 & \cos(\beta) \end{pmatrix}
   \]
   \[
   [R_{e_3, \gamma}]_{\mathcal{E}}^{\mathcal{E}} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos(\gamma) & -\sin(\gamma) & 0 \\ \sin(\gamma) & \cos(\gamma) & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}
   \]
   where $\alpha, \beta, \gamma \in \mathbb{R}$.

   (b) Let the mapping $R : \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3$ be defined by $R = R_{e_3, \gamma} \circ R_{e_2, \beta} \circ R_{e_1, \alpha}$. Explain the action of this mapping, and justify why one can interpret $R$ as a rotation in 3d.

   (c) Give the matrix representation $[R]_{\mathcal{E}}^{\mathcal{E}}$.

   (d) Show that $R$ is a unitary operator.

   (e) Show that $R$ is invariant with respect to the order in which the elementary operations $R_{e_1, \alpha}, \ldots, R_{e_3, \gamma}$ are taken.
#+end_quote

** Solution

*** (1)

#+begin_quote
*Theorem 2.24:  Unitary operators*

Let $T \in \mathcal{B}(E, E)$. Then the following properties are equivalent:

1. $TT^* = T^*T = \mathrm{Id}_E$,

2. $T$ is bijective and $T^{-1} = T^*$.

3. $T$ is surjective and it preserves inner products:

   $$\langle T(x), T(y) \rangle_E = \langle x, y \rangle_E, \quad \forall x, y \in E.$$

4. $T$ is surjective and it preserves the norm:

   $$\|T(x)\|_E = \|x\|_E, \quad \forall x \in E.$$

5. $[T]_{\mathcal{E}}^{\mathcal{E}}$ is unitary ($\mathbb{K} = \mathbb{C}$) or orthogonal ($\mathbb{K} = \mathbb{R}$) if $E$ is finite dimensional and $\mathcal{E} = \{e_i\}_{i=1}^n$ is an orthonormal basis.

If one of these properties hold, we say that $T$ is a /unitary operator/ ($\mathbb{K} = \mathbb{C}$) or /orthogonal operator/ ($\mathbb{K} = \mathbb{R}$).
#+end_quote

\begin{align*}
M &= [R_{\theta}]_{\mathcal{E}}^{\mathcal{E}} \\
M^{\top}M &=
\begin{pmatrix} \cos(\theta) & \sin(\theta) \\ -\sin(\theta) & \cos(\theta) \end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix} \cos(\theta) & -\sin(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta) & \cos(\theta) \end{pmatrix} \\
&= \begin{pmatrix} \cos(\theta)^2+\sin(\theta)^2 & 0 \\ 0 & \sin(\theta)^2+\cos(\theta)^2 \end{pmatrix} \\
&= I_2 \\
\end{align*}

Each $R_{e_i, \theta}$ fixes $e_i$ and rotates the orthogonal plane $\mathrm{span}(e_j, e_k)$ by $\theta$, where $(j,k)$ is chosen so that $e_j \times e_k = e_i$.

- $R_{e_1, \alpha}$: fixes $e_1$, rotates $(e_2, e_3)$ by $\alpha$.

- $R_{e_2, \beta}$: fixes $e_2$, rotates $(e_3, e_1)$ by $\beta$. The transposed sign pattern in the $(1,3)$ block reflects that the canonical order $(e_1, e_3)$ is left-handed relative to $e_2$.

- $R_{e_3, \gamma}$: fixes $e_3$, rotates $(e_1, e_2)$ by $\gamma$.

**** (c)

Using the shorthand $c_x := \cos x$, $s_x := \sin x$, and noting that
composition of linear maps corresponds to matrix product in the same
order:

$$
[R]_{\mathcal{E}}^{\mathcal{E}}
= [R_{e_3,\gamma}]_{\mathcal{E}}^{\mathcal{E}}\,
  [R_{e_2,\beta}]_{\mathcal{E}}^{\mathcal{E}}\,
  [R_{e_1,\alpha}]_{\mathcal{E}}^{\mathcal{E}}
$$

*Step 1.* Compute $[R_{e_2,\beta}][R_{e_1,\alpha}]$:

\begin{align*}
\begin{pmatrix} c_\beta & 0 & s_\beta \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -s_\beta & 0 & c_\beta \end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & c_\alpha & -s_\alpha \\ 0 & s_\alpha & c_\alpha \end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
c_\beta & s_\beta s_\alpha & s_\beta c_\alpha \\
0 & c_\alpha & -s_\alpha \\
-s_\beta & c_\beta s_\alpha & c_\beta c_\alpha
\end{pmatrix}
\end{align*}

*Step 2.* Left-multiply by $[R_{e_3,\gamma}]$:

\begin{align*}
\begin{pmatrix} c_\gamma & -s_\gamma & 0 \\ s_\gamma & c_\gamma & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
c_\beta & s_\beta s_\alpha & s_\beta c_\alpha \\
0 & c_\alpha & -s_\alpha \\
-s_\beta & c_\beta s_\alpha & c_\beta c_\alpha
\end{pmatrix}
\end{align*}

Therefore:

\begin{align*}
[R]_{\mathcal{E}}^{\mathcal{E}}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
c_\gamma c_\beta & c_\gamma s_\beta s_\alpha - s_\gamma c_\alpha & c_\gamma s_\beta c_\alpha + s_\gamma s_\alpha \\
s_\gamma c_\beta & s_\gamma s_\beta s_\alpha + c_\gamma c_\alpha & s_\gamma s_\beta c_\alpha - c_\gamma s_\alpha \\
-s_\beta & c_\beta s_\alpha & c_\beta c_\alpha
\end{pmatrix}
\end{align*}

**** (d)

It suffices to show that $R$ is surjective and preserves the Euclidean norm.

*Each $R_{e_i, \theta}$ preserves the norm.* For $R_{e_1, \alpha}$ and $x = (x_1, x_2, x_3)$:

\begin{align*}
\|R_{e_1, \alpha} x\|_2^2
&= x_1^2 + (c_\alpha x_2 - s_\alpha x_3)^2 + (s_\alpha x_2 + c_\alpha x_3)^2 \\
&= x_1^2 + (c_\alpha^2 + s_\alpha^2)(x_2^2 + x_3^2)
 = \|x\|_2^2.
\end{align*}

The cases $R_{e_2, \beta}$ and $R_{e_3, \gamma}$ are identical up to a permutation of the coordinate roles.

*Each $R_{e_i, \theta}$ is surjective.* The matrix $[R_{e_i, -\theta}]_{\mathcal{E}}^{\mathcal{E}}$ is a left and right inverse (direct check), so each elementary rotation is bijective.

*Composition.* Both properties pass to compositions:

$$
\|R(x)\|_2 = \|R_{e_3,\gamma}(R_{e_2,\beta}(R_{e_1,\alpha}(x)))\|_2 = \|x\|_2,
$$

and a composition of bijections is a bijection, hence $R$ is surjective. By Theorem 2.24, $(4) \Rightarrow$ $R$ is unitary. $\blacksquare$

#+begin_quote
(Inner product of continuous functions (Programming Exercise)). In this exercise, we want to illustrate that computing inner-products in infinite-dimensional spaces in practice is not an easy task. We illustrate what happens for $E = \mathcal{C}([0,1], \mathbb{R})$.

(a) Prove that $\mathcal{C}([0,1], \mathbb{R})$ is a vector space over the field $\mathbb{K} = \mathbb{R}$.

(b) Prove that the operator
\[
\omega : \mathcal{C}([0,1], \mathbb{R}) \times \mathcal{C}([0,1], \mathbb{R}) \to \mathbb{R}
\]
\[
(f, g) \mapsto \omega(f, g) := \int_0^1 f(x) g(x) \, dx
\]
is an inner product on $\mathcal{C}([0,1])$.

In general, calculating analytically the inner product of two functions is not possible or difficult, so the inner product is approximated. One way to do so is by using Riemann sums. Consider a partition of $[0, 1]$ into $n$ equal intervals with grid points
\[
x_i^n = \frac{i}{n}, \quad i = 0, \ldots, n
\]
and step size $\Delta x = \frac{1}{n}$. We define the following Riemann sums:

- Left Riemann sum: $R_-(f) := \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} f(x_i^n) \Delta x$.
- Right Riemann sum: $R_+(f) := \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} f(x_{i+1}^n) \Delta x$.
- Central Riemann summm: $R_0(f) := \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} f(x_i^n + \frac{\Delta x}{2}) \Delta x$.

We next ask you to answer the following questions by programming a code that evaluates the above Riemann sums:

(c) Let $n = 10$. Visualize the left, right and central Riemann sum approximation of the inner product of $f(x) = x^2$ and $g(x) = \sin(2\pi x)$. Plot the piecewise constant approximations corresponding to the left, right, and midpoint Riemann sums of $f$, $g$ and $fg$, i.e. one plot for left Riemann sum, one for the right Riemann sum, etc. Compare the approximation of each method with the analytical solution.

We now consider the space $L^2([0,1], \mathbb{R})$, containing all functions that are square integrable, i.e. $f \in L^2([0,1], \mathbb{R}) \Leftrightarrow \int_0^1 |f(x)|^2 dx < \infty$.

(d) Show that $\mathcal{C}([0,1], \mathbb{R}) \subset L^2([0,1], \mathbb{R})$.

$L^2([0,1])$ also contains non-continuous functions, for example piecewise continuous functions.

(e) Let $f(x) = \lfloor 3x \rfloor$ and $g(x) = x^2 \sin(2\pi x)$. Note the discontinuities of $f$ at $x = \frac{1}{3}, \frac{2}{3}$. Visualize the three Riemann approximations and notice the difference.

(f) Now increase $n$. Plot the error (i.e. difference of approximation with analytical solution) of the methods for both the functions of (c) and (e) against $n$ for $n = \{10, 11, \ldots, 30\}$. Discuss what you observe.

(g) What would happen to the error if there are $k$ discontinuities on $[0, 1]$?

*Programming hints*

When implementing the numerical approximations, it can be useful to structure your code into small reusable functions. For example, you may define a Julia function that numerically approximates the inner product
\[
\langle f, g \rangle = \int_0^1 f(x) g(x) \, dx
\]
using a Riemann sum with $n$ bins: ~riemann(f, g, n, method)~ with

- ~f~ and ~g~ are functions,
- ~n~ is the number of bins,
- ~method~ specifies the Riemann method (e.g. 'left', 'right', or 'midpoint').

Inside the function you can construct the grid points $x_i = i/n$, evaluate the product $f(x)g(x)$ at the appropriate sample points, multiply by $\Delta x = 1/n$ and sum the contributions.
