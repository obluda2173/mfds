* Problem 2.11 :inner_product:dual_space:matrix:
** Task
#+begin_quote
(Inner product defined from a set of dual elements.)

Let $\{f^1, \ldots, f^n\} \in \mathbb{E}^*$ be an independent set of vectors in the dual space of $\mathbb{E} = \mathbb{R}^n$, we define the following inner product
\[
\omega(x, y) = \sum_{h=1}^n f^h(x) f^h(y)
\]
Suppose that, given a basis $\mathcal{U} = \{u_1, \ldots, u_n\}$ of $\mathbb{E}$ and the corresponding dual basis $\mathcal{U}^* = \{u^1, \ldots, u^n\}$ in $\mathbb{E}^*$, the dual elements $f^i$ have the representation
\[
[f^i]_{\mathcal{U}} = [a^i_1, \ldots, a^i_n]
\]
Then there exists the following alternative definition for $\omega$:
\[
\omega(x, y) = \left([x]^{\mathcal{U}}\right)^T A^T A \, [y]^{\mathcal{U}}, \quad \text{where} \quad A = \begin{pmatrix} a^1_1 & \cdots & a^1_n \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a^n_1 & \cdots & a^n_n \end{pmatrix}
\]
and consequently the matrix representation of $\omega$ w.r.t. the basis $\mathcal{U}$ is $[\omega]_{\mathcal{U}\mathcal{U}} = A^T A$.
#+end_quote

** Solution

\begin{align*}
\omega (x, y) &= \sum_{h = 1}^n f^h(x) f^h(y) \\
&= \sum_{h = 1}^n \left( [f^h]_{U}^{\top}[x]_{U} \right) \left( [f^h]_{U}^{\top}[y]_{U} \right) \\
&= \sum_{h = 1}^n \left( [f^h]_{U}^{\top}[x]_{U} \right)^{\top} \left( [f^h]_{U}^{\top}[y]_{U} \right) \\
&= \sum_{h = 1}^n \left( [x]_{U}^{\top}[f^h]_{U} \right) \left( [f^h]_{U}^{\top}[y]_{U} \right) \\
&= [x]_{U}^{\top} \left( \sum_{h = 1}^n [f^h]_{U} [f^h]_{U}^{\top} \right) [y]_{U}
\end{align*}

where $[f^h]_{U}^{\top} = [a_1^h, \ldots, a_n^h]$ and $[f^h]_{U} = [a_1^h, \ldots, a_n^h]^{\top}$. The $(i,j)$ entry of the sum is:

\[
\left( \sum_{h=1}^n [f^h]_{U} [f^h]_{U}^{\top} \right)_{ij}
= \sum_{h=1}^n \left( [f^h]_{U} [f^h]_{U}^{\top} \right)_{ij}
= \sum_{h=1}^n a_i^h a_j^h
= \sum_{h=1}^n A_{hi} A_{hj}
= (A^{\top} A)_{ij}
\]

hence $\displaystyle\sum_{h=1}^n [f^h]_{U} [f^h]_{U}^{\top} = A^{\top} A$, and

\begin{align*}
\omega(x, y) &= [x]_{U}^{\top} (A^{\top} A) [y]_{U}
\end{align*}

* Problem 2.12 :projection:inner_product:
** Task
#+begin_quote
(Orthogonal projection and inner product)

Let $\mathbb{E} = \mathbb{R}^4$, and let $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ be the standard dot product on $\mathbb{E}$. Calculate the orthogonal projection of the vector $x = (1, 2, 1, 2)^T$ onto the subspace $\mathbb{F} = \operatorname{span}\{(1, 2, 3, 4)^T, (4, 3, 2, 1)^T\}$ with respect to $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$.
#+end_quote

** Solution

- Setup

  Let $f_1 = (1,2,3,4)^T$ and $f_2 = (4,3,2,1)^T$. Since $\hat{x} \in \mathbb{F}$, we write:

  \begin{align*}
  \hat{x} = \alpha f_1 + \beta f_2 = Ac, \qquad A = [f_1 \mid f_2], \quad c = (\alpha, \beta)^T
  \end{align*}

- Normal equations

  The projection is characterised by $x - \hat{x} \perp \mathbb{F}$, i.e. the error is orthogonal to every basis vector:

  \begin{align*}
  f_1^{\top}(x - Ac) = 0, \qquad f_2^{\top}(x - Ac) = 0
  \end{align*}

  Stacking these conditions gives the normal equations:

  \begin{align*}
  A^{\top}(x - Ac) = 0 \implies A^{\top}Ac = A^{\top}x
  \end{align*}

- Computing $A^\top A$ and $A^\top x$

  \begin{align*}
  A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 2 & 3 \\ 3 & 2 \\ 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad
  A^{\top}A = \begin{bmatrix} 30 & 20 \\ 20 & 30 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad
  A^{\top}x = \begin{bmatrix} 16 \\ 14 \end{bmatrix}
  \end{align*}

- Solving the system

  The normal equations become:

  \begin{align*}
  \begin{cases} 15\alpha + 10\beta = 8 \\ 10\alpha + 15\beta = 7 \end{cases}
  \end{align*}

  From the first equation: $\alpha = \dfrac{8 - 10\beta}{15}$. Substituting into the second:

  \begin{align*}
  \frac{10(8 - 10\beta)}{15} + 15\beta = 7
  \implies 80 - 100\beta + 225\beta = 105
  \implies \beta = \frac{1}{5}
  \end{align*}

  Back-substituting: $\alpha = \dfrac{8 - 2}{15} = \dfrac{2}{5}$.

- Result

  \begin{align*}
  \hat{x} = \frac{2}{5} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} + \frac{1}{5} \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{5} \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ 7 \\ 8 \\ 9 \end{bmatrix}
  \end{align*}

- Verification

  The error $x - \hat{x} = \tfrac{1}{5}(-1, 3, -3, 1)^T$ must be orthogonal to both basis vectors:

  \begin{align*}
  f_1^{\top}(x - \hat{x}) &= \tfrac{1}{5}(-1 + 6 - 9 + 4) = 0 \checkmark \\
  f_2^{\top}(x - \hat{x}) &= \tfrac{1}{5}(-4 + 9 - 6 + 1) = 0 \checkmark
  \end{align*}

* Problem 2.15 :inner_product:polynomial:
** Task
#+begin_quote
(Inner products)

In this exercise we ask whether the following $\omega(\cdot, \cdot): \mathbb{E} \times \mathbb{E} \to \mathbb{R}$ are inner products:

(i) $\mathbb{E} = \mathbb{R}_n[X]$, with $\omega(P(X), Q(X)) = P(1)Q(1) + P(2)Q(2) + \ldots + P(n)Q(n)$

(ii) $\mathbb{E} = \mathbb{R}_n[X]$, with $\omega(P(X), Q(X)) = P(0)Q(0) + P(1)Q(1) + \ldots + P(n)Q(n)$

(iii) $\mathbb{E} = C[0, 1]$ is the space of continuous functions defined on $[0, 1]$ and $\omega(f, g) = \int_0^{1/2} f(t) g(t) \, dt$.
#+end_quote

** Solution

*** (i)

$\omega$ is *not* an inner product.

Symmetry and bilinearity hold trivially. Positive definiteness fails.

Compute:

$$\omega(P, P) = P(1)^2 + P(2)^2 + \cdots + P(n)^2$$

This is zero iff $P(1) = P(2) = \cdots = P(n) = 0$, i.e. iff $P$ has roots at $1, 2, \ldots, n$.

Take:

$$P(X) = (X-1)(X-2)\cdots(X-n)$$

This has degree $n$, so $P \in \mathbb{R}_n[X]$, and $P \neq 0$. Yet $P(k) = 0$ for all $k \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$, so $\omega(P, P) = 0$.

Positive definiteness fails. $\omega$ is not an inner product. $\square$

*** (ii)

$\omega$ *is* an inner product.

*Symmetry:* $\omega(P, Q) = \sum_{k=0}^{n} P(k)Q(k) = \sum_{k=0}^{n} Q(k)P(k) = \omega(Q, P)$. $\checkmark$

*Bilinearity:* For $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$\omega(\lambda P + R,\ Q) = \sum_{k=0}^{n}(\lambda P(k) + R(k))Q(k) = \lambda\sum_{k=0}^{n}P(k)Q(k) + \sum_{k=0}^{n}R(k)Q(k) = \lambda\,\omega(P,Q) + \omega(R,Q) \quad \checkmark$$

*Positive definiteness:*

$$\omega(P, P) = \sum_{k=0}^{n} P(k)^2 \geq 0$$

Suppose $\omega(P, P) = 0$. Then $P(k) = 0$ for all $k \in \{0, 1, \ldots, n\}$ â€” that is $n+1$ roots. But $P \in \mathbb{R}_n[X]$ has degree $\leq n$, so a nonzero $P$ has at most $n$ roots. Therefore $P = 0$. $\checkmark$

All three axioms hold. $\omega$ is an inner product. $\square$

*** (iii)

$\omega$ is *not* an inner product.

Symmetry and bilinearity hold trivially (multiplication of real values and linearity of the integral). Positive definiteness fails.

Compute:

$$\omega(f, f) = \int_0^{1/2} f(t)^2\, dt \geq 0$$

This is zero iff $f(t)^2 = 0$ for a.e. $t \in [0, 1/2]$, and since $f$ is continuous, iff $f \equiv 0$ on $[0, 1/2]$. But $f$ need not vanish on $(1/2, 1]$.

Take:

$$f(t) = \max(0,\ t - 1/2)$$

This is continuous on $[0,1]$ and $f \neq 0$ in $C[0,1]$. Yet $f(t) = 0$ for all $t \in [0, 1/2]$, so $\omega(f, f) = 0$.

Positive definiteness fails. $\omega$ is not an inner product. $\square$
