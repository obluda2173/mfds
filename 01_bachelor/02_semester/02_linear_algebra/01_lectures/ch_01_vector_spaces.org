* Definition  1.1:  Binary operation $+$ on a set $E$

A binary operation on set $E$ is a mapping

\begin{align*}
+ : E \times E &\rightarrow E \\
(x, y) &\mapsto x + y \\
\end{align*}

which produces an element $E$ out of two elements from $E$.

* Definition  1.3:  Commutative Group

We say that $(E, +)$ is a group when $E$ is a set, and $+$ is a binary operation on $E$ satisfying:

1. Associativity: $(x + y) + z = x + (y + z), \quad \forall x, y, z \in E$
2. Identity element: $\exists 0_{E} : x + 0_{E} = x$
3. Inverse element: $\forall x \in E, \exists \tilde{x} \in E : x + \tilde{x} = 0_{E}$
4. Commutativity: $x + y = y + x$

* Definition  1.5:  Scalar multiplication

Scalar multiplication on $E$ over the field $\mathbb{K}$ is a map

\begin{align*}
\times : \mathbb{K} \times E &\rightarrow E \\
(\lambda, x) &\mapsto \lambda \times x \\
\end{align*}

which produces an element in $E$ out of scalar $\lambda$ and an input element $x \in E$.

* Definition  1.6:  Compatibility between $\cdot$ and $+$

We say that $\cdot$ is compatible with $+$ if and only if for all $x, y \in E$ and all $\lambda, \mu \in \mathbb{K}$:

1. identity element of the scalar multiplication: $1_{\mathbb{K}} \cdot x = x$ ($1_{\mathbb{K}}$ being the multiplicative identity of $\mathbb{K}$)

2. compatibility of scalar multiplication with field multiplication:

   $$(\lambda \mu) \cdot x = \lambda \cdot (\mu \cdot x)$$

3. distributivity of scalar multiplication w.r.t. vector addition:

   $$\lambda \cdot (x + y) = \lambda \cdot x + \lambda \cdot y$$

4. distributivity of scalar multiplication w.r.t. field addition:

   $$(\lambda + \mu) \cdot x = \lambda \cdot x + \mu \cdot y$$

* Definition  1.7:  Vector space

*Definition (Vector Space)* A vector space $V$ over a field $\mathbb{F}$ is equipped with two operations:

- Vector addition $+ : V + V \rightarrow V$
- Vector multiplication $\times : \mathbb{F} \times V \rightarrow V$

Such that the following axioms hold for all $u, v, w \in V$ and all scalars $a,b \in \mathbb{F}$:

1. Associativity of addition

   $(u + v) + w = u + (v + w)$

2. Commutativity of addition

   $u + v = v + u$

3. Additive identity

   $\exists 0 \in V : \forall u \in V,  u + 0 = u$

4. Additive inverse

   $\forall u \in V, \exists -u \in V : u + (-u) = 0$

5. Distributivity over scalar additon

   $(a + b) \times u = (a \times u) + (b \times u)$

6. Distributivity over vector addition

   $a \times (u + v) = (a \times u) + (a \times v)$

7. Compatability of scalar multiplication

   $a \times (b \times u) = (a \times b) \times u$

8. Scalar multiplicative identity

   $1 \times u = u$

   Where $1$ is a multiplicative identity of $\mathbb{F}$.

* Definition  1.10: $\mathbb{K}[X]$ and degree of a polynomial

The set of polynomials over $\mathbb{K}$ is defined as

$$\mathbb{K}[X] := \left\{ \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a_k X^k \;:\; a_k \in \mathbb{K} \;\text{and}\; \exists\, n \in \mathbb{N}_0 \;\text{s.t.}\; a_k = 0_{\mathbb{K}} \;\text{for}\; k > n \right\}.$$

The degree of a polynomial $p \in \mathbb{K}[X]$ is

$$\deg(p) := \max\{ n \in \mathbb{N}_0 \;:\; a_n \neq 0_{\mathbb{K}} \}.$$

* Definition  1.13: Linear combination

Let $E$ be a $\mathbb{K}$ vector space. Given vectors $x_1, \ldots, x_n \in E$, a /linear combination/ of these vectors is any vector of the form

$$v = \lambda_1 x_1 + \lambda_2 x_2 + \cdots + \lambda_n x_n, \qquad \text{with } \lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n \in \mathbb{K}, \text{ and } n \in \mathbb{N}.$$

* Definition  1.14: Generating set (Spanning set)

Let $\mathcal{X} = \{x_1, \ldots x_n\}$ be a finite subset of vectors of $E$ with $n \in \mathbb{N}$. We say that $\mathcal{X}$ a /generating set/ (or /spanning set/) of $E$ if any element of $E$ can be expressed as a linear combination of the elements of $\mathcal{X}$:

$$\forall\, x \in E, \; \exists\, \lambda_1, \ldots \lambda_n \in \mathbb{K} \;\text{s.t.}\; x = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \lambda_i x_i.$$

Equivalently,

$$E = \text{span}\{x_i\}_{i=1}^{n}.$$

* Definition  1.15: Linearly independent set (Free family)

Let $\mathcal{X} = \{x_1, \ldots x_n\}$ be a finite subset of vectors of $E$ with $n \in \mathbb{N}$. We say that $X$ is a linearly independent set (or a free family) if the only linear combination that gives the zero vector is the trivial one:

$$\lambda_1 x_1 + \cdots + \lambda_n x_n = 0 \;\text{for some}\; \lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n \in \mathbb{K} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \lambda_1 = \cdots = \lambda_n = 0.$$

* Definition  1.16: Basis

A set of vectors $\mathcal{B} = \{x_1, \ldots x_n\} \subset E$ is a /basis/ of $E$ if it is both generating and linearly independent.

* Proposition 1.17: Coordinate of a vector on a given basis

Let $\mathcal{B} = \{x_1, \ldots x_n\} \subset E$ be a basis of $E$. Then for any vector $x \in E$ there exist unique scalars $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n \in \mathbb{K}$ such that

$$x = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \lambda_i x_i.$$

The tuple of scalars

$$[x]^{\mathcal{B}} := (\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)^{\top} \in \mathbb{K}^n$$

is called the coordinate vector of $x$ with respect to the basis $\mathcal{B}$, and the linear mapping

\begin{align*}
\text{Vec} : E &\rightarrow \mathbb{K}^n \\
x &\mapsto [x]^{\mathcal{B}}
\end{align*}

is a isomorphism.

** Q&A

- Q_01

  What is the last point?

- A_01

  The point is that isomorphism means the two spaces are "structurally identical" as a vector spaces. So once you fix a basis $\mathcal{B}$, your abstract vectors space $E$ becomes indistinguishable from $\mathbb{K}^n$ in terms of linear algebra.

* Proposition 1.18: Linearity of coordinate representation

Let $x, y \in E$. Then

$$[\alpha x + \beta y]^{\mathcal{E}} = \alpha [x]^{\mathcal{E}} + \beta [y]^{\mathcal{E}}, \quad \forall\, \alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{K}.$$

*Proof.*

We simply use the definition of the coordinate representation and the fact that $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a vector space. $\square$

* Definition  1.19: Subspace

Let $(E, +, \cdot)$ be a $\mathbb{K}$ vector space, and let $F \subset E$ be a subset of $E$. We say that $F$ is a subspace of $E$ if and only if $F$ is nonempty and stable by linear combinations:

1. $F \neq \emptyset$

2. $\forall\, x, y \in F$, $x + y \in F$

3. $\forall\, x \in F$, $\lambda \in \mathbb{K}$, $\lambda x \in F$.

* Proposition 1.20: Subspace characterization via closure under linear combinations

$F \subset E$ is a subspace of $E$ if and only if:

1. $0 \in F$

2. $\forall\, x, y \in F$, $\forall\, \lambda, \mu \in \mathbb{K}$, $\lambda x + \mu y \in F$.

* Proposition 1.22: Subspace intersection, union, and sum

Let $F$ and $G$ be two subspaces of a vector space $E$. Then:

1. $F \cap G$ is a subspace of $E$

2. $F \cup G$ is in general not a subspace of $E$

3. $F + G := \{x + y : x \in F, y \in G\}$ is a subspace of $E$, and we have the Grassmann formula

   $$\dim(F + G) = \dim(F) + \dim(G) - \dim(F \cap G)$$

** Q&A

- Q_01

  Why is $F \cap G$ is a subspace of $E$, while $F \cup G$ is "generaly" not?

- A_01

  The intersection $F \cap G$ is a subspace because if two elements both belong to $F$ and to $G$, any linear combination of them remains in $F$ (since $F$ is a subspace) and in $G$ (since $G$ is a subspace), so it stays in $F \cap G$. The union $F \cup G$ generally fails because you can pick $x \in F \setminus G$ and $y \in G \setminus F$, and their sum $x + y$ typically belongs to neither $F$ nor $G$, breaking closure under addition. (It only works when one subspace contains the other, making the union trivially equal to the larger one.)

* Definition  1.23: Direct sum

The sum of two subspaces $F$, $G$ of a vector space $E$ is said to be direct, and is written $F + G = F \oplus G$, when for any $x \in E$ there exists a unique decomposition

$$x = x_F + x_G$$

such that $x_F \in F$ and $x_G \in G$. This is equivalent to the condition $F \cap G = \{0\}$. The two subspaces are said to be complementary when their sum is direct and $E = F \oplus G$.

* Definition  1.24: Linear map

Let $E$ and $F$ be two $\mathbb{K}$ vector spaces. The mapping $f : E \rightarrow F$ is linear if and only if

$$f(\lambda x + \mu y) = \lambda f(x) + \mu f(y), \quad \forall x, y \in E, \; \lambda, \mu \in \mathbb{K}$$

We denote by $\mathcal{L}(E, F)$ the set of all linear mappings from $E$ to $F$. When $F = E$, the mapping is called an endomorphism, and the set of endomorphisms is denoted as $\mathcal{L}(E) := \mathcal{L}(E, E)$.

* Proposition 1.25: Subspace and kernel/image structure of linear maps

Let $E$ and $F$ be two $\mathbb{K}$ vector spaces, and $f \in \mathcal{L}(E, F)$. Then:

1. $\mathcal{L}(E, F)$ is a subspace of $\mathcal{F}(E, F)$ (with addition and scalar multiplication as described Sec 1.2.5), and this implies that $\mathcal{L}(E, F)$ is a vector space.

2. The kernel and image sets

   $$\ker(f) := \{x \in E : f(x) = 0\}$$

   $$\mathrm{im}(f) := \{y \in F : \exists x \in E \; \text{s.t.} \; y = f(x)\}$$

   are subspaces of $E$.

** Q&A

- Q_01

  I understand that $\mathcal{L}(E, F)$ is a subspace of $\mathcal{F}(E, F)$, but how can it be a vector space when it is a set of linear maps?

- A_01

  The key insight is that a vector space is just a set equipped with addition and scalar multiplication satisfying the axioms - the "vectors" don't have to be arrows or tuples. Here, the elements of $\mathcal{L}(E, F)$ are linear maps, and the operations are pointwise: $(f + g)(x) = f(x) + g(x)$ and $(\lambda f)(x) = \lambda f(x)$. These operations satisfy all the vector space axioms (the zero vector is the zero map, additive inverses are $(-f)(x) = -f(x)$, etc.), so $\mathcal{L}(E, F)$ is a perfectly valid vector space whose "vectors" happen to be functions. In fact, this is precisely what being a subspace of $\mathcal{F}(E, F)$ gives you for free: $\mathcal{F}(E, F)$ is already a vector space under pointwise operations, and any subspace of a vector space is itself a vector space, so you only need to verify the subspace criteria rather than re-checking all the axioms from scratch.

* Definition  1.27: Linear form, dual space

Let $E$ be a $\mathbb{K}$ vector space. The dual space of $E$ is the set $E^* := \mathcal{L}(E, \mathbb{K})$. Every $f \in E^*$ is called a linear functional.

/The underlying intuition is that elements of the dual space are "machines" that take a vector and return a number./

* Theorem     1.29: Existence of dual basis

Let $E$ be a $\mathbb{K}$ vector space of finite dimension $n \in \mathbb{N}$, and let $\mathcal{E} = \{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$ be a basis of $E$. For every $1 \le i \le n$, we define

\begin{align*}
e_i^* : E &\rightarrow \mathbb{K} \\
x &\mapsto [x]_i^{\mathcal{E}},
\end{align*}

which is the mapping that returns the $i$ th coordinate component of the elements of $E$ when expressed in the basis $\mathcal{E}$. Then $e_i^* \in E^*$ and

$$\mathcal{B}^* := \{e_1^*, \ldots, e_n^*\}$$

is a basis of $E^*$, which is called the dual basis of $\mathcal{E}$. Furthermore,

$$e_i^*(e_j) = \delta_{i,j}, \quad \forall \; 1 \le i, j \le n$$

*Proof.*

The mapping $e_i^*$ is linear by /Proposition 1.18/, and the fact that component extraction is also a linear application. We next prove that $\mathcal{B}^*$ is a basis of $E^*$ by showing that it is both a linearly independent and generating set.

- Linear Independence: Let $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n \in \mathbb{K}$ such that $\lambda_1 e_1^* + \cdots + \lambda_n e_n^* = 0$. Then, $(\lambda_1 e_1^* + \cdots + \lambda_n e_n^*)(e_i) = \lambda_i = 0$ for all $1 \le i \le n$, which shows that $\mathcal{B}^*$ is a linearly independent set.

- Generating set: Let $\varphi \in E^*$. For every $x = \sum_{i=1}^{n} [x]_i^{\mathcal{E}} \; e_i \in E$, it holds that

  $$\varphi(x) = \varphi(x_1 e_1 + \cdots + x_n e_n) = [x]_1^{\mathcal{E}} \; \varphi(e_1) + \cdots + [x]_n^{\mathcal{E}} \; \varphi(e_n) = \varphi(e_1) e_1^*(x) + \cdots + \varphi(e_n) e_n^*(x),$$

  where we have used that $[x]_i^{\mathcal{E}} = e_i^*(x)$ by definition of the $e_i^*$. Since the equality holds for every $x \in E$, we obtain the equality of linear functionals

  $$\varphi = \varphi(e_1) e_1^* + \cdots + \varphi(e_n) e_n^*$$

  It expresses $\varphi$ as linear combination of the elements $e_i^* \in E^*$, thus showing that $\varphi \in \mathrm{span}\{\mathcal{B}^*\}_{i=1}^{n}$. Since this holds for every $\varphi \in E^*$, we have shown that $E^* = \mathrm{span}\{\mathcal{B}^*\}$.

$\square$

* Corollary   1.30: Dimension of dual space

If $E$ is finite-dimensional, then $\dim(E) = \dim(E^*)$.

* Definition  1.31: Annihilator

Let $E$ be a $\mathbb{K}$ vector space of dimension $n$, and let $W$ be a subspace of $E$. The annihilator $W^{\circ}$ of $W$ is the set of linear functionals that vanish on $W$.

$$W^{\circ} := \{\varphi \in E^* : \varphi(x) = 0, \; \forall x \in W\}$$

* Theorem     1.33: Dimension of annihilator

Let $E$ be a $\mathbb{K}$ vector space of dimension $n$, and let $W$ be a subspace of $E$. Then

$$\dim(E) = \dim(W) + \dim(W^{\circ}).$$

*Proof.*

Suppose that $1 \le \dim(W) = k < n$, and let $(e_1, \ldots, e_k)$ be a basis of $W$. We complete this basis with $n - k$ vectors $(e_{k+1}, \ldots, e_n)$ to obtain a basis of $E$ which we call $\mathcal{B}$. We introduce the dual basis $\mathcal{B}^* = \{e_1^*, \ldots, e_n^*\}$, and observe that $W^{\circ} = \text{span}\{e_{k+1}^*, \ldots, e_n^*\}$. Indeed, we clearly have that $e_{k+1}^*, \ldots, e_n^* \in W^{\circ}$. In addition, any element $\mu \in W^{\circ}$ cannot have components in $e_1^*, \ldots, e_k^*$ since $\mu(e_1^*) = \cdots = \mu(e_k^*) = 0$. Therefore $\dim(W^{\circ}) = n - k$. $\square$

* Lemma       1.34: Matrix representation of a linear map

Let:

1. $E$ and $F$ be two finite-dimensional $\mathbb{K}$ vector spaces (of dimension $p$ and $n$ respectively),

2. $\mathcal{E} = \{e_1, \ldots, e_p\}$ and $\mathcal{F} = \{u_1, \ldots, u_n\}$ be two bases of $E$ and $F$ respectively.

The mapping

$$\text{Mat} : \mathcal{L}(E, F) \rightarrow \mathbb{K}^{p \times n}$$

$$f \mapsto \text{Mat}(f) = [f]_{\mathcal{E}}^{\mathcal{F}} := \left( [f(e_1)]^{\mathcal{F}} \mid \ldots \mid [f(e_p)]^{\mathcal{F}} \right)$$

is an isomorphism. For every $f \in \mathcal{L}(E, F)$, $[f]_{\mathcal{E}}^{\mathcal{F}}$ is called the matrix representation of $f$ in the bases $\mathcal{E}$ and $\mathcal{F}$.

*Proof.*

We next prove that $\text{Mat}$ is both surjective and injective.

*Injectivity:* Let $f, g \in \mathcal{L}(E, F)$ such that $\text{Mat}(f) = \text{Mat}(g)$. Then $[f(e_i)]^{\mathcal{F}} = [g(e_i)]^{\mathcal{F}}$ for all $i = 1, \ldots, n$. Since the coordinate representation is unique, it follows that this equality is also true at the level of the vectors, $f(e_i) = g(e_i)$. Let now $x \in E$ such that $x = \sum_{i=1}^{n} c_i e_i$. Then $f(x) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} c_i f(e_i) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} c_i g(e_i) = g(x)$. Since this holds for all $x \in E$, it follows that $f = g$, and we have proven injectivity.

*Surjectivity:* Let $A \in \mathbb{K}^{p \times n}$. We are going to show that there exists $f_A \in \mathcal{L}(E, F)$ such that $\text{Mat}(f_A) = A$. For this, we define a mapping $f_A : E \rightarrow F$ such that

$$[f_A(x)]^{\mathcal{F}} := A[x]^{\mathcal{E}}, \quad \forall x \in E.$$

The mapping $f_A$ is linear since, by construction, $[f_A(x)]^{\mathcal{F}} = 0_{\mathbb{K}^n}$ and therefore $f_A(0) = 0$. Also, for all $x, y \in E$ and $\mu, \lambda \in \mathbb{K}$, $[f_A(\mu x + \lambda y)]^{\mathcal{F}} = A[\mu x + \lambda y]^{\mathcal{E}} = \lambda A[x]^{\mathcal{E}} + \mu A[x]^{\mathcal{E}} = \lambda f_A(x) + \mu f_A(y)$. This shows surjectivity. $\square$

* Definition  1.52: Norm and normed vector space

Let $(E, +, \cdot)$ be a $\mathbb{K}$ vector space. A norm is a mapping $\|\cdot\|_E : E \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ satisfying:

1. positivity: for all $x \in E$, it holds that $\|x\|_E \ge 0$,

2. non-degeneracy: for all $x \in E$, if $\|x\|_E = 0$, then $x = 0_E$,

3. homogeneity: for all $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$ and all $x \in E$, $\|\lambda x\|_E = |\lambda| \|x\|_E$,

4. triangle inequality: for all $(x, y) \in E$, it holds that $\|x + y\|_E \le \|x\|_E + \|y\|_E$.

$(E, \|\cdot\|_E)$ is called a normed vector space.

* Proposition 1.53: Norm induced metric

Let $(E, \|\cdot\|_E)$ be a normed vector space. Define the function

\begin{align*}
d_{\|\cdot\|_E} &: E \times E \rightarrow \mathbb{R} \\
(x, y) &\mapsto d_{\|\cdot\|_E}(x, y) = \|x - y\|_E.
\end{align*}

Then $d_{\|\cdot\|_E}$ is a distance on $E$, and $(E, d_{\|\cdot\|_E})$ is a metric space.

* Definition  1.55: Norm equivalence

Let $E$ be a $\mathbb{K}$ vector space with $\mathbb{K} = \mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$, and let $\|\cdot\|_{\alpha}$ and $\|\cdot\|_{\beta}$ be two norms on $E$. We say that $\|\cdot\|_{\alpha}$ and $\|\cdot\|_{\beta}$ are equivalent if there exists two constants $0 < \underline{c} \le \overline{c}$ such that

$$\underline{c} \|x\|_{\alpha} \le \|x\|_{\beta} \le \overline{c} \|x\|_{\alpha}, \quad \forall x \in E.$$

* Theorem     1.56: All norms are equivalent in finite dimension

Let $E$ be a $\mathbb{K}$ vector space with $\mathbb{K} = \mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$. Then:

1. If $E$ is finite dimensional, then all norms are equivalent,

2. If $E$ is infinite dimensional, then there always exists norms that are not equivalent.
