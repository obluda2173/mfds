The informal definition you provided simplifies the complex concepts associated with a mathematical entity called a "ring."

- When we say we can "add and multiply" in a set to form a ring, it means that the set has binary operations (in this case, addition and multiplication) that map any two elements of the set to another element in the set.
  For addition, these operations follow the usual rules like closure (the result is in the set), associativity (the order of operation doesn't matter), and an identity element (there's an element that doesn't change the other elements when added). It also encompasses the existence of additive inverses (for any element, there's another that results in the identity element when added together).
  For multiplication, it's not as restrictive, but at the very least, the multiplication over the set needs to be associative and distributive over addition.

- "Behaving like the integers" refers to the properties of integer addition and multiplication extended to the set. Addition is associative and commutative, and has an identity (zero). Every integer has an additive inverse (negative). Multiplication is also associative and distributive over addition.

- "Reasonably well" in terms of multiplication means that, the multiplication is associative (a/(b*c)=(a*b)*c) and it's distributive over addition (a/(b+c)=a*b + a*c and (b+c)*a=b*a + c*a).

- The ring concept "generalizes ℤ, polynomial arithmetic, matrix arithmetic, and lots of algebraic structures." This means that all these mathematical structures can be thought of as specific types of rings. They share the properties of rings but might have additional distinctive features.
