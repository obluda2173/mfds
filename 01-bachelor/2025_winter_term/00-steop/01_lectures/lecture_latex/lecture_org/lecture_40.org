* Orthogonality and Coordinate Systems
** Orthogonal Vectors
Two vectors $a$ and $b$ are called *orthogonal* (perpendicular) if the angle $\varphi$ between them is $\frac{\pi}{2}$ ($90^{\circ}$).
- Notation: $a \perp b$

** Rectangular Cartesian Coordinate System
A rectangular Cartesian coordinate system is a set of points and unit vectors mutually orthogonal to each other.

*** In $\mathbb{R}^2$
- Vectors: $e_1, e_2$
- Properties:
  - $|e_1| = 1, |e_2| = 1$
  - $\langle e_1, e_2 \rangle = 0$ (Orthogonal)
- Vector representation: $a = (a_1, a_2) = a_1e_1 + a_2e_2$

*** Distance between two points in $\mathbb{R}^2$
Given points $A(x_1, y_1)$ and $B(x_2, y_2)$:
$$ |AB| = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2} $$

*** In $\mathbb{R}^3$
- Defined by origin $O$ and three mutually orthogonal unit vectors $e_1, e_2, e_3$.
- Given points $A(x_1, y_1, z_1)$ and $B(x_2, y_2, z_2)$:
$$ |AB| = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2 + (z_2 - z_1)^2} $$

* Scalar Product (Inner Product)
** Definition
The scalar (or dot/inner) product of two vectors $a$ and $b$ is the number:
$$ \langle a, b \rangle = |a| \cdot |b| \cdot \cos \varphi $$
where $\varphi$ is the angle between $a$ and $b$.

** Geometric Interpretation
- If the angle is *acute* ($0 \le \varphi < 90^\circ$): $\langle a, b \rangle > 0$
- If the angle is *obtuse* ($90^\circ < \varphi \le 180^\circ$): $\langle a, b \rangle < 0$
- If vectors are orthogonal: $\langle a, b \rangle = 0$

** Properties
1. $\langle a, b \rangle = \langle b, a \rangle$ (Symmetry)
2. $\langle \lambda a, b \rangle = \lambda \langle a, b \rangle$ for $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$ (Linearity)
3. $\langle a + b, c \rangle = \langle a, c \rangle + \langle b, c \rangle$ (Distributivity)
4. $\langle a, a \rangle = 0 \iff a = 0$ (Positive definiteness)

* Inner Product in Coordinates
** Derivation in $\mathbb{R}^2$
Let $a = a_1e_1 + a_2e_2$ and $b = b_1e_1 + b_2e_2$.
Using the properties of the inner product and the fact that coordinates are orthonormal ($\langle e_i, e_j \rangle = \delta_{ij}$):

$$
\begin{aligned}
\langle a, b \rangle &= \langle a_1e_1 + a_2e_2, b_1e_1 + b_2e_2 \rangle \\
&= a_1b_1\langle e_1, e_1 \rangle + a_1b_2\langle e_1, e_2 \rangle + a_2b_1\langle e_2, e_1 \rangle + a_2b_2\langle e_2, e_2 \rangle \\
&= a_1b_1(1) + a_1b_2(0) + a_2b_1(0) + a_2b_2(1) \\
&= a_1b_1 + a_2b_2
\end{aligned}
$$

** Formula for Angle
Since $\langle a, b \rangle = |a| \cdot |b| \cdot \cos \varphi = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2$:
$$ \cos \varphi = \frac{a_1b_1 + a_2b_2}{\sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2} \cdot \sqrt{b_1^2 + b_2^2}} $$

** Generalization to $\mathbb{R}^3$
For $a = (a_1, a_2, a_3)$ and $b = (b_1, b_2, b_3)$:
$$ \langle a, b \rangle = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + a_3b_3 $$
$$ \cos \varphi = \frac{a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + a_3b_3}{\sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2 + a_3^2} \cdot \sqrt{b_1^2 + b_2^2 + b_3^2}} $$

* Equation of a Line
** Ways to Define a Line
1. Two points.
2. A point and a direction vector.
3. A point and an orthogonal (normal) vector.

** Normal Form (Point and Orthogonal Vector)
Given a point $P_0(x_0, y_0)$ and a normal vector $n = (a, b)$.
For any point $P(x, y)$ on the line, the vector $\vec{P_0P}$ is orthogonal to $n$:
$$ \langle n, \vec{P_0P} \rangle = 0 $$
$$ a(x - x_0) + b(y - y_0) = 0 $$

Let $c = -(ax_0 + by_0)$, then the *general equation of a line* is:
$$ ax + by + c = 0 $$
where $n = (a, b)$ is the normal vector.

** Angle Between Two Lines
The angle $\varphi$ between two lines $l_1$ and $l_2$ is the angle between their normal vectors $n_1$ and $n_2$.
Given lines:
- $l_1: a_1x + b_1y + c_1 = 0 \implies n_1 = (a_1, b_1)$
- $l_2: a_2x + b_2y + c_2 = 0 \implies n_2 = (a_2, b_2)$

$$ \cos \varphi = \frac{|\langle n_1, n_2 \rangle|}{|n_1| \cdot |n_2|} = \frac{|a_1a_2 + b_1b_2|}{\sqrt{a_1^2 + b_1^2} \cdot \sqrt{a_2^2 + b_2^2}} $$

** Two-Point Form
Equation of the line passing through $P_1(x_1, y_1)$ and $P_2(x_2, y_2)$.
A point $P(x, y)$ is on the line if $\vec{P_1P}$ is parallel to $\vec{P_1P_2}$.
$$ \vec{P_1P} = \lambda \vec{P_1P_2} $$
In coordinates:
$$ \frac{x - x_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{y - y_1}{y_2 - y_1} $$

* Equation of a Plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$
** Ways to Define a Plane
1. Three points.
2. A point and a normal vector.
3. Two lines.

** Normal Form
Given a point $P_0(x_0, y_0, z_0)$ and a normal vector $n = (a, b, c)$.
For any point $P(x, y, z)$ on the plane:
$$ \langle n, \vec{P_0P} \rangle = 0 $$
$$ a(x - x_0) + b(y - y_0) + c(z - z_0) = 0 $$

Let $d = -(ax_0 + by_0 + cz_0)$, then the general equation is:
$$ ax + by + cz + d = 0 $$

** Angle Between Two Planes
The angle between two planes is the angle between their normal vectors.
Given planes with normals $n_1 = (a_1, b_1, c_1)$ and $n_2 = (a_2, b_2, c_2)$:

$$ \cos \varphi = \frac{\langle n_1, n_2 \rangle}{|n_1| \cdot |n_2|} = \frac{a_1a_2 + b_1b_2 + c_1c_2}{\sqrt{a_1^2 + b_1^2 + c_1^2} \cdot \sqrt{a_2^2 + b_2^2 + c_2^2}} $$
