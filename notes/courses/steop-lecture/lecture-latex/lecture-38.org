* Vector Fundamentals
** Definition and Notation
- *Definition*: Vectors are defined by two properties: length (magnitude) and direction.
- *Notation*: A vector from point $A$ to $B$ is denoted as $\vec{AB}$ or by a bold lowercase letter like $\mathbf{a}$.
- *Magnitude*: The length is a scalar denoted by $|\vec{AB}|$ or $|\mathbf{a}|$.
- *Collinear Vectors*: Vectors are considered collinear if they lie on the same line or on parallel lines.
- *Equality*: Two vectors are equal if they have the same length and direction.

** Operations
*** Addition
Vector addition satisfies the following laws:
- *Commutative*: $\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b} + \mathbf{a}$
- *Associative*: $\mathbf{a} + (\mathbf{b} + \mathbf{c}) = (\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}) + \mathbf{c}$

*** Scalar Multiplication
Multiplying a vector $\mathbf{a}$ by a scalar $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$ scales the length of the vector. Linearity properties include:
- $(\alpha + \beta)\mathbf{a} = \alpha\mathbf{a} + \beta\mathbf{a}$
- $\alpha(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}) = \alpha\mathbf{a} + \alpha\mathbf{b}$

* Segment Division and Position Vectors
** Segment Division Formula
Given a segment $AB$ and a point $C$ on it such that the segment is divided in a specific ratio $\frac{AC}{CB} = \frac{\alpha}{\beta}$. We want to find the position vector $\vec{OC}$ relative to an origin $O$.

Using vector addition $\vec{OC} = \vec{OA} + \vec{AC}$ and the ratio property, we derive:

$$ \vec{OC} = \frac{\beta}{\alpha + \beta}\vec{OA} + \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}\vec{OB} $$

** The Centroid of a Triangle
*Theorem*: The medians of a triangle intersect at a single unique point (the centroid $M$), which divides the medians in a ratio of $2:1$ (counting from the vertex).

*** Proof
1. Let $M$ be a point on median $AA_1$ such that $AM : MA_1 = 2:1$.
2. The point $A_1$ is the midpoint of $BC$, so $\vec{OA_1} = \frac{1}{2}\vec{OB} + \frac{1}{2}\vec{OC}$.
3. Applying the segment division formula to $M$:
   $$ \vec{OM} = \frac{1}{3}\vec{OA} + \frac{2}{3}\vec{OA_1} $$
4. Substituting $\vec{OA_1}$:
   $$ \vec{OM} = \frac{1}{3}\vec{OA} + \frac{2}{3}\left(\frac{1}{2}\vec{OB} + \frac{1}{2}\vec{OC}\right) $$
   $$ \vec{OM} = \frac{1}{3}(\vec{OA} + \vec{OB} + \vec{OC}) $$

*Uniqueness*: If we choose point $N$ on median $BB_1$ or point $R$ on median $CC_1$ with the same ratio, we arrive at the exact same expression. Thus, $M = N = R$.

* Linear Independence and Basis
** Linear Combinations
A linear combination of vectors $\mathbf{a}_1, \dots, \mathbf{a}_n$ is the sum $\alpha_1\mathbf{a}_1 + \dots + \alpha_n\mathbf{a}_n$.

** Collinear Theorem
*Theorem*: If $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are non-collinear vectors, then:
$$ \alpha\mathbf{a} + \beta\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{0} \iff \alpha = \beta = 0 $$

*Proof*:
Suppose $\alpha\mathbf{a} + \beta\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{0}$. If $\alpha \neq 0$, then $\mathbf{a} = -\frac{\beta}{\alpha}\mathbf{b}$, which implies the vectors are collinear. This is a contradiction.

** Basis in a Plane
- *Definition*: A vector basis for a plane is any set of two non-collinear vectors $\{\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2\}$.
- *Theorem*: For any vector $\mathbf{a}$ on the plane, there exist unique scalars $\alpha_1, \alpha_2 \in \mathbb{R}$ such that:
  $$ \mathbf{a} = \alpha_1\mathbf{e}_1 + \alpha_2\mathbf{e}_2 $$

* Coordinates
If $\mathbf{a} = \alpha_1\mathbf{e}_1 + \alpha_2\mathbf{e}_2$, the pair $(\alpha_1, \alpha_2)$ are the coordinates of the vector in that basis.

** Operations in Coordinates
Given $\mathbf{a} = (\alpha_1, \alpha_2)$ and $\mathbf{b} = (\beta_1, \beta_2)$:
1. *Equality*: $\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{b} \iff \alpha_1 = \beta_1 \text{ and } \alpha_2 = \beta_2$
2. *Scalar Multiplication*: $\lambda\mathbf{a} = (\lambda\alpha_1, \lambda\alpha_2)$
3. *Addition*: $\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} = (\alpha_1 + \beta_1, \alpha_2 + \beta_2)$

** Extension to $\mathbb{R}^3$
- *Coplanar*: Vectors are coplanar if they lie in the same plane or parallel planes.
- *Basis in $\mathbb{R}^3$*: Any three non-coplanar vectors form a basis in 3D space.
